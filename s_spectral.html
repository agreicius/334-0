    Section 1.23 Spectral theorems

    Among the many takeaways from Section 1.17 is the simple fact that not all linear operators are diagonalizable. In principle Procedure 1.17.5 gives a complete answer to the question of diagonalizability in terms of eigenspaces. However, you should not be mislead by the artificially simple examples treated in Section 1.17. In practice even the determination (or approximation) of the distinct eigenvalues of an \(n\times n\) matrix poses a very challenging computational problem as \(n\) gets large. As such the general question of whether a matrix is diagonalizable remains an intractable one. This makes all the more welcoming the main results of this section: if \(V\) is a finite-dimensional inner product space, then all normal operators on \(V\) are diagonalizable (when \(F=\C\)), and all self-adjoint operators are diagonalizable (when \(F=\R\) or \(F=\C\)). These surprising facts are elaborated upon in the two spectral theorems of this section. These results in turn fit into a larger suite of more general spectral theorems that treat the diagonalizability of various families of linear operators of inner product spaces (both finite and infinite dimensional).

    Subsection Spectral theorem for normal operators

    Definition 1.23.1. Invariant subspace.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{V}\text{.}\) A subspace \(W\subseteq V\) is \(T\)-invariant if \(T(W)\subseteq W\text{:}\) equivalently, if \(w\in W\) implies \(T(w)\in W\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.23.2. Invariant subspace.

    When \(W\) is \(T\)-invariant, on the other hand, we know that \(T(W)\subseteq W\text{,}\) yielding the the following commutative diagram.
    As you can see, when \(W\) is \(T\)-invariant, the restriction map \(T\vert_W\) can now be thought of as an operator on the smaller space \(W\text{:}\) i.e., we have \(T\vert_W\in \mathcal{L}(W)\text{.}\) As we will see below, this can be useful in the context of proofs that proceed by induction on the dimension of a space.

    Definition 1.23.3. Polynomial evaluation of operator.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{.}\) Given a polynomial \(f(x)=\anpoly\) with coefficients \(a_i\in F\text{,}\) we define the operator \(f(T)\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) as
    \begin{equation*} f(T)=a_nT^n+a_{n-1}T^{n-1}+\cdots +a_1T+a_0I_V\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Definition 1.23.4. Polynomial conjugate.

    Let \(F\in \{\R, \C\}\text{.}\) Given a polynomial \(f(x)=\anpoly\) with coefficients \(a_i\in F\text{,}\) we define its conjugate \(\overline{f}(x)\) as
    \begin{equation*} \overline{f}(x)=\overline{a_n}x^n+\overline{a_{n-1}}x^{n-1}+\cdots +\overline{a_1}x+\overline{a_0}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.23.5. Polynomial evaluation of operator.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{V}\text{,}\) and let \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) be polynomials with coefficients in \(F\text{.}\)
    1. \(f(T)\) and \(g(T)\) commute: i.e.,
      \begin{equation} f(T)g(T)=g(T)f(T)\text{.}\tag{1.112} \end{equation}
      In particular, \(T\) commutes with \(f(T)\text{:}\) that is, we have \(T\, f(T)=f(T)\, T\text{.}\)
    2. If \(W\) is a \(T\)-invariant subspace, then \(W\) is a \(f(T)\)-invariant subspace.
    3. Assume \(F\in \{\R, \C\}\) and that \(V\) is an inner product space. We have
      \begin{align} (f(T))^*\amp =\overline{f}(T^*) \tag{1.113}\\ \amp = \overline{a_n}(T^*)^n+a_{n-1}(T^*)^{n-1}+\cdots +\overline{a_1}T^*+\overline{a_0}I_V \text{,}\tag{1.114} \end{align}
      where \(f(x)=\anpoly\text{.}\)
    4. Assume \(F\in \{\R, \C\}\) and that \(V\) is an inner product space. If \(T\) is normal, then \(f(T)\) is normal.

    Proof.

    1. Let \(f(x)=\anpoly\) and \(g(x)=\bmpoly\text{.}\) First observe that for all integers \(k\geq 0\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} T^k\, g(T) \amp =T^k(b_mT^m+\cdots +b_1T+b_0)\\ \amp = b_mT^{m+k}+\cdots b_1T^{k+1}+b_0T^k\\ \amp (b_mT^m+\cdots +b_1T+b_0)T^k \text{.} \end{align*}
      It follows that
      \begin{align*} f(T)g(T) \amp = a_nT^ng(T)+\cdots +a_1Tg(T)+a_0g(T)\\ \amp = a_ng(T)T^n+\cdots +a_1g(T)T+a_0g(T)I_V\\ \amp g(T)(a_nT^n+\cdots +a_1T+a_0I_V) \\ \amp =g(T)f(T)\text{.} \end{align*}
    2. First observe that if \(W\) is \(S\)- and \(T\)-invariant for operators \(S,T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) then it is \((cS+dT)\)-invariant for any \(c,d\in F\text{.}\) Indeed, if \(w\in W\text{,}\) then \(S(w)\in W\) and \(T(w)\in W\) implies that \((cS+dT)(w)=cS(w)+dT(w)\in W\text{.}\) It follows by induction that if \(W\) is \(T_i\)-invariant for operators \(T_i\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) \(1\leq i\leq n\text{,}\) then it is \((c_1T_1+\cdots +c_nT_n)\)-invariant for any \(c_i\in F\text{.}\) Next, an easy induction argument shows that if \(W\) is \(T\)-invariant, then it is \(T^k\)-invariant for all \(k\geq 0\text{.}\) Thus, if \(W\) is \(T\)-invariant, then it is \(T^k\)-invariant for all \(0\leq k\leq n\text{,}\) and thus also \((a_nT^n+\cdot +a_1T+a_0I_V)\)-invariant for any \(a_i\in F\text{.}\) This shows \(W\) is \(f(T)\)-invariant.
    3. We have
      \begin{align*} (f(T))^* \amp = (a_nT^n+\cdot +a_1T+a_0I_V)^*\\ \amp = \overline{a_n}(T^n)^*+\cdot +\overline{a_1}T^*+\overline{a_0}I_V^* \amp \knowl{./knowl/xref/th_adjoint_props.html}{\text{1.21.5}}\\ \amp = \overline{a_n}(T^*)^n+\cdot +\overline{a_1}T^*+\overline{a_0}I_V \amp \knowl{./knowl/xref/th_adjoint_props.html}{\text{1.21.5}}\\ \amp =\overline{f}(T^*)\text{.} \end{align*}
    4. We have
      \begin{align*} (f(T))^*f(T) \amp = \overline{f}(T^*)f(T)\\ \amp = \sum_{0\leq i,j\leq n}\overline{a_i}a_j(T^*)^iT^j\\ \amp \sum_{0\leq i,j\leq n}\overline{a_i}a_jT^j(T^*)^i \\ \amp = f(T)\overline{f}(T^*)\\ \amp =f(T)(f(T))^*\text{.} \end{align*}

    Theorem 1.23.6. Invariant subspaces and adjoints.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) be a linear operator on the inner product space \((V,\angvec{\, , }\text{,}\) and let \(T^*\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) be its adjoint.
    1. If \(W\) is \(T\)-invariant, then \(W^\perp\) is \(T^*\)-invariant.
    2. If \(W\) is \(T\)- and \(T^*\)-invariant, then
      \begin{equation} (T\vert_W)^*=T^*\vert_W\text{.}\tag{1.115} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    1. Assume \(W\) is \(T\)-\invariant. For any \(v\in W^\perp\) and \(w\in W\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \angvec{w, T^*(v)} \amp = \angvec{T(w),v}\\ \amp = \angvec{w', v} \amp (T(w)=w'\in W)\\ \amp =0 \amp (v\in W^\perp)\text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus \(v\in W^\perp\) implies \(T^*(v)\in W^\perp\text{,}\) showing that \(W^\perp\) is \(T^*\)-invariant.
    2. Assume \(W\) is \(T\)- and \(T^*\)-invariant. This allows us to define maps \(T\vert_W\colon W\rightarrow W\) and \(T^*\vert_W\colon W\rightarrow W\text{.}\) Thus to show \(T^*\vert_W=(T\vert_W)^*\text{,}\) we need only show that \(T^*\vert_W\) satisfies the adjoint property
      \begin{equation*} \angvec{T\vert_W(w),w'}=\angvec{w,T^*\vert_W(w')} \end{equation*}
      for all \(w,w'\in W\text{.}\) But this is easy! We have
      \begin{align*} \angvec{T\vert_W(w),w'}\amp = \angvec{T(w),w'} \amp (\text{def. of } T\vert_W)\\ \amp =\angvec{w,T^*(w')}\\ \amp =\angvec{w,T^*\vert_W(w')} \amp (\text{def. of } T^*\vert_W)\text{.} \end{align*}
    Before getting to our first spectral theorem, we prove an interesting standalone fact about the eigenspaces of normal operators over any field.

    Theorem 1.23.7. Eigenspaces of normal operators.

    Let \(F\in \{\R, \C\}\text{,}\) and let \(T\) be a normal operator on the finite-dimensional inner product space \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\text{.}\) Given distinct scalars \(\lambda_1\ne \lambda_2\in F\text{,}\) the eigenspaces \(E(\lambda_1,T)\) and \(E(\lambda_2, T)\) are orthogonal: i.e., \(\angvec{v,w}=0\) for all \(v\in E(\lambda_1, T)\) and \(w\in E(\lambda_2,T)\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\) be distinct scalars. Take any vectors \(v\in E(\lambda_1, T)\) and \(w\in E(\lambda_2, T)\) and consider first the adjoint property
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{T(v),w}=\angvec{v,T^*(w)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(v\in E(\lambda_1, T)\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{T(v),w}=\lambda_1\angvec{v,w}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(T\) is normal, we have \(w\in E(\lambda', T)=E(\overline{\lambda'}, T^*)\) by Corollary 1.22.10, and thus
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{v,T^*(w)}=\angvec{v,\overline{\lambda_2}w}=\overline{\overline{\lambda_2}}\angvec{v,w}=\lambda_2\angvec{v,w}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Putting it all together, we have
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\angvec{v,w}=\angvec{T(v),w}=\angvec{v,T^*(w)}=\lambda_2\angvec{v,w}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\lambda_1\angvec{v,w}=\lambda_2\angvec{v,w}\) and \(\lambda_1\ne \lambda_2\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\angvec{v,w}=0\text{,}\) as desired.

    Theorem 1.23.8. Spectral theorem for normal operators: \(F=\C\).

    Let \(F=\C\text{,}\) let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be a nonzero finite-dimensional inner product space over \(\C\text{,}\) and let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) with distinct eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(T\) is normal.
    2. \(V=\bigoplus_{i=1}^r E(\lambda_i, T)\) and the eigenspaces \(E(\lambda_i, T)\) are pairwise orthogonal: i.e., if \(i\ne j\text{,}\) then \(\angvec{v,w}=0\) for all \(v\in E(\lambda_i, T)\) and \(w\in E(\lambda_j, T)\text{.}\)
    3. \(T\) is orthogonally diagonalizable: that is, there is an orthogonal (or orthonormal) eigenbasis of \(T\text{.}\)
    4. There is an orthogonal (or orthonormal) basis \(B\) such that \([T]_B=D\) is diagonal.

    Proof.

    We prove a cycle of implications (1)\(\implies\)(2)\(\implies\)(3)\(\implies\)(4)\(\implies\)(1). It turns out that only (1)\(\implies\)(2) is the only implication that requires any work. Accordingly, we take care of the easier implications first.
    Implication (2)\(\implies\)(3).
    From Theorem 1.17.4, we know that choosing any bases \(B_i\) for each eigenspace \(E(\lambda_i, T)\) and then concatenating these as \(B=B_1*B_2*\cdots *B_r\) yields an eigenbasis of \(V\text{.}\) Since we know the eigenspaces are pairwise orthogonal, if we choose each \(B_i\) to be an orthogonal (resp. orthonormal) basis, then resulting concatenated basis \(B\) will be orthogonal (resp. orthonormal), since the elements of \(B_i\) are orthogonal to the elements of \(B_j\) for all \(j\ne i\text{.}\) Thus \(T\) is orthogonally diagonalizable.
    Implication (3)\(\implies\)(4).
    We know from Theorem 1.17.4 that if \(B\) is an eigenbasis of \(T\text{,}\) then \([T]_B\) is diagonal. Assuming (3), not only does there exist an eigenbasis of \(T\text{,}\) but in fact we can find an orthogonal (or orthonormal) eigenbasis \(B\text{.}\) We have \([T]_B=D\) for this choice of \(B\text{.}\)
    Implication (4)\(\implies\)(1).
    Assume \(B\) is an orthonormal basis of \(T\) such that \([T]_B=D\) is diagonal. For a diagonal matrix we have
    \begin{equation*} D^*=\overline{D^T}=\overline{D}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and it is then easy to see that
    \begin{equation*} D^*D=\overline{D}D=D\overline{D}=DD^*\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(D\) is a normal matrix. Since \(B\) is orthonormal, it follows from Theorem 1.22.6 that \(T\) is normal.
    Implication (1)\(\implies\)(2).
    We now turn to the last implication in our cycle. Assume \(T\) is normal. By Theorem 1.23.7, the distinct eigenspaces of any normal operator are orthogonal, so it suffices to show that we have \(V=\oplus_{i=1}^r E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\) We will do so by induction on the dimension of \(V\text{.}\) More precisely, we will show that for all \(n\geq 1\text{,}\) given any normal operator on any \(n\)-dimensional complex inner product space of dimension \(n\text{,}\) we have the direct sum decomposition into eigenspaces as specified in (2).
    Base case: \(n=1\text{.}\) If \(V\) is \(1\)-dimensional, then for any operator (normal or not) \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) we have \(T=\lambda_V\) for some \(\lambda\in F\text{,}\) in which case \(V=E(\lambda, T)\) and \(\lambda\) is the only eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\)
    Now assume by (strong) induction that for all \(1\leq k< n\text{,}\) for any normal operator on any \(k\)-dimensional complex inner product space, we have the direct sum decomposition into eigenspaces as specified in (2). Let \(T\) be a normal operator on the \(n\)-dimensional inner product space \(V\text{.}\) Since \(\lambda_1\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\text{,}\) the eigenspace \(E(\lambda_1,T)\) is nonzero.
    If \(\dim E(\lambda_1,T)=n\text{,}\) then we have \(V=E(\lambda_1,T)\text{.}\) It follows that \(\lambda_1\) is the only eigenvalue of \(T\text{,}\) and \(V=E(\lambda_1,T)\) is the desired direct sum decomposition into eigenspaces.
    Assume that \(\dim E(\lambda_1,T)< n\text{,}\) and consider the direct sum decomposition
    \begin{equation*} V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(1\leq \dim E(\lambda_1,T)\leq n-1\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} 1\leq \dim E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\leq n-1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Our proof concludes as follows:
    • show that \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) is both \(T\)- and \(T^*\)-invariant;
    • show that \(T\vert_W\) is normal;
    • apply the induction hypothesis to \(T\vert_W\text{.}\)
    Let \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{.}\) We first show that \(W\) is \(T\)-invariant: given \(w\in E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) and \(v\in E(\lambda, T)\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{v,T(w)} \amp = \angvec{T^*(v),w}\\ \amp = \angvec{\overline{\lambda_1}v,w} \amp (E(\lambda, T)=E(\overline{\lambda},T^*)\\ \amp = \overline{\lambda_1}\angvec{v,w}\\ \amp = 0 \amp (w\in E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp)\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus \(w\in E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) implies \(T(w)\in E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{,}\) as desired. Similarly, given any \(w\in E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) and \(v\in E(\lambda_1,T)\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{v, T^*(w)} \amp =\angvec{T(v),w} \\ \amp =\angvec{\lambda v, w}\\ \amp =\lambda\angvec{v,w}\\ \amp =0\text{,} \end{align*}
    showing that \(w\in E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) implies \(T^*(w)\in E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{,}\) as desired.
    Since \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) is \(T\)- and \(T^*\)-invariant, we know that
    \begin{equation*} (T\vert_W)^*=T^*\vert_W \end{equation*}
    by Theorem 1.23.6. Since
    \begin{align*} \norm{T\vert_W(w)}\amp = \norm{T(w)} \\ \amp = \norm{T^*(w)}\\ \amp = \norm{T^*\vert_W(w)}\\ \amp =\norm{(T\vert_W)^*(w)} \end{align*}
    for all \(w\in W\text{,}\) it follows from Theorem 1.22.9 that \(T\vert_W\) is a normal operator on \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{.}\) Lastly, since \(\dim W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp< n\) we can apply our induction hypothesis to the normal operator \(T|vert_W\) on \(W\) to conclude that
    \begin{equation*} W=\bigoplus_{\lambda \in \Lambda} E(\lambda, T\vert_W)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\Lambda\) is the set of distinct eigenvalues of \(T\vert_W\text{.}\) Since clearly, any eigenvalue of \(T\vert_W\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\text{,}\) we conclude that
    \begin{align*} V \amp =E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus W \amp (W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp)\\ \amp =E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus \bigoplus_{\lambda \in \Lambda} E(\lambda, T\vert_W) \\ \amp =\bigoplus_{i=1}^r E(\lambda_i, T)\text{,} \end{align*}
    where \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\) are distinct eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\) It then follows easily that these are all of the distinct eigenvalues of \(T\text{.}\)

    Example 1.23.9. Normal operator (\(F=\C\)).

    Let \(A=\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ -1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and consider the matrix transformation \(T_A\colon \C^2\rightarrow \C^2\text{,}\) where \(\C^2\) is given the standard Hermitian inner product.
    1. Show that \(T_A\) is normal, skew-Hermitian, and unitary!
    2. Verify that (2) of Theorem 1.23.8 applies for \(T_A\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. We have
      \begin{equation*} T_A^*=T_{A^*}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where
      \begin{equation*} A^*=\overline{A^T}=\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      It is easily verified that
      \begin{equation*} A^*=-A=A^{-1}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      and hence that \(A\) is skew-Hermitian and unitary, and hence also normal.
    2. The usual computations show that the characteristic polynomial of \(T_A\) is
      \begin{equation*} f(x)=x^2+1=(x-i)(x+i)\text{,} \end{equation*}
      and that the nonzero eigenspaces of \(T_A\) are
      \begin{align*} E(i,T_A) \amp =\Span(1,i)\\ E(-i,T_A) \amp = \Span(1,-i)\text{.} \end{align*}
      Since \(\dim \C^2=2=\dim E(i,T_A)+\dim E(-i,T_A)\text{,}\) we see that \(V=E(i,T_A)\oplus E(-i,T_A)\) by Theorem 1.17.4. Lastly since
      \begin{equation*} \angvec{(1,i),(1,-i)}=1\cdot 1+i\cdot\overline{-i}=1+i^2=1-1=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
      it follows that \(E(i,T_A)\) and \(E(-i,T_A)\) are orthogonal.

    Example 1.23.10. Normal operator (\(F=\R\)).

    Let \(A=\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ -1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\) again, but now consider the matrix transformation \(T_A\colon \R^2\rightarrow \R^2\text{.}\) Just as in Example 1.23.9, \(T_A\) has characteristic polynomial \(f(x)=x^2+1\text{,}\) and is normal, skew-symmetric (real version of skew-Hermitian), and orthogonal (real version of unitary). However, not only is \(T_A\) not diagonalizable, it does not even have any eigenvalues! This illustrates the necessity of \(F=\C\) in Theorem 1.23.8.

    Subsection Self-adjoint operators

    If you look closely at the induction argument for the implication (1)\(\implies\)(2) in Theorem 1.23.8, you see that the only thing we need to get the induction argument moving is the existence of an eigenvalue. By adding the condition \(F=\C\) to Theorem 1.23.8 we guarantee the existence of this eigenvalue: this is because for any operator \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) on a complex vector space of finite dimension, its characteristic polynomial \(f(x)\) of \(T\) factors completely over \(F=\C\) as
    \begin{equation} f(x)=(x-\lambda_1)^{n_1}\cdots (x-\lambda_r)^{n_r}\tag{1.116} \end{equation}
    for scalars \(\lambda_i\in \C\text{.}\) Thus, our induction argument in Theorem 1.23.8 could be adapted for the \(F=\R\) case, as long as the characteristic polynomial of the operators always factor completely over \(\R\) as in (1.116). Unfortunately, this is simply not the case for normal operators of real vector spaces. However, it is the case for self-adjoint operators, as the next result shows. Before getting to that, let’s give a manageable name for this factoring property of a polynomial.

    Definition 1.23.11. Splits completeley.

    Let \(F\) be a field, and let \(f(x)=\anpoly\) be a polynomial with coefficients in \(F\text{.}\) We say the \(f\) splits completely over \(F\) if \(f\) factors as
    \begin{equation} f(x)=(x-\lambda_1)^{n_1}(x-\lambda_2)^{n_2}\cdots (x-\lambda_r)^{n_r}\text{,}\tag{1.117} \end{equation}
    with \(\lambda_i\in F\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.23.12. Eigenvalues of self-adjoint operators.

    Let \(F\in \{\R, \C\}\text{,}\) let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space over \(F\text{,}\) and let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) be a self-adjoint operator.
    1. If \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue \(T\text{,}\) then \(\lambda\in \R\text{.}\) In other words, all eigenvalues of \(T\) are real.
    2. Assume further that \(\dim V< \infty\text{.}\) The characteristic polynomial \(f\) of \(T\) factors as
      \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-\lambda_1)^{n_1}(x-\lambda_2)^{n_2}\cdots (x-\lambda_r)^{n_r}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where \(\lambda_i\ne \lambda_j\) for all \(i\ne j\text{,}\) and \(\lambda_i\in \R\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. Assume \(v\) is a \(\lambda\)-eigenvector of \(T\text{.}\) First observe that we have
      \begin{align*} \angvec{T(v),v} \amp = \angvec{v,T^*(v)}\\ \amp =\angvec{v,T(v)} \amp (T=T^*)\text{.} \end{align*}
      Next, since \(v\in E(\lambda, T)\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \angvec{T(v),v} \amp =\angvec{\lambda v, v}=\lambda\angvec{v,v}\\ \angvec{v,T(v)} \amp =\angvec{v,\lambda v}=\overline{\lambda}\angvec{v,v}\text{.} \end{align*}
      Putting it all together, we have
      \begin{equation*} \lambda\angvec{v,v}=\angvec{T(v),v}=\angvec{v,T(v)}=\overline{\lambda}\angvec{v,v}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since \(\lambda\angvec{v,v}=\overline{\lambda}\angvec{v,v}\) and \(\angvec{v,v}\ne 0\) (since \(v\ne \boldzero\) by definition), we conclude from (1) that \(\lambda=\overline{\lambda}\text{,}\) and hence that \(\lambda\in \R\text{.}\)
    2. We treat the cases \(F=\R\) and \(F=\C\) separately.
      If \(F=\C\text{,}\) then by the fundamental theorem of algebra the characteristic polynomial factors as
      \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-\lambda_1)^{n_1}\cdots (x-\lambda_r)^{n_r}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      with \(\lambda_i\in \C\) for all \(i\text{.}\) Since \(T\) is self-adjoint, and since the \(\lambda_i\) are the eigenvalues of \(T\text{,}\) we conclude that in fact \(\lambda_i\in \R\) for all \(i\text{,}\) as desired.
      Now assume \(F=\R\text{.}\) We can compute the characteristic polynomia of \(T\) as
      \begin{equation*} f(x)=\det(xI-A)\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where \(A=[T]_B\) is any matrix representation of \(T\) with respect to an orthonormal basis \(B\text{.}\) Now consider the complex matrix transformation
      \begin{equation*} T_A\colon \C^n\rightarrow \C^n\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since \(T\) is self-adjoint and \(B\) is orthonormal, the matrix \(A=[T]_B\) is self-adjoint by Theorem 1.22.6. It then follows that \(T_A\colon \C^n\rightarrow \C^n\) is self-adjoint. From the previous case (\(F=\C\)), we conclude that the characteristic polynomial \(g(x)\) of \(T_A\) factors as
      \begin{equation*} g(x)=(x-\lambda_1)^{n_1}\cdots (x-\lambda_r)^{n_r} \end{equation*}
      with \(\lambda_i\in \R\text{.}\) But (again by definition) the characteristic polynomial of \(T_A\) is precisely \(g(x)=\det(xI-A)=f(x)\text{,}\) the characteristic polynomial of \(T\text{!}\) Thus \(f\) splits completely over \(\R\text{,}\) as desired.

    Theorem 1.23.13. Spectral theorem for self-adjoint.

    Let \(F\in \{\R,\C\}\text{,}\) let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be a finite-dimensional inner product space over \(F\text{,}\) and let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) with distinct eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(T\) is self-adjoint.
    2. \(\lambda_i\in \R\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) \(V=\bigoplus_{i=1}^r E(\lambda_i, T)\) and the eigenspaces \(E(\lambda_i, T)\) are pairwise orthogonal.
    3. \(T\) has real eigenvalues and is orthogonally diagonalizable.
    4. There is an orthogonal (or orthonormal) basis \(B\) such that \([T]_B=D\) is diagonal with real entries.

    Proof.

    We may proceed almost identically to the proof of Theorem 1.23.8. As in that proof we prove a cycle of implications (1)\(\implies\)(2)\(\implies\)(3)\(\implies\)(4)\(\implies\)(1). And as in that previous proof, the chain of implications (2)\(\implies\)(3)\(\implies\)(4)\(\implies\)(1) is easily established. We leave that mostly to the reader, but point out that for the implication (4)\(\implies\)(1), we need the entries of \(D\) to be real in order to have \(D^*=D\) (and hence \(T^*=T\)).
    Now consider the implication (1)\(\implies\)(2). Assume \(T\) is self-adjoint. Since in particular \(T\) is normal, we know that the eigenspaces of \(T\) are orthogonal by Theorem 1.23.7; and since \(T\) is self-adjoint we know that the eigenvalues of \(T\) are real by Theorem 1.23.12. It remains only to show that \(V=\bigoplus_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)\text{,}\) and we prove this using the exact same induction argument as in Theorem 1.23.8. As such we can in good conscience content ourselves with a sketch of how this argument plays out in this case:
    • Since the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) splits completelely over \(\R\text{,}\) there is an eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) and nonzero eigenspace \(E(\lambda_1,T)\text{.}\)
    • Consider the orthogonal decomposition \(V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{.}\)
    • Since \(T\) is self-adjoint (and hence normal) the space \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) is both \(T\)- and \(T^*\)-invariant.
    • Since \(T\) is self-adjoint, it follows easily that the restriction \(T\vert_W\) is also self-adjoint.
    • The induction hypothesis then applies to the smaller space \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{,}\) allowing us to decompose \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) into further eigenspaces of \(T\text{,}\) and giving us the full decomposition of \(V\) into eigenspaces of \(T\text{.}\)
