    Section 1.22 Normal operators

    Definition 1.22.1. Normal operator.

    Let \(T\) be a linear operator on the inner product space \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\text{,}\) and suppose \(T\) has an adjoint \(T^*\text{.}\) We say \(T\) is normal if it commutes with \(T^*\text{:}\) i.e., if \(T*T=TT^*\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.22.2. Self-adjoint, skew-Hermitian, unitary.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space. Below we define several special types of normal operators. Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) and suppose \(T\) has an adjoint \(T^*\text{.}\)
    • Self-adjoint.
      \(T\) is self-adjoint (or symmetric when \(F=\R\)) if \(T^*=T\text{.}\)
    • Skew-Hermitian.
      \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) is skew-Hermitian (or skew-symmetric when \(F=\R\)) if \(T^*=-T\text{.}\)
    • Unitary.
      \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) is unitary (or orthogonal when \(F=\R\)) if \(T\) is invertible and \(T^{-1}=T^*\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.22.3. Self-adjoint, skew-Hermitian, unitary.

    To show each of the subtypes described in Definition 1.22.2 is indeed normal we simply have to show that an operator of a given type satisfies \(T^*T=TT^*\text{.}\) This is not difficult:
    \begin{align*} \text{Self-adjoint}: T^*=T \amp \implies T^*T=TT=TT^*\\ \text{Skew-Hermitian}: T^*=-T \amp \implies T^*T=-TT=T(-T)=TT^*\\ \text{Unitary}: T^*=T^{-1} \amp \implies T^*T=T^{-1}T=I=TT^{-1}=TT^*\text{.} \end{align*}

    Example 1.22.4. Orthogonal projection.

    Let \(W\) be a finite-dimensional subspace of the inner product space \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\text{,}\) and let \(\proj_W\colon V\rightarrow V\) be orthogonal projection onto \(W\text{.}\) Show that \(\proj_W\) is self-adjoint.
    Solution.
    This is part of a homework exercise.

    Example 1.22.5. Derivative operator.

    Let \(W\) be the subspace of real periodic \(C^\infty\) functions on \(\R\) with period \(1\text{.}\) Recall that the derivative operator \(T\in \mathcal{L}(W)\) defined as \(T(f)=f'\) has adjoint \(T^*-T\) with respect to the inner product \(\angvec{f,g}=\int_0^1f(x)g(x)\, dx\text{.}\) Hence \(T\) is skew-symmetric. In particular, \(T\) is normal.
    The following matrix-based result gives us an easy way of producing normal operators of varying types.

    Theorem 1.22.6. Normality and matrix representations.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be a finite-dimensional inner product space, let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) let \(B\) be an orthonormal basis of \(V\text{,}\) and let \(A=[T]_B\text{.}\)
    • \(T\) is normal if and only if \(A^*A=AA^*\text{.}\)
    • \(T\) is self-adjoint if and only if \(A^*=A\text{.}\)
    • \(T\) is skew-symmetric if and only if \(A^*=-A\text{.}\)
    • \(T\) is unitary if and only if \(A\) is invertible and \(A^*=A^{-1}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    The equivalences are simple consequences of the following facts:
    • \([TS]_B=[T]_B[S]_B\) for all \(S,T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) (Corollary 1.13.17);
    • \([T^*]_B=[T]_B^*\) for all \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) (Corollary 1.21.11).
    The next theorem tells us that these various types of normality are preserved by familiar operations on linear transformations.

    Theorem 1.22.7. Normality preserved.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space. Let \(\operatorname{Norm}(V)\text{,}\) \(\operatorname{SelfAdj}(V)\text{,}\) \(\operatorname{Skew}(V)\text{,}\) and \(\operatorname{Uni}(V)\) denote the sets of normal, self-adjoint, skew-Hermitian, and unitary operators on \(V\text{,}\) respectively.
    1. \(T\in \operatorname{Norm}(V)/\operatorname{SelfAdj}(V)/\operatorname{Skew}(V)/\operatorname{Uni}(V)\) if and only if \(T^*\in\operatorname{Norm}(V)/\operatorname{SelfAdj}(V)/\operatorname{Skew}(V)/\operatorname{Uni}(V)\text{.}\)
    2. If \(S,T\in \operatorname{SelfAdj}(V) \text{,}\) then \(cS+dT\in \operatorname{SelfAdj}(V)\) for all \(c,d\in \R\text{.}\)
    3. If \(T\in \operatorname{Norm}(V)/\operatorname{SelfAdj}(V)/\operatorname{Uni}(V)\text{,}\) then \(T^k\in \operatorname{Norm}(V)/\operatorname{SelfAdj}(V)/\operatorname{Uni}(V)\) for any positive integer \(k\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    The statements follow easily from elementary properties of the adjoint (Theorem 1.21.5).

    Theorem 1.22.8. Polar identities.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space. For all \(v,w\in V\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation} \angvec{v,w}=\frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+w}^2-\norm{v-w}^2)+\frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+iw}^2-\norm{v-iw}^2)\, i\text{,}\tag{1.104} \end{equation}
    or equivalently,
    \begin{align} \Re \angvec{v,w} \amp = \frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+w}^2-\norm{v-w}^2)\tag{1.105}\\ \Im \angvec{v,w} \amp = \frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+iw}^2-\norm{v-iw}^2) \text{.}\tag{1.106} \end{align}
    As a consequence, if \(F=\R\text{,}\) then we have
    \begin{equation} \angvec{v,w}=\frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+w}^2-\norm{v-w}^2)\text{.}\tag{1.107} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    This is a simple, if somewhat tedious exercise of expanding out the right side of (1.104) using the definition \(\norm{u}^2=\angvec{u,u}\text{,}\) and then simplifying using properties of the inner product, and the fact that
    \begin{align*} \Re z \amp =\frac{1}{2}(z+\overline{z})\\ \Im z \amp =\frac{1}{2i}(z-\overline{z}) \end{align*}
    for all \(z\in \C\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.22.9. Normality equivalence.

    Let \(T\) be a linear operator on the inner product space \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(T\) is normal.
    2. \(\norm{T(v)}=\norm{T^*(v)}\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\)
    3. \(\angvec{T(v),T(w)}=\angvec{T^*(v),T^*(w)}\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    We prove a cycle of implications.
    If \(T\) is normal, then
    \begin{align*} \norm{T(v)}^2 \amp =\angvec{T(v),T(v)}\\ \amp =\angvec{v,T^*Tv}\\ \amp =\angvec{v,TT^*v} \amp (T^*T=TT^*)\\ \amp =\angvec{T^*(v),T^*(v)}\\ \amp =\norm{T^*(v)}^2\text{.} \end{align*}
    Taking square-roots of both sides (and using the fact that norms are nonnegative), we conclude that \(\norm{T(v)}=\norm{T^*(v)}\text{.}\) This proves (1)\(\implies\)(2).
    To prove (2)\(\implies\)(3), we use the polarity identity. If \(\norm{T(v)}=\norm{T^*(v)}\) for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) then we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{T(v),T(w)} \amp = \frac{1}{4}(\norm{T(v)+T(w)}^2-\norm{T(v)-T(w)}^2)+\frac{1}{4}(\norm{T(v)+iT(w)}^2-\norm{T(v)-iT(w)}^2)i\\ \amp =\frac{1}{4}(\norm{(T(v+w))}^2-\norm{T(v-w)}^2)+\frac{1}{4}(\norm{T(v+iw)}^2-\norm{T(v-iw)}^2)i\\ \amp = \frac{1}{4}(\norm{(T^*(v+w))}^2-\norm{T^*(v-w)}^2)+\frac{1}{4}(\norm{T^*(v+iw)}^2-\norm{T^*(v-iw)}^2)i\\ \amp =\frac{1}{4}(\norm{T^*(v)+T^*(w)}^2-\norm{T^*(v)-T^*(w)}^2)+\frac{1}{4}(\norm{T^*(v)+\overline{i}T^*(w)}^2-\norm{T^*(v)-\overline{i}T^*(w)}^2)i \\ \amp = \angvec{T^*(v),T^*(w)} \end{align*}

    Corollary 1.22.10. Normal operator properties.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space, and assume \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) is normal.
    1. \(T(v)=\boldzero\) if and only if \(T^*(v)=\boldzero\text{.}\) As a consequence, we have
      \begin{equation} \NS T=\NS T^*\text{.}\tag{1.108} \end{equation}
    2. \(T(v)=\lambda v\) if and only if \(T^*(v)=\overline{\lambda}v\text{.}\) As a consequence, we have
      \begin{equation} E(\lambda, T)=E(\overline{\lambda},T^*)\tag{1.109} \end{equation}
      for all \(\lambda\in F\text{.}\)
    3. Finite-dimensional space.
      Assume further that \(\dim V=n< \infty\text{.}\) We have
      \begin{equation} \im T=\im T^*\tag{1.110} \end{equation}
      and
      \begin{equation} V=\ker T\oplus \im T=\ker T^*\oplus \im T^*\text{.}\tag{1.111} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    Assume \(T\) is normal.
    1. We have
      \begin{align*} v\in \NS T \amp \iff T(v)=\boldzero\\ \amp \iff \norm{T(v)}=0\\ \amp \iff \norm{T^*(v)}=0\\ \amp \iff T^*(v)=\boldzero\\ \amp \iff v\in \NS T^*\text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus \(\NS T=\NS T^*\text{.}\)
    2. Since \(T\) and \(I_V\) are normal, so is, \(\lambda I_V-T\) for any \(\lambda\in F\) by Theorem 1.22.7. Applying the result in (1) to \(\lambda I_V-T\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} T(v)=\lambda v \amp \iff v\in E(\lambda, T) \\ \amp \iff v\in \NS (\lambda I_V-T)\\ \amp \iff v\in \NS(\lambda I_V-T)^*\\ \amp \iff v\in \NS(\overline{\lambda}I_V-T^*) \amp \knowl{./knowl/xref/th_adjoint_props.html}{\text{Theorem 1.21.5}} \\ \amp \iff v\in E(\overline{\lambda},T^*) \\ \amp \iff T^*(v)=\overline{\lambda}v\text{.} \end{align*}
    3. If we assume \(V\) is finite dimensional, then we have
      \begin{align*} \im T^* \amp =(\NS T)^\perp\\ \amp =(\NS T^*)^\perp\\ \amp = ((\im T)^\perp)^\perp\\ \amp =\im T\text{.} \end{align*}
      It follows that
      \begin{align*} V \amp = \NS T\oplus (\NS T)^\perp\\ \amp = \NS T \oplus \im T^*\\ \amp =\NS T\oplus \im T\\ \amp =\NS T^*\oplus \im T^*\text{.} \end{align*}
