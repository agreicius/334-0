Theorem 1.12.5. Isomorphisms.

Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
  1. \(T\) is an isomorphism.
  2. \(T\) is invertible.
  3. \(T\) is bijective.

Proof.

The implication (1)\(\implies\)(2) follows directly from the definition. Furthermore, the equivalence (2)\(\iff\)(3) is a purely set-theoretic result relating invertible and bijective functions. It remains then only to show that (2)\(\implies\)(1): i.e., that if \(T\) is linear and invertible, then its inverse \(T^{-1}\) is itself linear.
Assume \(T\) is linear and invertible. We will use the one-step technique to prove \(T^{-1}\) is linear. First, given any \(w,w'\in W\text{,}\) since \(T\) and \(T^{-1}\) are inverses of one another, there are vectors \(v,v'\in V\) satisfying
\begin{align*} T(v) \amp =w \amp T^{-1}(w)\amp= v\\ T(v') \amp =w' \amp T^{-1}(w')\amp= v'\text{.} \end{align*}
It then follows that given any scalars \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} T^{-1}(cw+dw') \amp = T^{-1}(cT(v)+dT(v'))\\ \amp =T^{-1}(T(cv+dv')) \amp (T \text{ linear})\\ \amp =cv+dv' \amp (T^{-1}T=I_V)\\ \amp =cT^{-1}(w)+dT^{-1}(w')\text{.} \end{align*}
