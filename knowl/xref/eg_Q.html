Example 1.1.16. Rational numbers.

Let \(\Q\) be the set of all rational numbers: i.e.,
\begin{equation*} \Q=\{r\in \R\colon r=m/n \text{ for some integers } m,n\in \Z \}\text{.} \end{equation*}
  1. Show that the restriction of real number addition and multiplication to \(\Q\) gives rise to operations
    \begin{align*} +\colon \Q\times \Q \amp \rightarrow \Q \amp \times\colon \Q\times \Q\amp \rightarrow \Q\text{.} \end{align*}
  2. Explain why \(\Q\text{,}\) together with these restricted operations, constitutes a field.
Solution.
  1. It is clear that we can add and multiply two rational numbers; what we need to show is that the result of applying either of these operations to two rational numbers is again a rational number. In other words, we must show that the sum of two rational numbers is a rational number, and the product of two rational numbers is a rational number. To this end, let \(r,s\in \Q\text{,}\) and write \(r=m/n\) and \(s=p/q\text{,}\) where \(m,n,p,q\in \Z\text{.}\) We then have
    \begin{align*} r+s \amp = \frac{mq+pn}{mn} \amp rs\amp=\frac{mp}{nq}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since \(mq+pn,mn, mp, nq\in \Z\text{,}\) we see that \(r+s, rs\in \Q\text{,}\) as desired.
  2. Once we know that real number addition and multiplication restrict to well-defined operations on \(\Q\text{,}\) that these restricted operations satisfy the field axioms follows directly from the fact that the original operations on \(\R\) satisfy these axioms. For example, since addition and multiplication are satisfy Axiom (i) for all real numbers, they certainly satisfy Axiom (i) for all rational numbers.
    Similarly, the existential Axioms (iv)-(vii) are easily seen to be satisfied for the operations restricted to \(\Q\) once we observe that \(0,1\in \Q\text{,}\) and that given any nonzero \(r=m/n\in \Q\text{,}\) we have \(r^{-1}=n/m\in \Q\text{.}\)
