Theorem 1.26.10. Diagonal plus nilpotent.

Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite-dimensional, and assume the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) splits completely over the base field. There are operators \(S,N\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) satisfying the following conditions:
  • \(T=S+N\text{;}\)
  • \(S\) is diagonalizable;
  • \(N\) is nilpotent (i.e., \(N^k=0_V\) for some \(k\in \Z_+\)).
In other words \(T\) is the sum of a diagonalizable and a nilpotent operator.

Proof.

Let \(B\) be a Jordan basis of \(T\text{,}\) and let
\begin{equation*} J=[T]_B=\begin{bmatrix}J_{1}\amp \\ \amp J_{2}\amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \\ \amp \amp \amp J_{\ell} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
be its corresponding Jordan form. It is not difficult to see that we can write
\begin{equation*} J=D+M\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(D\) is diagonal and \(M\) is upper triangular with zeros along the diagonal. Moreover, this implies that \(M^k=\boldzero\) for some \(k\in \Z\text{:}\) i.e., any such \(M\) is nilpotent. Since the matrix representation operator \([\phantom{A}]_B\) is an isomorphism of \(\mathcal{L}(V)\) to \(F^{n,n}\text{,}\) \(n=\dim V\text{,}\) there are operators \(S, N\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) satisfying
\begin{equation*} [S]_B=D, [N]_B=M\text{.} \end{equation*}
Furthermore, since \(D\) is diagonal and \(M\) is a nilpotent matrix, it follows that \(S\) is diagonalizable and \(N\) is a nilpotent operator. Lastly, since
\begin{equation*} [S+N]_B=[S]_B+[N]_B=D+M=J=[T]_B\text{,} \end{equation*}
and since \([\phantom{A}]_B\) is bijective, it follows that \(T=S+N\text{.}\)
