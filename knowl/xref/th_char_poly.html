Theorem 1.16.5. Characteristic polynomial of a matrix.

Let \(A\in F^{n,n}\text{,}\) and let \(f(x)=\det(xI-A)\) be its characteristic polynomial.
  1. \(\lambda\in F\) is an eigenvalue of \(T_A\) if and only if \(f(\lambda)=0\text{.}\)
  2. \(f\) is a monic polynomial of degree \(n\text{,}\) and we have
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=x^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots +a_1x+a_0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{align*} a_{n-1} \amp = \tr A\\ a_0 \amp = (-1)^n\det A\text{.} \end{align*}
  3. If \(A'=P^{-1}AP\) for some invertible matrix \(P\in F^{n,n}\text{,}\) then \(A'\) and \(A\) have the same characteristic polynomial. In other words, similar matrices have the same characteristic polynomial.

Proof.

  1. We have
    \begin{align*} \lambda \text{ an eigenvalue of } T_A \amp \iff \NS (\lambda I-T_A)\ne \{\boldzero\} \\ \amp \iff \NS (\lambda I-A)\ne \{\boldzero\}\\ \amp \iff \lambda I-A \text{ is not invertible}\\ \amp \iff \det (\lambda I-A)=0\\ \amp \iff f(\lambda)=0\text{.} \end{align*}
  2. This proved by induction on \(n\text{,}\) using various properties of the determinant. You can find a proof of this in my textbook for a first course in linear algebra

    agreicius.github.io/linear-algebra/s_eigenvectors.html
    .
  3. Let \(f_A(x)\) and \(f_{A'}(x)\) be the characteristic polynomials of \(A\) and \(A'\text{,}\) respectively. We have
    \begin{align*} f_{A'}(x) \amp =\det(xI-A')\\ \amp = \det(xI-P^{-1}AP)\\ \amp = \det(P^{-1}(xI-A)P) \amp (\text{matrix mult. props.})\\ \amp = \det P^{-1}\det(xI-A)\det P \amp (\det A_1A_2=\det A_1\det A_2) \\ \amp = \det(xI-A) \amp (\det P^{-1}=(\det P)^{-1})\\ \amp = f_A(x)\text{,} \end{align*}
    as claimed.
