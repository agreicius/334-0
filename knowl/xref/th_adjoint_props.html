Theorem 1.21.4. Adjoint properties.

Let \((V,\angvec{\, }_V)\text{,}\) \((W,\angvec{\, }_W)\text{,}\) and \((U,\angvec{\, ,}_U)\) be inner product spaces, let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) and let \(S\in \mathcal{L}(W,U)\text{.}\)
  1. There is at most one adjoint of \(T\text{:}\) i.e., if there exists a function \(T^*\) satisfying the adjoint property, then it is unique.
  2. An adjoint of \(T\) is linear: i.e., if \(T^*\) exists, then \(T^*\in \mathcal{W,V}\)
  3. If \(T^*\) exists, then so does \((T^*)^*\text{.}\) Moreover, in this case we have
    \begin{equation} (T^*)^*=T\text{.}\tag{1.86} \end{equation}
  4. If \(T^*\) and \(S^*\) exist, then so does \((ST)^*\text{.}\) Moreover, in this case we have
    \begin{equation} (ST)^*=T^*S^*\text{.}\tag{1.87} \end{equation}

Proof.

  1. Suppose \(T^*\colon W\rightarrow V\) and \(S\colon W\rightarrow V\) are adjoint functions of \(T\text{:}\) i.e., \(T^*\) and \(S\) both satisfy (1.84). It follows that for all \(v\in V\) and \(w\in W\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{v,S(w)-T^*(w)} \amp =\angvec{v,S(w)}-\angvec{v,T^*(w)} \\ \amp = \angvec{T(v),w}-\angvec{T(v),w} \amp (S, T^* \text{ adj. of } T) \\ \amp = 0\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since \(\angvec{v,S(w)-T^*(w)}=0\) for all \(v\text{,}\) it follows from Theorem 1.18.9 that \(S(w)-T^*(w)=0\) for all \(w\in W\text{,}\) and hence that \(S=T^*\text{,}\) as desired.
  2. Assume an adjoint \(T^*\colon V\rightarrow W\) exists. We use the 1-step technique to show \(T^*\) is linear. Given any \(w_1,w_2\in W\) and \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) we will show that \(T^*(cw_1+dw_2)=cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2)\) by proving that
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{T^*(cw_1+dw_2),v}=\angvec{cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2),v} \end{equation*}
    for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) using Theorem 1.18.9. To this end, we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{T^*(cw_1+dw_2),v} \amp = \angvec{cw_1+dw_2,T(v)}\\ \amp = c\angvec{w_1,T(v)}+d\angvec{w_2,T(v)}\\ \amp = c\angvec{T^*(w_1),v}+d\angvec{T^*(w_2),v}\\ \amp =\angvec{cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2),v} \end{align*}
    for all \(v\text{.}\) Thus \(T^*(cw_1+dw_2)=cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2)\text{,}\) as desired.
  3. Assume \(T^*\) and \(S^*\) exists. By uniqueness of adjoints, it suffices to show that \(T^*S^*\) satisfies the adjoint property. We have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{v,T^*S^*(u)} \amp = \angvec{v,T^*(S^*(u))} \\ \amp =\angvec{S(v),T^*(u)}\\ \amp =\angvec{T(S(v)),u}\\ \amp = \angvec{TS(v),u} \end{align*}
    for all \(v\in V\) and \(u\in U\text{.}\) Since this property uniquely defines the adjoint \((TS)^*\text{,}\) we conclude that \((TS)^*=S^*T^*\text{.}\)
  4. Assume \(T^*\) exists. From (2) we know that \(T^*\) is linear. That \(T\) is its adjoint follows now from the equivalence of the two properties (1.84)–(1.85):i.e., we can think of the first as saying that \(T^*\) is an adjoint of \(T\text{,}\) and the second as saying that \(T\) is an adjoint of \(T^*\text{.}\)
