Example 1.17.6. Transposition.

Let \(S\colon F^{2,2}\rightarrow F^{2,2}\) be defined as \(S(A)=A^T\text{.}\) Decide whether \(S\) is diagonalizable; if it is, find an eigenbasis \(B\) of \(S\) and compute \([S]_B\text{.}\)
Solution.
We have already done most of the work of Procedure 1.17.5 for this linear operator in Example 1.15.15. There we saw that \(1\) and \(-1\) are the only eigenvalues of \(S\text{,}\) and the the bases of \(E(1,S)\) and \(E(-1,S)\) are
\begin{align*} B_1=\left( \begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\right) \amp \\ B_2 \amp =\left(\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ -1\amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \right)\text{,} \end{align*}
respectively. It follows that
\begin{align*} \dim E(1,S) \amp =\len B_1=3\\ \dim E(-1,S) \amp =\len B_2=1\text{.} \end{align*}
Since we have
\begin{equation*} \dim F^{2,2}=4=3+1=\dim E(1,S)+\dim E(-1,S)\text{,} \end{equation*}
we conclude that \(S\) is diagonalizable, and furthermore that
\begin{equation*} B=\left(\begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ -1\amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\right) \end{equation*}
is an eigenbasis of \(S\text{.}\)
