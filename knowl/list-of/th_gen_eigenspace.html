Theorem 1.25.6. Generalized eigenspaces.

Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional, and assume the characteristic polynomial of \(f\) splits completely over the base field \(F\text{.}\)
  1. For all \(\lambda\in F\text{,}\) \(G(\lambda, T)\) is a \(T\)-invariant subspace and \(E(\lambda, T)\subseteq G(\lambda, T)\text{.}\)
  2. \(G(\lambda, T)\cap G(\lambda', T)=\{\boldzero\}\) for \(\lambda\ne \lambda'\in F\text{.}\)
  3. Let \(\lambda\) be an eigenvalue of \(T\) of algebraic multiplicity \(m\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation} 1\leq \dim E(\lambda, T)\leq \dim G(\lambda, T)\leq m\tag{1.126} \end{equation}
    and
    \begin{equation} G(\lambda, T)=\NS(T-\lambda I)^m\text{.}\tag{1.127} \end{equation}

Proof.

  1. For each \(k\in \Z_+\) define \(W_k=\NS(T-\lambda I)^k\text{,}\) which is a subspace by Theorem 1.10.4. It is easy to see that
    \begin{equation*} W_k\subseteq W_{k+1} \end{equation*}
    and that \(G(\lambda, T)=\bigcup_{k=0}^{\infty} W_k\text{.}\) Although in general the union of subspaces is not a subspace, the union of nested subspaces is a subspace, as we have had occasion to prove elsewhere. Thus \(W\) is a subspace. In general \(\NS S^k\) is \(S\)-invariant for any linear transformation \(S\text{.}\) Thus, letting \(N=T-\lambda I\text{,}\) we see that \(W_k\) is \(N\)-invariant for all \(k\text{.}\) It follows easily that the nested union \(G(\lambda, T)=\bigcup_{k=0}^\infty W_k\) is also \(N\)-invariant. Lastly, since a subspace being \(N\)-invariant implies it is \(g(N)\)-invariant for any polynomial \(g\in P(F)\text{,}\) and since \(T=g(N)\text{,}\) where \(g(x)=x+\lambda\text{,}\) we see that \(G(\lambda, T)\) is \(T\)-invariant.
    Lastly, since \(E(\lambda, T)=\NS (T-\lambda I)=W_1\text{,}\) we see that \(E(\lambda, T)\subseteq G(\lambda, T)\text{.}\)
  2. Fix scalars \(\lambda\ne \lambda'\text{,}\) and suppose \(v\in G(\lambda, T)\cap G(\lambda', T)\text{.}\) By definition, this means that \((T-\lambda I)^k(v)=(T-\lambda'I)^{\ell}(v)=\boldzero\) for some positive integers \(k\) and \(\ell\text{.}\) Since \(\lambda\ne\lambda'\text{,}\) the polynomials \((x-\lambda)^k\) and \((x-\lambda')^\ell\) are relatively prime. By Theorem 1.25.2, we have
    \begin{equation*} 1=f(x)(x-\lambda)^k+g(x)(x-\lambda')^\ell \end{equation*}
    for some polynomials \(f,g\in P(F)\text{,}\) and thus also
    \begin{equation*} I_V=f(T)(T-\lambda)^k+g(T)(T-\lambda')^\ell\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It follows that
    \begin{align*} v \amp =I_V(v)\\ \amp =f(T)(T-\lambda)^k(v)+g(T)(T-\lambda')^\ell(v)\\ \amp =\boldzero+\boldzero\\ \amp =\boldzero\text{.} \end{align*}
    We conclude that \(G(\lambda, T)\cap G(\lambda', T)=\{\boldzero\}\text{.}\)
  3. Since \(E(\lambda, T)\subseteq G(\lambda, T)\text{,}\) and since \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue, we have
    \begin{equation*} 1\leq \dim E(\lambda, T)\leq \dim G(\lambda, T)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of algebraic multiplicity \(m\text{,}\) and since the characteristic polynomial \(f\) splits completely, we have a factorization of the form
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-\lambda)^m\prod_{i=2}^r(x-\lambda_i)^{m_i}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\lambda\) and the \(\lambda_i\) are all distinct. Let \(W=G(\lambda, T)\text{.}\) Since \(W\) is \(T\)-invariant, the characteristic polynomial \(h\) of \(T\vert_W\) divides \(f\) (Theorem 1.24.2). Since \(G(\lambda, T)\cap G(\lambda_i, T)=\{\boldzero\}\text{,}\) \(\lambda_i\) cannot be an eigenvalue of \(T\vert_W\) for all \(i\geq 2\text{,}\) and thus \((x-\lambda_i)\nmid h(x)\) for all \(i\geq 2\text{.}\) It follows that \(h(x)=(x-\lambda)^{\dim W}\text{.}\) Lastly, since
    \begin{equation*} (x-\lambda)^{\dim W}\mid f(x)=(x-\lambda)^m\prod_{i=2}^r(x-\lambda_i)^{m_i}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    it follows from the uniqueness claim in Theorem 1.25.3, that \(\dim W\leq m\text{,}\) as desired.
