Theorem 1.24.10. Minimal polynomial.

Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional and let \(m_T(x)\) be the minimal polymonial of \(T\text{.}\) If \(g\in P(F)\) satisfies \(g(T)=0_V\text{,}\) then \(m_T\mid g\text{:}\) i.e., we have
\begin{equation*} g(x)=q(x)m_T(x) \end{equation*}
for some \(q\in P(F)\text{.}\) In particular, if \(f\) is the characteristic polynomial of \(g\text{,}\) then \(m_T(x)\mid f\text{.}\)

Proof.

Assume \(g(T)=0_V\) for the polynomial \(g\in P(F)\text{.}\) Using the division algorithm (DefinitionÂ 1.24.1), we can write
\begin{equation*} g(x)=q(x)m_T(x)+r(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(\deg r(x)^lt; \deg m_T(x)\text{.}\) We thus have
\begin{equation*} 0_V=g(T)=q(T)m_T(T)+r(T)=q(T)0_V+r(T)=r(T)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Thus \(r(T)=0_V\text{.}\) Since by definition \(m_T(x)\) is the nonconstant polynomial of minimal degree satisfying \(m_T(T)=0_V\text{,}\) we conclude that \(r(x)\) is a constant polynomial. Since \(r(T)=0_V\text{,}\) it follows that \(r(x)=0\text{,}\) the zero polynomial. But then \(g(x)=q(x)m_T(x)\text{,}\) showing that \(m_T\mid g\text{,}\) as desired.
