Theorem 1.26.9. Similarity.

Let \(A, A'\in \F^{n,n}\) and suppose both characteristic polynomials factor completely over \(\F\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
  1. \(A\) and \(A'\) are similar (i.e. \(A'=P^{-1}AP\) for some invertible \(P\)).
  2. \(T_A\) and \(T_A'\) have the same Jordan form.

Proof.

Assume \(A\) and \(A'\) are similar, and thus that there is an invertible matrix \(Q\) such that \(A=Q^{-1}A'Q\text{.}\) Let \(J\) be a Jordan form for \(T_A\text{.}\) By definition, there is a basis \(B'\) such that
\begin{equation*} [T_A]_{B'}=J\text{.} \end{equation*}
Let \(B\) be the standard basis, and let \(P=\underset{B'\to B}{P}\text{.}\) Using the change of basis formula, we have
\begin{equation*} J=P^{-1}AP\text{,} \end{equation*}
and thus
\begin{equation*} J=P^{-1}Q^{-1}A'QP=(QP)^{-1}A'(QP)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Equivalently, letting \(B''\) be the basis consisting of the columns of \(QP\text{,}\) we see that
\begin{equation*} J=[T_{A'}]_{B''}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Thus \(A\) and \(A'\) have the same Jordan form.
Now assume \(A\) and \(A'\) have the same Jordan form \(J\text{.}\) Reasoning as above, this implies that there are invertible matrices \(P\) and \(Q\) such that
\begin{equation*} J=P^{-1}AP=Q^{-1}A'Q\text{.} \end{equation*}
It follows that
\begin{equation*} A=PQ^{-1}A'QP^{-1}=(QP^{-1})^{-1}A'(QP^{-1})\text{,} \end{equation*}
showing that \(A\) and \(A'\) are similar.
