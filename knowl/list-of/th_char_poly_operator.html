Theorem 1.16.8. Characteristic polynomial of operator.

Let \(T\in\mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(\dim V=n< \infty\text{,}\) and let \(f(x)=x^n+a_{n-1}x+\cdots +a_1x+a_0\) be the characteristic polynomial of \(T\text{.}\)
  1. The polynomial \(f\) can be factored over \(F\) as
    \begin{align} f(x) \amp =(x-\lambda_1)^{n_1}(x-\lambda_2)^{n_2}\cdots (x-\lambda_r)^{n_r}g(x)\text{,}\tag{1.48} \end{align}
    where \(\lambda_i\in F\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) \(\lambda_i\ne \lambda_j\) for \(i\ne j\text{,}\) and \(g(x)\in P(F)\) is a polynomial with no roots in \(F\text{.}\) It follows that \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\) are the distinct eigenvalues of \(T\text{.}\)
  2. \(T\) has at most \(n=\dim V\) distinct eigenvalues.

Proof.

  1. Let \(A=[T]_B\) be any matrix representation of \(T\text{.}\) We know from Theorem 1.16.1 that the eigenvalues of \(A\) are precisely the eigenvalues of \(T\text{.}\) Furthermore, by Theorem 1.16.5, the eigenvalues of \(A\) are precisely the roots of \(f\) lying in the base field \(F\text{.}\) Elementary polynomial properties imply that a scalar \(\lambda\) is a root of \(f(x)\) if and only if we can factor \(f\) as
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-\lambda)q(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where the quotient \(q(x)\) is a polynomial of degree \(n-1\text{.}\) Continuing this factoring out of roots process until the quotient polynomial has no roots in \(F\text{,}\) we see that we can factor \(f\) as in (1.48). From that factorization, we see that \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\) are the distinct roots of \(f\text{,}\) and hence the distinct eigenvalues of \(T\text{.}\)
  2. As we have mentioned before, a polynomial in \(P(F)\) of degree \(n\geq 0\) can have at most \(n\) distinct roots in \(F\text{.}\) Thus the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) can have at most \(n\) distinct roots in \(F\text{,}\) from which it follows that \(T\) can have at most \(n\) distinct eigenvalues.
