Example 1.16.11. Complex matrix transformation.

Let \(\theta\in (0,2\pi)\text{,}\) let \(A=\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta \amp -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta\amp \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) and let \(T_A\colon \C^2\rightarrow \C^2\) be the corresponding matrix transformation.
  1. Compute the characteristic polynomial of \(A\text{.}\)
  2. Determine the eigenvalue of \(T_A\text{.}\)
Solution.
  1. We compute the characteristic polynomial \(f(x)=\det(xI-A)=x^2-2\cos\theta x+1\text{,}\) exactly as in Example 1.16.10.
  2. Using the complex version of the quadratic formula, we can factor \(f\) over \(\C\) as \(f(x)=(x-\lambda_1)(x-\lambda_2)\text{,}\) where
    \begin{align*} \lambda_1\amp =\cos\theta+i\sin\theta \amp \lambda_2\amp =\cos\theta-i\sin\theta\text{.} \end{align*}
    Note that since \(\theta\in (0,2\pi)\) we have \(-\sin^2\theta< 0\text{,}\) from whence it follows that the two complex square-roots of \(-\sin^2\theta\) are \(i\sin\theta\) and \(-i\sin\theta\text{.}\) Using the Euler formula \(e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\text{,}\) we can further express these eigenvalues as
    \begin{align*} \lambda_1 \amp = e^{i\theta} \amp \lambda_2\amp =e^{-i\theta}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Finally, observe that in contrast to the linear operator \(T_A\colon \R^2\rightarrow \R^2\text{,}\) which has no eigenvalues (Example 1.16.10), the linear operator \(T_A\colon \C^2\rightarrow \C^2\) has two distinct (non-real) eigenvalues.
