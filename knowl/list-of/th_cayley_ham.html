Theorem 1.24.8. Cayley-Hamilton theorem.

Let \(T\in\mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional, and let \(f\in P(F)\) be the characteristic polynomial of \(T\text{.}\) We have
\begin{equation} f(T)=0_V\text{.}\tag{1.122} \end{equation}
In other words, \(T\) is a “root” of its own characteristic polynomial.

Proof.

It suffices to show that \(f(T)(v)=\boldzero\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) This is clearly true for \(v=\boldzero\text{.}\) Let \(v\in V\) be nonzero and let \(W=\Span (T^i(v))_{i=0}^\infty\) be the \(T\)-cyclic subspace it generates. By Theorem 1.24.7, \(W\) has a basis \(B\) of the form
\begin{equation*} B=(v,T(v), \dots, T^{k-1}(v)) \end{equation*}
for some \(k\text{,}\) in which case \(\dim W=\len B=k\text{,}\) and we have
\begin{equation*} T^k(v)=a_0v+a_1T(v)+\cdots +T^{k-1}(v) \end{equation*}
for some scalars \(a_i\in F\text{.}\) From the nature of \(B\text{,}\) and the representation of \(T^k(v)\) above, it follows that the restriction \(T\vert_W\) has matrix representation
\begin{equation*} A=[T\vert_W]_B=\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 0\amp \dots \amp 0 \amp a_0\\ 1\amp 0 \amp \amp \amp a_1 \\ 0\amp 1 \amp \ddots\amp \amp \vdots\\ \vdots \amp \amp \ddots \amp 0 \amp \\ 0 \amp \dots\amp 0 \amp 1 \amp a_{k-1} \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
An easy proof by induction shows that the characteristic polynomial of a matrix like \(A\) is
\begin{equation*} g(x)=x^k-a_{k-1}x^{k-1}+\cdots +a_1x+a_0\text{.} \end{equation*}
Note that
\begin{equation*} g(T)(v)=T^k(v)-a_{k-1}T^{k-1}+\cdots +a_1T+a_0v=\boldzero \end{equation*}
since \(T^k(v)=\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}a_iT^i(v)\text{.}\) Lastly, since \(g\mid f\) by Theorem 1.24.2, we have \(f(x)=q(x)g(x)\) for some polynomial \(q\in P(F)\text{,}\) and hence
\begin{equation*} f(T)(v)=q(T)g(T)(v)=q(T)(\boldzero)=\boldzero\text{,} \end{equation*}
as desired.
