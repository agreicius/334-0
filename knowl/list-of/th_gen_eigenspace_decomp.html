Theorem 1.25.7. Generalized eigenspace decomposition.

Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional, and assume the characteristic polynomial \(f\) of \(T\) factors as
\begin{equation*} f(x)=\prod_{i=1}^r(x-\lambda_i)^{n_i}\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\) are the distinct eigenvalues of \(T\text{.}\) We have the following direct sum decomposition of \(V\text{:}\)
\begin{equation} V=\oplus_{i=1}^rG(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\tag{1.128} \end{equation}
It follows that
\begin{equation} \dim G(\lambda_i, T)=n_i\tag{1.129} \end{equation}
for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{.}\) In other words, the dimension of the generalized \(\lambda\)-eigenspace is equal to the algebraic multiplicity of the eigenvalue \(\lambda_i\text{.}\)

Proof.

We will first show that \(V=\sum_{i=1}^r G(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\) For each \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) define
\begin{equation*} f_i(x)=\prod_{j\ne i}(x-\lambda_j)^{n_j}=\frac{f(x)}{(x-\lambda_i)^{n_i}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
The list \(f_1,f_2,\dots, f_r\) is easily seen to be relatively prime: any nonconstant monic common irreducible factor of all the \(f_i\) is an irreducible factor of \(f\) (since \(f_i\mid f\)), and thus must be \(x-\lambda_k\) for some \(k\text{;}\) however, by design \((x-\lambda_k)\mid f_k(x)\text{;}\) thus there is no nonconstant irreducible factor common to all the \(f_i\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.25.2, it follows that we have
\begin{equation*} 1=\sum_{i=1}^rg_i(x)f_i(x) \end{equation*}
for some polynomials \(g_i\in P(F)\text{,}\) and hence (evaluating the left- and right-side polynomials above at \(T\))
\begin{equation*} I_V=\sum_{i=1}^rg_i(T)f_i(T)\text{.} \end{equation*}
It follows that for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} v \amp = I_V(v)\\ \amp = sum_{i=1}^rg_i(T)f_i(T)(v)\text{.} \end{align*}
Let \(w_i=f_i(T)(v)\text{.}\) We have
\begin{align*} (T-\lambda I)^{n_i}w_i \amp = (T-\lambda I)^{n_i}f_i(T)(v)\\ \amp =f(T)v \amp ((x-\lambda_i)^{n_i}f_i(x)=f(x))\\ \amp =\boldzero \amp (\text{Cayley-Ham.})\text{.} \end{align*}
Thus \(w_i=f_i(T)(v)\in G(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\) Since \(G(\lambda_i, T)\) is \(T\)-invariant, it follows that \(g_i(T)(w_i)=g_i(T)f_i(T)(v)\in G(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\) Setting \(v_i=g_i(T)f_i(T)(v)\text{,}\) we conclude that we can write
\begin{equation*} v=\sum_{i=1}^rv_i \end{equation*}
with \(v_i\in G(\lambda_i, T)\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{.}\) This proves \(V=\sum_{i=1}^rG(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\)
Finally we show that this subspace sum is in fact a direct sum. Letting \(n=\dim V\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} \dim V\amp =\dim \sum_{i=1}^rG(\lambda_i, T) \amp (V=\sum_{i=1}^rG(\lambda_i, T)) \\ \amp \leq \sum_{i=1}^r\dim G(\lambda_i, T) \\ \amp \leq \sum_{i=1}^rn_i (\knowl{./knowl/xref/th_gen_eigenspace.html}{\text{1.25.6}}) \\ \amp = n \\ \amp = \dim V\text{,} \end{align*}
where the last line follows from the fact that
\begin{equation*} n=\deg f=\sum_{i=1}^r \deg (x-\lambda_i)^{n_i}=\sum_{i=1}^{r}n_i\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since the beginning and end of our chain of equalities above are in equal, we conclude that all the inequalities involved are in fact equalities, and hence that
\begin{align*} \dim \sum_{i=1}^rG(\lambda_i, T) \amp = \sum_{i=1}^r\dim G(\lambda_i, T) \\ \sum_{i=1}^r\dim G(\lambda_i, T) \amp = \sum_{i=1}^rn_i \text{.} \end{align*}
The first equality implies \(V=\oplus_{i=1}^rG(\lambda_i, T)\) by Theorem 1.8.13; the second equality implies that \(\dim G(\lambda_i, T)=n_i\) for all \(i\text{.}\)
