Theorem 1.7.12. Finite spanning set.

Assume \(B=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_n)\) is a spanning set of the vector space \(V\) of finite length \(n\text{.}\) Any tuple of vectors of \(V\) of length greater than \(n\) (including infinite tuples) is linearly dependent. Equivalently, if \(S=(w_i)_{i\in I}\) is a linearly independent tuple of vectors of \(V\text{,}\) then \(\abs{I}\leq n\text{.}\)

Proof.

Axler provides a pleasant proof where one does not have to dirty oneâ€™s hands with matrices. Personally, I enjoy digging in the dirt sometimes. In any case the argument below is a useful reminder about properties of linear systems.
First observe that it suffices to show that any finite tuple of length \(m> n\) is linearly dependent, since if \((w_i)_{i\in I}\) is an infinite tuple, then the subtuple \((w_{i_1},\dots, w_{i_{n+1}})\) is linearly dependent for any distinct list of \(n+1\) indices \(i_1,i_2,\dots, i_{n+1}\text{.}\) But then clearly the entire tuple is also linearly dependent since a nontrivial linear combination \(\boldzero=\sum_{k=1}^{n+1}c_{i_{k}}w_{i_k}=\boldzero\) gives rise to a nontrivial linear combination \(\sum_{i\in I}c_iw_i=\boldzero\) of all of the vectors \(v_i\) simply by setting \(c_i=0\) for \(i\ne i_k\text{.}\) (This argument shows that in general a tuple \((w_i)_{i\in I}\) is linearly dependent if and only if some finite subtuple \((w_{i_1}, w_{i_2},\dots, w_{i_m})\) is linearly dependent.)
It remains to show that any tuple \((w_1,w_2,\dots , w_{m})\) of \(V\) of length \(m> n\) is linearly dependent. Since \(B=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_n)\) is a spanning set of \(V\) we may write, for each \(1\leq j\leq m\)
\begin{equation*} w_j=\sum_{i=1}^na_{ij}v_i \end{equation*}
where \(a_{ij}\in F\text{.}\) We now show that there is a nontrivial linear combination \(\sum_{j=1}^mc_jw_j=\boldzero\text{.}\) Indeed, we have
\begin{align*} \sum_{j=1}^mc_jw_j=\boldzero \amp \iff \sum_{j=1}^mc_j(\sum_{i=1}^na_{ij}v_i)=\boldzero\\ \amp \iff \sum_{i=1}^n(\sum_{j=1}^ma_{ij}c_j)v_i=\boldzero\text{.} \end{align*}
It follows that to produce a nontrivial linear combination of the \(w_j\) equal to \(\boldzero\text{,}\) it suffices to find a nonzero \(m\)-tuple \((c_1,c_2,\dots, c_m)\) satisfying
\begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^ma_{ij}c_j=0 \end{equation*}
for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{,}\) or equivalently, a nonzero solution to the matrix equation
\begin{equation*} \underset{n\times m}{A}\, \underset{m\times 1}{\boldx}=\underset{n\times 1}{\boldzero}\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(A=[a_{ij}]\text{.}\) Since \(A\) has more columns than rows (\(m> n\)), our Gaussian elimination technique for solving such matrix equations gives rise to a free variable: \(A\) row reduces to a matrix \(U\) in row echelon form, one of whose columns does not have a leading one. The existence of a free variable in turn guarantees that there are infinitely many solutions to \(A\boldx=\boldzero\text{.}\) In particular, there is some nontrivial solution \(\boldc=(c_1,c_2,\dots, c_m)\ne \boldzero\text{.}\) Our argument above implies that \(\sum_{j=1}^mc_jw_j=\boldzero\) is then our desired nontrivial linear combination equal to \(\boldzero\text{.}\)
