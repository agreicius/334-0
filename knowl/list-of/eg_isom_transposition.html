Example 1.12.7. Transposition.

Define \(S\colon F^{m,n}\rightarrow F^{n,m}\) as \(S(A)=A^T\text{.}\) Prove that \(S\) is an isomorphism.
Solution.
We saw in ExampleÂ 1.9.15 the the transposition operation is linear. To show it is an isomorphism, we produce an inverse. Define \(S^{-1}\colon F^{n,m}\rightarrow F^{m,n}\) as \(S^{-1}(B)=A^T\text{.}\) (Note that even though the rule for \(S^{-1}\) is the same as that for \(S\) (i.e., take the transpose), we do not necessarily have \(S=S^{-1}\) as functions since their domains (and codomains) are not necessarily equal.) We have
\begin{align*} SS^{-1}(B) \amp =S(B^T)=(B^T)^T=B \text{ for all } B\in F^{n,m}\\ S^{-1}S(A) \amp =S(A^T)=(A^T)^T=A \text{ for all } A\in F^{m,n}\text{,} \end{align*}
proving that the two maps are indeed inverses of one another. We conclude that \(S\) is invertible, and hence an isomorphism.
