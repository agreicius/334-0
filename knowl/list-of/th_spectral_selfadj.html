Theorem 1.23.13. Spectral theorem for self-adjoint.

Let \(F\in \{\R,\C\}\text{,}\) let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be a finite-dimensional inner product space over \(F\text{,}\) and let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) with distinct eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
  1. \(T\) is self-adjoint.
  2. \(\lambda_i\in \R\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) \(V=\bigoplus_{i=1}^r E(\lambda_i, T)\) and the eigenspaces \(E(\lambda_i, T)\) are pairwise orthogonal.
  3. \(T\) has real eigenvalues and is orthogonally diagonalizable.
  4. There is an orthogonal (or orthonormal) basis \(B\) such that \([T]_B=D\) is diagonal with real entries.

Proof.

We may proceed almost identically to the proof of Theorem 1.23.8. As in that proof we prove a cycle of implications (1)\(\implies\)(2)\(\implies\)(3)\(\implies\)(4)\(\implies\)(1). And as in that previous proof, the chain of implications (2)\(\implies\)(3)\(\implies\)(4)\(\implies\)(1) is easily established. We leave that mostly to the reader, but point out that for the implication (4)\(\implies\)(1), we need the entries of \(D\) to be real in order to have \(D^*=D\) (and hence \(T^*=T\)).
Now consider the implication (1)\(\implies\)(2). Assume \(T\) is self-adjoint. Since in particular \(T\) is normal, we know that the eigenspaces of \(T\) are orthogonal by Theorem 1.23.7; and since \(T\) is self-adjoint we know that the eigenvalues of \(T\) are real by Theorem 1.23.12. It remains only to show that \(V=\bigoplus_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)\text{,}\) and we prove this using the exact same induction argument as in Theorem 1.23.8. As such we can in good conscience content ourselves with a sketch of how this argument plays out in this case:
  • Since the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) splits completelely over \(\R\text{,}\) there is an eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) and nonzero eigenspace \(E(\lambda_1,T)\text{.}\)
  • Consider the orthogonal decomposition \(V=E(\lambda_1,T)\oplus E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{.}\)
  • Since \(T\) is self-adjoint (and hence normal) the space \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) is both \(T\)- and \(T^*\)-invariant.
  • Since \(T\) is self-adjoint, it follows easily that the restriction \(T\vert_W\) is also self-adjoint.
  • The induction hypothesis then applies to the smaller space \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\text{,}\) allowing us to decompose \(W=E(\lambda_1,T)^\perp\) into further eigenspaces of \(T\text{,}\) and giving us the full decomposition of \(V\) into eigenspaces of \(T\text{.}\)
