Theorem 1.16.1. Eigenspaces and matrix representations.

Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(\dim V=n< \infty\text{,}\) let \(B\) be a basis for \(V\text{,}\) and let \(A=[T]_B\text{.}\)
  1. \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\colon V\rightarrow V\) if and only if \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^n\text{.}\)
  2. For all \(\lambda\in F\text{,}\) the restriction of the coordinate vector map \([\phantom{v}]_B\colon V\rightarrow F^n \) to \(E(\lambda, T)\) yields an isomorphism
    \begin{align*} [\phantom{v}]_B\colon E(\lambda, T)\amp \rightarrow E(\lambda, T_A) \amp \\ v \amp \mapsto [v]_B\text{.} \end{align*}
    As a result we can produce a basis of \(E(\lambda, T)\) by computing a basis of \(E(\lambda, T_A)\) and “lifting” this to a basis of \(E(\lambda, T)\) using the inverse map \([\phantom{v}]_B^{-1}\text{.}\)

Proof.

Since \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\) (resp., \(T_A\)) if and only if \(E(\lambda, T)\) (resp., \(E(\lambda, T_A\))) is nonzero, we see that (1) follows directly from (2).
Furthermore, since
\begin{align*} E(\lambda, T)\amp =\NS(\lambda I_V-T) \amp \\ E(\lambda, T_A) \amp = \NS(\lambda I_{F^n}-T_A)=\NS(\lambda I-A)\text{,} \end{align*}
statement (2) follows from Theorem 1.13.20 (applied to \(\lambda I_V-T\) and \([\lambda I_V-T]_B\)) and the fact that
\begin{equation*} [\lambda I_V-T]_B=\lambda[I_V]_B-[T]_B=\lambda I-A\text{.} \end{equation*}
