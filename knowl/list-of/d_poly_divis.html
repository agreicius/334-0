Definition 1.24.1. Polynomial divisibility.

Let \(F\) be a field. Recall that \(P(F)\) is the set of all polynomials with coefficients in \(F\text{.}\)
  • Divisibility.
    Given polynomials \(f,g\in P(F)\text{,}\) we say the \(g\) divides \(f\) if there is a polynomial \(q\in P(F)\) satisfying
    \begin{equation} f(x)=q(x)g(x)\text{.}\tag{1.118} \end{equation}
    In this case we call \(f\) a multiple of \(g\text{,}\) \(g\) a factor of \(f\text{,}\) and the equation (1.118) a factorization of \(f\text{.}\)
  • Irreducible polynomial.
    A nonconstant polynomial \(f\in P(F)\) is irreducible if it has no nontrivial factorization: more precisely, if \(f(x)=g_1(x)g_2(x)\) for \(g_1,g_2\in F\text{,}\) then \(g_1(x)\) is a constant polynomial or \(g_2(x)\) is a constant polynomial.
  • Division algorithm.
    Given polynomials \(f,g\in P(F)\) with \(g\ne \boldzero\text{,}\) there is a unique pair of polynomials \((q(x),r(x))\) satisfying the following conditions:
    \begin{align} f(x) \amp =q(x)g(x)+r(x)\tag{1.119}\\ \deg r(x) \amp < \deg f(x)\text{.}\tag{1.120} \end{align}
    We call \(q\) and \(r\) the quotient and remainder, respectively, upon dividing \(f\) by \(g\text{.}\)
