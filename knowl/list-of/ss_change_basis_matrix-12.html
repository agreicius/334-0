Example 1.14.8. Taylor’s formula for polynomials.

Let \(V=P_2(\R)\text{,}\) \(B=(1,x,x^2)\text{,}\) and \(B'=(1,(x-2),(x-2)^2)\text{.}\)
  1. Prove that \(B'\) is a basis.
  2. Compute \(\underset{B\rightarrow B'}{P}\text{.}\)
  3. Compute \([x^2+x+1]_{B'}\) using (1.38).
Solution.
  1. As we have seen in homework, any tuple of polynomials with distinct degrees is linearly independent. Thus \(B'\) is linearly independent. Since \(\len B'=3=\dim P_2(\R)\text{,}\) it is a basis. Street smarts!
  2. We have
    \begin{align*} \underset{B\rightarrow B'}{P} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \vert \amp \vert \amp \vert \\ [1]_{B'}\amp [x]_{B'}\amp [x^2]_{B'} \\ \vert \amp \vert \amp \vert \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 0\amp 1 \\ 0\amp 1\amp 4\\ 1\amp 2\amp 4 \end{bmatrix} \text{.} \end{align*}
    The first two coordinate vector computations are nontrivial; you can verify for yourself that \(x^2=4+4(x-2)+(x-2)^2\) and \(x=2+(x-2)+0(x-2)^2\text{.}\) Alternatively, see Remark 1.14.9) for a neat trick for computing these coordinate vectors.
  3. Since \(B\) is the standard basis, we see easily that \([x^2+x+1]_{B}=(1,1,1)\text{.}\) Using (1.38) we have
    \begin{align*} [x^2+x+1]_{B'} \amp = \underset{B\rightarrow B'}{P}\begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp =\begin{bmatrix} 7\\ 5\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \text{.} \end{align*}
    Verify for yourself that we do indeed have
    \begin{equation*} x^2+x+1=7+5(x-2)+(x-2)^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
