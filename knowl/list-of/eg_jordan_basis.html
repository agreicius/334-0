Example 1.26.13. Jordan basis.

Let \(T=T_A\colon \R^6\rightarrow \R^6\text{,}\) where
\begin{equation*} A=\left[\begin{array}{rrrrrr} 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 2 \\ -3 \amp 3 \amp -4 \amp -8 \amp 10 \amp -14 \\ -2 \amp 1 \amp -1 \amp -7 \amp 8 \amp -12 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp -2 \\ -2 \amp 0 \amp -2 \amp -5 \amp 9 \amp -10 \\ -1 \amp 0 \amp -1 \amp -2 \amp 3 \amp -2 \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}
Proceeding as in the examples above, we find that the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) is \(f(x)=(x-2)^6\text{,}\) and that a normal form of \(T\) is
\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr|rr|r} 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ \hline 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ \hline 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 2 \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}
A Jordan basis of \(T\) will thus consist of three cycles
\begin{align*} B_1 \amp =(N^2(v_1),N(v_1),v_1)\\ B_2 \amp =(N(v_2),v_2)\\ B_3 \amp =(v_3)\text{.} \end{align*}
It is illuminating to arrange this basis as in our dot diagrams:
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{ccc} N^2(v_1) \amp N(v_2) \amp v_3\\ N(v_1) \amp v_2 \amp \\ v_1 \amp \amp \end{array} \end{equation*}
Following the argument in Theorem 1.26.5, we will first compute a Jordan basis for \(\im N^2\text{,}\) then modify this appropriately to get a Jordan basis of \(\im N\text{,}\) then repeat the previous step to get a Jordan basis of \(\im T^0=\R^6\text{.}\)
From the diagram above, we see that \(\dim \im N^2=1\text{,}\) and that \(\im N^2\subseteq \NS N=E(2, T)\text{.}\) Thus any basis of \(\im N^2=\CS (A-2I)^2\) will consist of a single eigenvector, and thus will automatically be a Jordan basis. As we see below, we can choose \(w_1=(1,-1,-1,0,0)\) for the sole element of this basis.
Following the induction argument in the proof of Theorem 1.26.5, to create a Jordan basis of \(\im N^2\text{,}\) we (a) lengthen the cycle \((w_1)\) to a cycle \((T(u_1), u_1)\text{,}\) where \(u_1\in \im N\) and \(T(u_1)=w_1\text{,}\) (b) extend \((w_1)\) to a basis \((w_1,w_2)\) of \(\NS T\cap \im N\text{,}\) producing the two cycles of generalized eigenvectors \((T(u_1),u_1), (w_2)\text{,}\) which form a basis of \(\im N\text{.}\) From the computation of \(N^2\) above, we see that \(N^2\bolde_1=w_1\text{.}\) Thus we can pick
\begin{equation*} u_1=N\bolde_1=(0,-3,-2,0,-2,-1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
To extend \((w_1)\) to a basis of \(\NS N\cap \im N\text{,}\) we first use Sage to compute a basis of this space.
Since \(\dim \NS N\cap \im N=2\text{,}\) we see that we can add either of the basis elements above to \(w_1\) to get a basis. We choose \(w_2=(1,5,4,1,5,2)\text{.}\) A Jordan basis of \(\im N\) is then obtained by concatenating the two cyles of generalized eigenvectors below
\begin{align*} (N^2\bolde_1,N\bolde_1) \amp = ((1,-1,-1,0,0,0),(0,-3,-2,0,-2,-1)) \\ (w_2) \amp = (1,5,4,1,5,2)\text{.} \end{align*}
We now repeat this process to transform the Jordan basis of \(\im N\) into a Jordan basis of \(\R^6\text{.}\) First we lengthen the two cycles above. Clearly the first cycle can be lengthened by appending \(\bolde_1\text{.}\) For the second we find \(v_2\) satisfying \(Nv_2=w_2\text{.}\) As we show below, we can choose \(v_2=(0,-3,0,-1,0,0)\text{.}\)
This yields the two cycles
\begin{align*} (N^2\bolde_1, N\bolde_1, \bolde_1) \amp = ((1,-1,-1,0,0,0),(2,-3,-2,0,-2,1),(1,0,0,0,0,0)) \\ (Nv_2,v_2) \amp = ((1,5,4,1,5,2),(0,-3,0,-1,0,0))\text{.} \end{align*}
Lastly, we extend \((N^2\bolde_1, N\boldv_2)\) to a basis \((N^2\bolde_1, N\boldv_2,v_3)\) of \(\NS N\text{.}\) This can be done systematically by putting \(N^2\bolde_1, N\boldv_2\) in as the first two columns of a matrix, followed by a complete basis for \(\NS N\text{,}\) and then using the column space algorithm to contract to a basis. The computation below creates this augmented matrix; we see by inspection that we can pick \(v_3=(1,1,0,1,1,0)\text{.}\)
Putting it all together, we obtain the following cycles, whose concatenation is a Jordan basis for \(T\text{:}\)
\begin{align*} (N^2(v_1),N(v_1),v_1)\amp= ((1,-1,-1,0,0,0),(0,-3,-2,0,-2,-1),(1,0,0,0,0,0)) \\ (N(v_2),v_2) \amp= ((1,5,4,1,5,2), (0,-3,0,-1,0,0)) \\ (v_3) \amp=(1,1,0,1,1,0) \end{align*}
Time to put our theory to the test. We should obtain the Jordan form of \(T\) by the change of basis formula
\begin{equation*} P^{-1}AP\text{,} \end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*} P=\left[\begin{array}{rrrrrr} 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp -3 \amp 0 \amp 5 \amp -3 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp -2 \amp 0 \amp 4 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp -1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp -2 \amp 0 \amp 5 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp -1 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}
We did it!
