Theorem 1.22.8. Polar identities.

Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space. For all \(v,w\in V\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation} \angvec{v,w}=\frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+w}^2-\norm{v-w}^2)+\frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+iw}^2-\norm{v-iw}^2)\, i\text{,}\tag{1.104} \end{equation}
or equivalently,
\begin{align} \Re \angvec{v,w} \amp = \frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+w}^2-\norm{v-w}^2)\tag{1.105}\\ \Im \angvec{v,w} \amp = \frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+iw}^2-\norm{v-iw}^2) \text{.}\tag{1.106} \end{align}
As a consequence, if \(F=\R\text{,}\) then we have
\begin{equation} \angvec{v,w}=\frac{1}{4}(\norm{v+w}^2-\norm{v-w}^2)\text{.}\tag{1.107} \end{equation}

Proof.

This is a simple, if somewhat tedious exercise of expanding out the right side of (1.104) using the definition \(\norm{u}^2=\angvec{u,u}\text{,}\) and then simplifying using properties of the inner product, and the fact that
\begin{align*} \Re z \amp =\frac{1}{2}(z+\overline{z})\\ \Im z \amp =\frac{1}{2i}(z-\overline{z}) \end{align*}
for all \(z\in \C\text{.}\)
