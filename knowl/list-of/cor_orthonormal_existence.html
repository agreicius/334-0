Corollary 1.19.6. Existence of orthonormal bases.

Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space of dimension \(n\text{.}\)
  1. There is an orthonormal basis for \(V\text{.}\) In fact, any basis of \(V\) can be converted to an orthonormal basis using the Gram-Schmidt procedure.
  2. Any orthogonal (resp. orthonormal) tuple \(S=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_k)\) in \(V\) can be extended to an orthogonal (resp. orthonormal) basis \(B=(v_1,\dots, v_k,\dots, v_n)\text{.}\)

Proof.

  1. See Procedure 1.19.5 and its proof.
  2. The orthogonal set \(S\) is linearly independent by Theorem 1.19.2. Let \(S=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_r)\) be the distinct elements of \(S\text{.}\) (We must have \(r\leq n\) since \(S\) is linearly independent.) By Theorem 1.8.8 we can extend \(S\) to a basis \(B=(v_1,\dots, v_r, v_{r+1}, \dots, v_n)\text{.}\) It is easy to see that when we apply the Gram-Schmidt procedure to \(B\text{,}\) the first \(r\) vectors are left unchanged, as they are already pairwise orthogonal. Thus Gram-Schmidt returns an orthogonal basis of the form \(B'=\{v_1,\dots, v_r, w_{r+1}, \dots, w_n\}\text{,}\) as desired.
