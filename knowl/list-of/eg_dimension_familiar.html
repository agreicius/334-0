Example 1.8.4. Dimensions of familiar spaces.

For each \(V\) below we provide its standard basis \(B\) and compute \(\dim V=\len B\text{.}\)
  • Zero space.
    The empty tuple \(B=()\) is a basis of \(V=\{\boldzero\}\text{.}\) Thus \(\dim V=\len B=0\text{.}\)
  • Tuples.
    The tuple \(B=(\bolde_1, \bolde_2,\dots, \bolde_n)\) is a basis of \(F^n\text{.}\) Thus \(\dim V=\len B=n\text{.}\)
  • Matrices.
    The tuple \(B=(E_{ij})_{(i,j)\in I\times J}\text{,}\) \(I=\{1,2,\dots, m\}\text{,}\) \(J=\{1,2,\dots, n\}\text{,}\) is a basis of \(F^{m,n}\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} \dim V=\len B=\abs{I\times J}=m\, n\text{.} \end{equation*}
  • Polynomials of bounded degree.
    Let \(I\) be an infinite subset of \(F\text{.}\) The tuple \(B=(1,x,\dots, x^n)\) is a basis of \(P_n(I)\text{.}\) Thus \(\dim P_n(I)=\len B=n+1\text{.}\)
  • Polynomials.
    Let \(I\) be an infinite subset of \(F\text{.}\) The tuple \(B=(x^n)_{n=0}^\infty=(1,x,x^2,\dots)\) is a basis of \(P(I)\text{.}\) Since \(B\) is a linearly independent tuple, we see that \(\dim P(I)=\infty\text{.}\)
