Example 1.7.3. Some standard bases.

It is easy to show that for each \(V\) below, the given \(B\) is a basis, which we will call the standard basis of \(V\text{.}\) See ExampleÂ 1.6.8 for an explanation of the notation \(\bolde_i\text{,}\) \(E(i,j)\) below.
  1. \(V=\{\boldzero\}\text{,}\) \(B=()\)
  2. \(V=F^n\text{,}\) \(B=(\bolde_1,\bolde_2,\dots, \bolde_n)\)
  3. \(V=F^{m,n}\text{,}\) \(B=(E(i,j))_{(i,j)\in I\times J}\text{,}\) \(I=\{1,2,\dots, m\}, J=\{1,2,\dots, n\}\) (Assume that \(F\in \{\R,\C\}\) and \(I\subseteq F\) is infinite.)
  4. \(V=P_n(I)\text{,}\) \(B=(1,x,x^2,\dots, x^{n})\) (Assume that \(F\in \{\R,\C\}\) and \(I\subseteq F\) is infinite.)
  5. \(V=P(I)\text{,}\) \(B=(x^i)_{i=0}^\infty=(1,x,x^2,\dots)\)
