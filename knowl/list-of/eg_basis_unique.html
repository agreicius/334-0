Example 1.7.6. Unique basis representation.

By way of illustration, we use TheoremÂ 1.7.5 to show that the tuple \(B=(E(i,j))_{(i,j)\in I\times J}\text{,}\) \(I=\{1,2,\dots, m\}\text{,}\) \(J=\{1,2,\dots, n\}\) is a basis of \(F^{m.n}\text{.}\) This follows from the simple observation that an arbitrary matrix \(A=[a_{ij}]\in F^{m,n}\) can be written as a linear combination of the \(E(i,j)\) in a unique way. Indeed, since for each \((i,j)\in I\times J\text{,}\) the matrix \(E(i,j)\) is the only matrix in this collection that has a nonzero \(ij\)-th entry, and since furthermore this entry is equal to \(1\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} A=\sum_{(i,j)\in I\times J}a_{ij}E(i,j) \end{equation*}
is the unique expression of \(A\) as a linear combination of the \(E(i,j)\text{.}\) This proves that every element of \(F^{m,n}\) can be written as a linear combination of the \(E(i,j)\) in a unique way, and hence that \(B\) is a basis.
