Theorem 1.8.8. Contracting and expanding.

Let \(V\) be a vector space of dimension \(n\text{,}\) and let \(S\) be a tuple of vectors of \(V\text{.}\)
  1. Contract to basis.
    If \(S\) spans \(V\text{,}\) then there is a subtuple of \(S\) that is a basis of \(V\text{:}\) i.e., we can contract a spanning set to a basis.
  2. Extend to basis.
    If \(S\) is linearly independent, then \(S\) is a subtuple of a basis of \(V\text{:}\) i.e., we can extend a linearly independent tuple to a basis.

Proof.

  1. Let \(S=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) and assume \(\Span S=V\text{.}\) We will assume that \(S\) is finite: i.e., \(\abs{I}=n< \infty\text{.}\) (We leave as a homework exercise the case where \(S\) is infinite.) Since \(I\) is finite, so too is the set \(\mathcal{P}(I)\) of all subsets \(J\) of \(I\text{:}\) in fact, we have \(\mathcal{P}(I)=2^n\text{.}\) It follows that there are \(2^n\) different subtuples \((v_j)_{j\in J}\) of \(S\text{.}\) Let \(\mathcal{S}=\{S_{1},S_{2},\dots, S_{r}\}\) be the set of all subtuples of \(S\) satisfying \(\Span S_k=V\text{,}\) and let \(S'\) be any element of \(\mathcal{S}\) of minimal length: i.e., we have \(\Span S'=V\text{,}\) and \(\Span S''\ne V\) for any subtuple \(S''\) of \(S\) with \(\len S'' < \len S'\text{.}\)
    We claim that \(S'\) is linearly independent, and hence a basis of \(V\text{,}\) as desired. We give a proof by contradiction. If \(S'=(v_{j})_{j\in J}\) were linearly dependent, then by Theorem 1.6.13, there is some index \(j_0\in J\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \Span (v_j)_{j\in J-\{j_0\}}=\Span(v_j)_{j\in J}=V\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But then the subtuple \(S''=(v_j)_{j\in J-\{j_0\}}\) would satisfy \(\Span S''=V\) and
    \begin{equation*} \len S''=\abs{J}-1=\len S'-1< \len S'\text{,} \end{equation*}
    contradicting the minimal length of \(S'\text{.}\) We conclude that \(S'\) must be linearly independent.
  2. Assume \(S\) is linearly independent. Since \(\dim V=n\text{,}\) we must have \(\len S\leq n\text{,}\) and hence we can write \(S=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_m)\) for some \(m\leq n\text{.}\) The procedure below constructs a sequence of tuples
    \begin{equation*} S=S_0, S_1\dots \subseteq S_k=B \end{equation*}
    such that \(S_i\) is a subtuple of \(S_{i+1}\) for all \(0\leq i\leq k\) and \(B=S_k\) is a basis of \(V\text{.}\)
    • Initialization.
      Let \(S=S_0=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_m)\)
    • Expansion loop.
      Suppose \(S_i=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_{m+i})\text{.}\) If \(\Span S_i=V\text{,}\) return \(B=S_i\text{.}\) Otherwise, define
      \begin{equation*} S_{i+1}=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_{m+i}, v_{m+i+1})\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where \(v_{m+i+1}\) is any element of \(V\) that is not contained in \(\Span S_i\text{.}\)
    Some observations:
    1. For all \(0\leq i\leq k\) the tuple \(S_i\) is linearly independent. This can be shown by induction, the crucial point being that if \(S_i\) is linearly independent, then so is \(S_{i+1}\text{,}\) precisely since \(v_{m+i+1}\notin \Span(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_{m+i})\text{.}\) (This follows from Corollary 1.6.14.)
    2. The procedure must halt. Why? Since \(\dim V=n\text{,}\) and since each \(S_i\) is linearly independent, we must have \(\len S_i\leq n\) for all \(i\) by Theorem 1.8.7. Since \(\len S_i=m+i\text{,}\) the procedure must halt in at most \(i=n-m\) steps.
    From (ii) we may assume the procedure halts at \(S_k\) for some \(k\geq 0\text{.}\) From (i) we know that \(S_k\) is linearly independent. Furthermore, since the procedure halts at \(S_k\text{,}\) we know that \(\Span S_k=V\text{.}\) It follows that \(B=S_k\) is a basis containing \(S\text{,}\) as desired.
