    Section 1.9 Linear transformations

    As detailed in Definition 1.9.1 a linear transformation is a special type of function between two vector spaces: one that respects in some sense the vector operations of both spaces.
    This manner of theorizing is typical in mathematics: first we introduce a special class of objects defined axiomatically, then we introduce special functions or maps between these objects. Since the original objects of study (e.g. vector spaces) come equipped with special structural properties (e.g. vector operations), the functions we wish to study are the ones that somehow acknowledge this structure.
    You have already seen this principle at work in your study of calculus. First we give \(\R\) some structure by defining a notion of proximity (i.e., \(x\) is close to \(y\) if \(\val{x-y}\) is small), then we introduce a special family of functions that somehow respects this structure: these are precisely the continuous functions!
    As you will see, linear transformations are not just interesting objects of study in their own right, they also serve as invaluable tools in our continued exploration of the intrinsic properties of vector spaces.
    In the meantime rejoice in the fact that we can now give a succinct definition of linear algebra: it is the theory of vector spaces and the linear transformations between them. Go shout it from the rooftops!

    Subsection Definition and examples

    Definition 1.9.1. Linear transformation.

    Let \(V\) and \(W\) be \(F\)-vector spaces. A linear transformation from \(V\) to \(W\) is a function
    \begin{equation*} T\colon V\rightarrow W \end{equation*}
    satisfying the following properties.
    1. \(T(v_1+v_2)=T(v)+T(v')\) for all \(v,v'\in V\text{.}\)
    2. \(T(cv)=cT(v)\) for all \(c\in F\) and \(v\in V\text{.}\)
    We let \(\mathcal{L}(V,W)\) denote the set of all linear transformations from \(V\) to \(W\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{L}(V,W)=\{f\colon V\rightarrow W\colon f \text{ is a linear transformation}\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Remark 1.9.2. Linear transformations.

    So what exactly do we mean when we say that a linear transformation “respects” the vector space structures? In plain English, the two axioms defining a linear transformation read as follows: the image of a sum is the sum of the images, and the image of a scalar multiple is the scalar multiple of the image. Alternatively, we could say that the application of a linear transformation to input vectors distributes over vector addition and scalar multiplication.
    Before getting to examples of linear transformations, it will perhaps be enlightening to consider how a function \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) between two vector spaces could fail to be a linear transformation. Figure 1.9.3 is an attempt at visualizing what it means for a function could fail one of the two linear transformation axioms. We will often fall back on these types of conceptual visualizations as a means of organizing our thinking about linear transformations.

    Example 1.9.4. A nonlinear example.

    Let \(T\colon \R^2\rightarrow \R^2\) be defined as \(T(x,y)=(x^2-y^2,2xy)\text{.}\)
    1. Does \(T\) satisfy Axiom i? If so, prove it. Otherwise, give an explicit counterexample.
    2. Does \(T\) satisfy Axiom ii? If so, prove it. Otherwise, give an explicit counterexample.
    Solution.
    1. \(T\) does not satisfy Axiom i. Let \(\boldx_1=(1,0)\) and \(\boldx_2=(0,1)\text{.}\) We have
      \begin{align*} T(\boldx_1+\boldx_2) \amp=T(1,1)\\ \amp = (0,2)\\ T(\boldx_1)+T(\boldx_2) \amp =T(1,0)+T(0,1)\\ \amp =(1,0)+(-1,0)=0\text{.} \end{align*}
      We thus see that \(T(\boldx_1+\boldx_2)\ne T(\boldx_1)+T(\boldx_2)\text{.}\)
    2. \(T\) does not satisfy Axiom ii. Let \(\boldx=(1,0)\) and \(c=2\text{.}\) We have
      \begin{align*} T(2\boldx) \amp=T(2,0)=(4,0)\\ 2T(\boldx)\amp = 2(1,0)=(2,0)\text{.} \end{align*}
      We thus see that \(T(2\boldx)\ne 2T(\boldx)\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.9.5. Notational quirk.

    Example 1.9.4 brings to light a notational quirk when dealing with functions of the form \(T\colon F^n\rightarrow W\text{.}\) Technically speaking, given an input \(\boldx=(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\in F^n\) we should write
    \begin{equation*} T(\boldx)=T((x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    And yet our inner aesthete cries out at the unnecessary nested parentheses, and pleads that the notational laws be relaxed in this specific setting. We shall make it so.

    Fiat 1.9.6. Parentheses shall be dropped.

    In the special case where the domain of function \(T\) is a subset of \(F^n\text{,}\) then given input \(\boldx=(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) we may write \(T(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) for \(T((x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n))\text{.}\)
    We now turn to functions that do satisfy the linear transformation axioms. As our first examples of linear transformations, we define zero transformations and identity transformations.

    Definition 1.9.7. Zero, identity, and scaling transformations.

    Let \(V\) and \(W\) be vector spaces.
    • Zero transformation.
      The zero transformation from \(V\) to \(W\), denoted \(0_{V,W}\text{,}\) is defined as follows:
      \begin{align*} 0_{V,W}\colon V \amp\rightarrow W \\ v \amp\mapsto 0_{V,W}(v)=\boldzero\text{.} \end{align*}
      In other words, \(0_{V,W}\) is the function that maps all elements of \(V\) to the zero vector of \(W\text{.}\)
    • Identity transformation.
      The identity transformation of \(V\), denoted \(I_V\text{,}\) is defined as follows:
      \begin{align*} I_V\colon V \amp\rightarrow V \\ v \amp\mapsto I_V(v)=v\text{.} \end{align*}
      In other words, \(I_V(v)=v\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) When the underlying vector space is clear from the context, we will drop the subscript and write \(I\) for \(I\text{.}\)
    • Dilation (or scaling transformation).
      Let \(c\in F\) be a fixed scalar. The function
      \begin{align*} c_V\colon V \amp \rightarrow V\\ v \amp \mapsto c_V(v)=c v \end{align*}
      is called a dilation (or scaling transformation) with scaling factor \(c\text{.}\)
      Observe that since \(1_c(v)=v\) for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) we have \(I_V=1_V \text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.9.8. Zero, identity, and scaling transformations.

    Let \(V\) and \(W\) be vector spaces.
    1. The zero transformation \(0_{V,W}\colon V\rightarrow W\) is a linear transformation.
    2. For all scalars \(c\in F\) the dilation
      \begin{align*} c_V\colon V \amp \rightarrow W\\ v \amp \mapsto cv \end{align*}
      is a linear transformation. In particular, \(I_V=1_V\) is a linear transformation.

    Proof.

    1. Let \(0_{V,W}\colon V\rightarrow W\) be the zero function. We verify each defining property separately.
      1. Given \(v, w\in V\text{,}\) we have
        \begin{align*} 0_{V,W}(v_1+v_2)\amp =\boldzero_W \amp (\text{by def.}) \\ \amp =\boldzero_W+\boldzero_W \\ \amp = 0_{V,W}(v_1)+0_{V,W}(v_2) \amp (\text{by def.})\text{.} \end{align*}
      2. Given \(c\in R\) and \(v\in V\text{,}\) we have
        \begin{align*} 0_{V,W}(cv) \amp = \boldzero_W \amp (\text{def. of } 0_{V,W})\\ \amp = c\boldzero_W \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/th_vs_props.html}{\text{1.3.6}} ) \\ \amp = c0_{V,W}(v) \amp (\text{def. of } 0_{V,W})\text{.} \end{align*}
      This proves that \(0_{V,W}\colon V\rightarrow W\) is a linear transformation.
    2. Fix a scalar \(c\in R\) and let \(T\colon V\rightarrow V\) be the scaling transformation defined as \(T(v)=cv\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\)
      1. Given \(v, w\in V\text{,}\) we have
        \begin{align*} T(v_1+v_2)\amp =c(v_1+v_2) \amp (\text{def. of } T)\\ \amp = cv_1+cv_2 \amp (\text{vec. props.})\\ \amp = cT(v_1)+cT(v_2) \amp (\text{def. of } T)\text{.} \end{align*}
      2. Given \(d\in R\) and \(v\in V\text{,}\) we have
        \begin{align*} T(dv) \amp = c(dv) \amp (\text{def. of } T)\\ \amp = (cd)v \amp (\text{vec. props.}) \\ \amp = (dc)v \amp (\text{real mult. is comm.})\\ \amp = d(cv) \amp (\text{vec. props.})\\ \amp = dT(v) \amp (\text{def. of } T)\text{.} \end{align*}
      This proves that \(T\colon V\rightarrow V\) is a linear transformation.

    Theorem 1.9.9. Basic properties of linear transformations.

    Let \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) be a linear transformation.
    1. We have \(T(\boldzero)=\boldzero\text{.}\) In other words, \(T\) maps the zero vector of \(V\) to the zero vector of \(W\text{.}\)
    2. For all \(v\in V\text{,}\) we have \(T(-v)=-T(v)\text{.}\)
    3. Let \(S=(v_i)_{i\in i}\) be a tuple of vectors of \(V\text{.}\) Given any linear combination
      \begin{equation*} v=\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i \end{equation*}
      of the \(v_i\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation*} T(v)=\sum_{i\in I}c_iT(v_i)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    1. We employ some similar trickery to what was done in the proof of Theorem 1.3.6. Assuming \(T\) is linear:
      \begin{align*} T(\boldzero) \amp= T(\boldzero+\boldzero)\\ \amp =T(\boldzero)+T(\boldzero) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/d_transformation.html}{\text{Definition 1.9.1}} ) \text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus, whatever \(T(\boldzero)\in W\) may be, it satisfies
      \begin{equation*} T(\boldzero)=T(\boldzero)+T(\boldzero)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Canceling \(T(\boldzero)\) on both sides using \(-T(\boldzero)\text{,}\) we conclude
      \begin{equation*} \boldzero=T(\boldzero_V)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. The argument is similar:
      \begin{align*} \boldzero_W \amp= T(\boldzero_V) \amp (\text{by (1)})\\ \amp =T(-v+v)\\ \amp = T(-v)+T(v)\text{.} \end{align*}
      Since \(\boldzero_W=T(-v)+T(v)\text{,}\) adding \(-T(v)\) to both sides of the equation yields
      \begin{equation*} -T(v)=T(-v)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    3. Recall that by definition Definition 1.6.1 that there is a subset \(J=\{j_1,j_2,\dots, j_n\}\subseteq I\) such that \(c_i=0\) for all \(i\notin J\text{,}\) in which case we have we can write
      \begin{equation*} v=\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i=c_{j_1}v_{j_1}+c_{j_2}v_{j_2}+\cdots +c_{j_n}v_{j_n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      An easy induction argument on \(n\text{,}\) using the two defining properties of a linear transformation, shows that
      \begin{equation*} T(v)=c_{j_1}T(v_{j_1})+c_{j_2}T(v_{j_2})+\cdots +c_{j_n}T(v_{j_n})\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since \(c_i=0\) for all \(i\notin J\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} T(v) \amp = c_{j_1}T(v_{j_1})+c_{j_2}T(v_{j_2})+\cdots +c_{j_n}T(v_{j_n})\\ \amp =\sum_{i\in I}c_iT(v_i)\text{,} \end{align*}
      as desired.

    Remark 1.9.10. Transformations distribute over combinations.

    Statement 3 of Theorem 1.9.9 combines and extends our distributive interpretations of Remark 1.9.2. It says that the application of a linear transformation distributes over arbitrary linear combinations of vectors.

    Subsection Interesting examples

    As a sort of converse to statement 3 of Theorem 1.9.9, observe that if \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) satisfies
    \begin{equation*} T(cv_1+dv_2)=cT(v_1)+dT(v_2) \end{equation*}
    for all \(c, d\in R\) and \(v_1,v_2\in V\text{,}\) then \(T\) is linear. Indeed, taking the special case \(c=d=1\) yields Axiom i of Definition 1.9.1; and choosing \(d=0\) yields Axiom ii of Definition 1.9.1. As a consequence, we have the following one-step procedure for proving whether a function \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) between vector spaces is a linear transformation.

    Procedure 1.9.11. One-step technique for transformations.

    Let \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) be a function between vector spaces. To prove \(T\) is a linear transformation, show that
    \begin{equation*} T(cv_1+dv_2)=cT(v_1)+dT(v_2) \end{equation*}
    for all scalars \(c, d\in R\) and all vectors \(v_1,v_2\in V\text{.}\)
    Next we introduce an extremely important family of linear transformations: any matrix \(A\in F^{m,n}\) has associated to it a linear transformation \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\) called a matrix transformation.

    Definition 1.9.12. Matrix transformations.

    Let \(A\in F^{m,n}\text{.}\) The matrix transformation associated to \(A\) is the function \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\) defined as
    \begin{equation*} T_A(\boldx)=A\boldx \end{equation*}
    for all \(\boldx\in F^n\text{.}\) We call \(A\) the standard matrix of \(T_A\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.9.13. Matrix transformation.

    Observe how Fiat 1.4.8 comes into play in the definition of \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\text{:}\) given a tuple \(\boldx=(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\in F^n\text{,}\) in order to make sense of the expression \(A\boldx\text{,}\) we must think of \(\boldx\) as the column vector
    \begin{equation*} \colvec{x_1\\x_2\\ \vdots \\ x_n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.9.14. Matrix transformation.

    Let \(A\in F^{m,n}\text{.}\) Prove that \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\) is a linear transformation.
    Solution.
    We use the 1-step technique. As you will see the proof follows directly from elementary properties of matrix multiplication. Given any \(\boldx,\boldy\in F^n\) and \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} T_A(c\boldx+d\boldy) \amp =A(c\boldx+d\boldy) \amp (\text{def. of } T_A)\\ \amp = cA\boldx+dA\boldy \amp (\text{matrix mult. props.})\\ \amp = cT_A(\boldx)+dT_A(\boldy) \amp (\text{def. of } T_A)\text{.} \end{align*}

    Example 1.9.15. Differentiation.

    Let \(I\) be an interval of \(\R\text{.}\) Prove that
    \begin{align*} T\colon C^1(I) \amp \rightarrow C(I)\\ f \amp \longmapsto f' \end{align*}
    is a linear transformation.
    Solution.

    Example 1.9.16. Integration.

    Let \([a,b]\) be a closed interval of \(\R\text{.}\) Prove that the function

    Subsection Transformations and bases

    Theorem 1.9.19. Transformations and bases.

    Let \(V\) and \(W\) be vector spaces, and let \(B=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) be a basis of \(V\text{.}\)
    1. Give linear transformations \(T_1,T_2\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) we have \(T_1=T_2\) if and only if \(T_1(v_i)=T_2(v_i)\) for all \(i\in I\text{.}\)
    2. Given any tuple \((w_i)_{i\in I}\) of vectors of \(W\text{,}\) there is a linear transformation \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) satisfying
      \begin{equation} T(v_i)=w_i\tag{1.24} \end{equation}
      for all \(i\in I\text{.}\) Furthermore, as a consequence of (1), this linear transformation \(T\) is unique.

    Proof.

    We assume \(B=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) is a basis of \(V\text{.}\)
    1. Assume \(T_1,T_2\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) and that \(T_1(v_i)=T_2(v_i)\) for all \(i\in I\text{.}\) Since \(B\) is a basis, given any \(v\in V\) we can write \(v\) as a linear combination
      \begin{equation*} v=\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i \end{equation*}
      for some \(c_i\in F\text{,}\) in which case we have
      \begin{align*} T_1(v) \amp =\sum_{i\in I}c_iT_1(v_i) \amp (T_1 \text{ is linear})\\ \amp = \sum_{i\in I}c_iT_2(v_i) \amp (T_1(v_i)=T_2(v_i))\\ \amp = T_2(v) \amp (T_2 \text{ is linear})\text{.} \end{align*}
      We have shown that \(T_1(v)=T_2(v)\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) By definition, this means that \(T_1=T_2\text{.}\)
    2. Given a tuple \((w_i)_{i\in i}\) of vectors of \(W\text{,}\) the following recipe defines a function \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\text{:}\)
      • given \(v\in V\text{,}\) write \(v=\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i\) as a linear combination of the vectors \(v_i\text{;}\)
      • define \(T(v)=\sum_{i\in I}c_iw_i\text{.}\)
      We need to address two details of this recipe in order to convince ourselves that this determines a well-defined function \(T\text{.}\) Firstly, since \(B\) is a basis, the coefficients \(c_i\) in the linear combination \(v=\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i\) are uniquely determined by \(v\text{,}\) making the value \(T(v)=\sum_{i\in I}c_iw_i\) uniquely determined. Secondly, observe that since \(c_i=0\) for all but finitely many \(i\in I\text{,}\) the expression \(\sum_{i\in I}c_iw_i\) is a (finite) linear combination of vectors, and thus defines an element of \(W\text{.}\)
      From our definition of \(T\) it follows that \(T(v_i)=w_i\) for all \(i\in I\text{.}\) Indeed, to compute \(T(v_i)\) following our recipe, we write \(v_i=1v_i\) as a linear combination of the elements of \(B\text{,}\) whence it follows that \(T(v_i)=1w_i=w_i\text{.}\)
      Lastly, we must show that \(T\) is linear using the 1-step technique. Given \(v, w\in V\) and scalars \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) write
      \begin{align*} v \amp =\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i\\ w \amp =\sum_{i\in I}d_iv_i \end{align*}
      as linear combinations of the \(v_i\text{,}\) and then compute
      \begin{align*} T(cv+dw) \amp =T(\sum_{i\in i}(cc_i+dd_i)v_i)\\ \amp = \sum_{i\in I}(cc_i+dd_i)v_i \amp (\text{def. of } T)\\ \amp =c\sum_{i\in i}c_iv_i+d\sum_{i\in I}d_iv_i\\ \amp =cT(v)+dT(w) \amp (\text{def. of } T)\text{.} \end{align*}

    Remark 1.9.20. Transformations and bases.

    The two statements of Theorem 1.9.19 deserve some elaboration and interpretation.
    1. The first statement tells us that a linear transformation \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) is uniqueley determined by its values \(T(v_i)\) for any basis \(B=(v_i)_{i\in I}\text{.}\) In other words, if you know that \(T\) is linear, and you know where it sends each element of a basis, then you know \(T\) completeley! Put another way, a linear transformation is uniquely determined by its action on any given basis.
    2. Consisder the second statement as a powerful “transformation building” result. In more detail, it says the following: given a basis \(B=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) of \(V\text{,}\) given any choice of values \(w_i\in W\text{,}\) there is a unique linear transformation \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) that sends \(v_i\) to \(w_i\) for all \(i\in I\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.9.21. Matrix transformations.

    Let \(T\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\) be a linear transformation. There is a unique matrix \(A\in F^{m,n}\) such that \(T=T_A\text{.}\) In particular, all linear transformations from \(F^n\) to \(F^m\) are matrix transformations.

    Proof.

    The proof is left as an exercise.

    Subsection \(\mathcal{L}(V,W)\) as a vector space

    We end this section by providing a vector space structure to the set \(\mathcal{L}(V,W)\) of all linear transformations from \(V\) to \(W\text{.}\) As in Section 1.2, we state the result as a definition with proof.

    Definition 1.9.22. Space of linear transformations.

    Let \(V\) and \(W\) be vector spaces. Below we define a vector space structure on \(\mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{.}\)
    • Vector operations.
      Given linear transformations \(T,S\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) and scalar \(c\in F\text{,}\) we define linear transformations \(T+S, cT\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) as follows:
      \begin{align*} (T+S)(v) \amp= T(v)+S(v) \text{ for all } v\in V \\ (cT)(v) \amp =cT(v) \text{ for all } v\in V\text{.} \end{align*}
    • Zero vector.
      The zero vector of \(\mathcal{L}(V,W)\) is the zero transformation \(0_{V,W}\colon V\rightarrow W\) defined as \(0_{V,W}(v)=\boldzero\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\)
    • Vector inverses.
      Given a linear transformation \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) its vector inverse is the linear transformation \(-T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) defined as \((-T)(v)=-T(v)\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\)
    We call \(\mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) together with these vector operations, the space of linear transformations from \(V\) to \(W\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    The proof is left as an exercises. Note that before verifying the vector space axioms (i)-(viii) you must first verify that the given operations are well-defined: i.e., you must show that if \(T\) and \(S\) are linear transformations from \(V\) to \(W\text{,}\) then so are the functions \(T+S\) and \(cT\text{.}\)
