    Section 1.17 Diagonalizable operators

    Our treatment of eigenvectors in Section 1.15 was motivated in part by the objective of finding particularly simple matrix representations \([T]_B\) of a linear transformation \(T\colon V\rightarrow V\text{.}\) The simplest situation we could hope for is that there is a choice of basis \(B\) for which \([T]_B\) is diagonal. We say that the basis \(B\) diagonalizes the transformation \(T\) in this case, and that \(T\) is diagonalizable. In this section we develop theoretical and computational tools for determining whether a linear transformation \(T\) is diagonalizable, and for finding a diagonalizing basis \(B\) when \(T\) is in fact diagonalizable.

    Definition 1.17.1. Diagonalizable.

    Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional vector space. A linear operator \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) is diagonalizable if there exists a basis \(B\) of \(V\) such that \([T]_B\) is a diagonal matrix. In this case, we say that the basis \(B\) diagonalizes \(T\text{.}\)

    Convention 1.17.2. Diagonalizable matrix.

    We will say a matrix \(A\in F^{n,n}\) is diagonalizable if its corresponding matrix transformation \(T_A\colon F^{n}\rightarrow F^{n}\) is diagonalizable.
    Let’s examine what this means in terms of matrix arithmetic. Let \(B=(\bolde_1,\bolde_2,\dots, \bolde_n)\) be the standard basis of \(F^n\text{,}\) and recall that \(A=[T_A]_B\text{:}\) i.e., the standard matrix of \(T=T_A\) is the matrix representation of \(T_A\) with respect to the standard basis. The matrix \(A\) is diagonalizable if and only if there is a basis \(B'\) of \(F^{n}\) such that \([T_A]_{B'}=D\) is a diagonal matrix. Using Theorem 1.14.18, this in turn is equivalent to there being an invertible matrix \(P\) such that
    \begin{equation} D=P^{-1}AP\text{.}\tag{1.49} \end{equation}
    Thus, we conclude that \(A\) is diagonalizable if and only if it is similar to a diagonal matrix. We say that the invertible matrix \(P\) in (1.49) diagonalizes the matrix \(A\text{.}\)
    Given a basis \(B=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_n)\) of \(V\) and linear operator \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) looking at the recipe for the matrix representation \([T]_B\text{,}\) we see that we have \([T]_B=D\) for some diagonal matrix
    \begin{equation*} D=\begin{bmatrix} d_1 \amp 0\amp \dots \amp \amp 0\\ 0 \amp d_2\amp 0\amp \dots \amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp \ddots \amp \amp 0\\ \vdots \amp \amp \amp \amp \vdots \\ 0\amp 0\amp \dots \amp 0\amp d_n \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    if and only if
    \begin{align*} \begin{amatrix}[cccc]\vert \amp \vert \amp \amp \vert \\[20pt] \left[T(v_1)\right]_{B}\amp [T(v_2)]_{B}\amp \dots \amp [T(v_n)]_{B} \\[20pt] \vert \amp \vert \amp \amp \vert\\[20pt] \end{amatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} d_1 \amp 0\amp \dots \amp \amp 0\\ 0 \amp d_2\amp 0\amp \dots \amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp \ddots \amp \amp 0\\ \vdots \amp \amp \amp \amp \vdots \\ 0\amp 0\amp \dots \amp 0\amp d_n \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{align*}
    if and only if
    \begin{equation*} [T(v_j)]_B=(0,0,\dots, d_j,0,\dots, 0) \end{equation*}
    for all \(1\leq j\leq n\text{,}\) if and only if
    \begin{equation*} T(v_j)=d_jv_j \end{equation*}
    for all \(1\leq j\leq n\text{.}\) Since vectors of a basis are necessarily nonzero, we conclude that \(B\) diagonalizes \(T\) if and only if it consists of eigenvectors of \(T\text{.}\) We call such a basis an eigenbasis.

    Definition 1.17.3. Eigenbasis.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{.}\) A basis \(B=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) of \(V\) is an eigenbasis of \(T\) if \(v_i\) is an eigenvector of \(T\) for all \(i\in I\text{.}\)
    Our discussion above Definition 1.17.3 constitutes a proof of the first two statements in Theorem 1.17.4.

    Theorem 1.17.4. Diagonalizable operators.

    Let \(T\) be a linear operator on the finite-dimensional vector space \(V\text{,}\) and let \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\) be the distinct eigenvalues of \(T\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(T\) is diagonalizable.
    2. \(T\) has an eigenbasis \(B\text{.}\)
    3. \(V=\bigoplus_{i=1}^r E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\)
    4. \(\dim V=\sum_{i=1}^r\dim E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\)
    We defer a full proof of Theorem 1.17.4 for the time being, opting instead to illustrate with some examples how we can decide whether a linear transformation is diagonalizable. The last statement of Theorem 1.17.4 will serve as the foundation for our computational technique for answering such questions, as we make official in the following procedure.

    Procedure 1.17.5. Diagonalizable operator.

    Let \(T\) be a linear operator of the finite-dimensional vector space \(V\text{.}\) To determine whether \(T\) is diagonalizable and/or find an eigenbasis of \(T\text{,}\) proceed as follows.
    1. Determine the distinct eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\) of \(T\text{.}\)
    2. For each \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) compute a basis \(B_i\) for \(E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\)
    3. \(T\) is diagonalizable if and only if \(\sum_{i=1}^r\dim E(\lambda_i, T)=\dim V\text{.}\)
    4. If \(T\) is diagonalizable, then the tuple \(B=B_1*B_2*\cdots *B_r\) obtained by concatenating the bases \(B_i\) is an eigenbasis of \(T\text{.}\)

    Example 1.17.6. Transposition.

    Let \(S\colon F^{2,2}\rightarrow F^{2,2}\) be defined as \(S(A)=A^T\text{.}\) Decide whether \(S\) is diagonalizable; if it is, find an eigenbasis \(B\) of \(S\) and compute \([S]_B\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We have already done most of the work of Procedure 1.17.5 for this linear operator in Example 1.15.15. There we saw that \(1\) and \(-1\) are the only eigenvalues of \(S\text{,}\) and the the bases of \(E(1,S)\) and \(E(-1,S)\) are
    \begin{align*} B_1\amp =\left( \begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\right) \amp \\ B_2 \amp =\left(\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ -1\amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \right)\text{,} \end{align*}
    respectively. It follows that
    \begin{align*} \dim E(1,S) \amp =\len B_1=3\\ \dim E(-1,S) \amp =\len B_2=1\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since we have
    \begin{equation*} \dim F^{2,2}=4=3+1=\dim E(1,S)+\dim E(-1,S)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we conclude that \(S\) is diagonalizable, and furthermore that
    \begin{equation*} B=\left(\begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\\ -1\amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\right) \end{equation*}
    is an eigenbasis of \(S\text{.}\)

    Example 1.17.7. Matrix transformation.

    Let \(T\colon \R^3\rightarrow \R^3\) be the matrix transformation \(T=T_A\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 1\amp 0 \\0\amp 1\amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Decide whether \(T_A\) is diagonalizable; if it is, find an invertible matrix \(P\) that diagonalizes \(A\) and compute \(D=P^{-1}AP\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We compute the characteristic polynomial \(f\) as
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp = \det(xI-A)\\ \amp = \det \begin{amatrix}[ccc] x-1\amp -1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp x-1\amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\amp x-2 \end{amatrix} \\ \amp = (x-1)^2(x-2)\text{.} \end{align*}
    (Note how easy \(f\) was to compute, thanks to \(A\) being upper triangular.) The distinct eigenvalues of \(A\) are thus \(1\) and \(2\text{.}\) We compute
    \begin{align*} E(1,T_A) \amp = \NS(I-A)\\ \amp =\NS \begin{amatrix}[ccc] 0\amp -1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0\amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\amp -1 \end{amatrix}\\ \amp = \Span((1,0,0))\\ E(2,T_A) \amp = \NS(2I-A)\\ \amp =\NS \begin{amatrix}[ccc] 1\amp -1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1\amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\amp 0 \end{amatrix}\\ \amp = \Span((0,0,1))\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since
    \begin{align*} \dim E(1,T_A)+\dim E(2,T_A) \amp = 1+1=2\ne 3\text{,} \end{align*}
    we conclude that \(T_A\) is not diagaonalizable.

    Example 1.17.8. Matrix transformation.

    Let \(T\colon \R^3\rightarrow \R^3\) be the matrix transformation \(T=T_A\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 0\amp 1 \\0\amp 1\amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Decide whether \(T_A\) is diagonalizable; if it is, find an invertible matrix \(P\) that diagonalizes \(A\) and compute \(D=P^{-1}AP\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We compute the characteristic polynomial \(f\) as
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp = \det(xI-A)\\ \amp = \det \begin{amatrix}[ccc] x-1\amp 0 \amp -\\ 0 \amp x-1\amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\amp x-2 \end{amatrix} \\ \amp = (x-1)^2(x-2)\text{.} \end{align*}
    The distinct eigenvalues of \(A\) are thus \(1\) and \(2\text{.}\) We compute
    \begin{align*} E(1,T_A) \amp = \NS(I-A)\\ \amp =\NS \begin{amatrix}[ccc] 0\amp 0\amp -1\\ 0 \amp 0\amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\amp -1 \end{amatrix}\\ \amp = \Span((1,0,0), (0,1,0))\\ E(2,T_A) \amp = \NS(2I-A)\\ \amp =\NS \begin{amatrix}[ccc] 1\amp 0 \amp -1\\ 0 \amp 1\amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\amp 0 \end{amatrix}\\ \amp = \Span((1,0,1))\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since
    \begin{align*} \dim E(1,T_A)+\dim E(2,T_A) \amp = 2+1=3\text{,} \end{align*}
    we conclude that \(T_A\) is diagaonalizable. Furthermore we obtain an eigenbasis
    \begin{equation*} B'=((1,0,0),(0,1,0),(1,0,1)) \end{equation*}
    by concantenating the bases for our two eigenspaces. Let \(B\) be the standard basis of \(\R^3\text{.}\) By the change of basis formula, we have
    \begin{equation*} [T_A]_{B'}=(\underset{B'\to B}{P})^{-1}[T_A]_B\underset{B'\to B}{P}=(\underset{B'\to B}{P})^{-1}A\underset{B'\to B}{P}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(B'\) is an eigenbasis, we easily compute
    \begin{equation*} D=[T_A]_{B'}=\begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 0\amp 0\\ 0\amp 1\amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus we have
    \begin{equation*} D=P^{-1}AP, \hspace{10pt} P=\underset{B'\to B}{P}=\begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 0\amp 0\\ 0\amp 1\amp 0\\ 1\amp 0\amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The next example is a first glimpse at the algebraic usefulness of diagonalizability. If \(A\) is diagonalizable, we have
    \begin{align*} D \amp =P^{-1}AP \amp A=PDP^{-1} \end{align*}
    and hence
    \begin{align*} A^n \amp =(PDP^{-1})^n\\ \amp =PD^nP^{-1} \end{align*}
    for any positive integer \(n\text{.}\) This gives us a convenient means of computing powers of diagonalizable matrices.

    Example 1.17.9. Matrix power.

    Let \(A=\begin{bmatrix} 3\amp 1\\ 1\amp 3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Compute \(A^{100}\)
    Solution.
    The characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is \(f(x)=x^2-6x+8=(x-4)(x-2)\text{.}\) The usual computation shows that
    \begin{align*} E(2, T_A) \amp = \Span ((1,-1)) \\ E(4,T_A) \amp =\Span ((1,1))\text{,} \end{align*}
    and thus that we have \(D=P^{-1}AP\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} D=\begin{bmatrix} 2\amp 0 \\ 0\amp 4 \end{bmatrix}, P=\begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 1 \\ -1\amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    From the discussion above, we conclude that
    \begin{align*} A^{100} \amp =(PDP^{-1})^{100}\\ \amp = PD^{100}P^{-1}\\ \amp =\begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 1 \\ -1\amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2^{100}\amp 0 \\ 0\amp 4^{100} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1/2\amp -1/2 \\ 1/2\amp 1/2 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1\amp 1 \\ -1\amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2^{99}\amp -2^{99} \\ 2^{199}\amp 2^{199} \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 2^{199}+2^{99} \amp 2^{199}-2^{99} \\ 2^{199}-2^{99} \amp 2^{199}+2^{99} \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{align*}

    Theorem 1.17.10. Independence of eigenvectors.

    Let \(T\) be a linear operator on the vector space \(V\) (not necessarily finite dimensional). If \(S=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) is a tuple of eigenvectors of \(T\) satisfying \(T(v_i)=\lambda_i v_i\) for all \(i\in I\) and \(\lambda_i\ne \lambda_j\) for all \(i\ne j\) (i.e., the eigenvalues are distinct), then \(S\) is linearly independent.

    Proof.

    We prove the result by contradiction. If \(S=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) is linearly depenendent, then we can find a linearly dependent finite subtuple \(S'=(v_{i_1}, v_{i_2},\dots, v_{i_r})\) of minimal length \(r\text{.}\) Since each \(v_{i_j}\) is an eigenvector of \(T\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} T(v_{i_j})=\lambda_j v_{i_j} \end{equation*}
    for all \(1\leq j\leq r\text{;}\) since the eigenvalues are assumed to be distinct, we have \(\lambda_j\ne \lambda_\ell\) for \(j\ne \ell\text{.}\)
    Note first that we must have \(r\geq 2\text{:}\) since eigenvectors are nonzero by definition, no subtuple of \(S\) of length 1 is linearly dependent. Since \(S'\) is linearly dependent we have
    \begin{equation} c_1v_{i_1}+c_2v_{i_2}+\cdots +c_rv_{i_r}=\boldzero\text{,}\tag{1.50} \end{equation}
    where \(c_i\ne 0\) for some \(1\leq i\leq r\text{.}\) After reordering, we may assume without loss of generality that \(c_1\ne 0\text{.}\) Next we apply \(T\) to both sides of (1.50):
    \begin{align} c_1v_{i_1}+c_2v_{i_2}\cdots +c_rv_{i_r}\amp=\boldzero \tag{1.51}\\ T(c_1v_{i_1}+c_2v_{i_2}+\cdots +c_rv_{i_r})\amp=T(\boldzero)\tag{1.52}\\ c_1T(v_{i_1})+c_2T(v_{i_2})+\cdots +c_rT(v_{i_r})\amp =\boldzero \tag{1.53}\\ c_1\lambda_1v_{i_1}+c_2\lambda_2v_{i_2}+\cdots +c_r\lambda_rv_{i_r}=\boldzero\text{.}\tag{1.54} \end{align}
    From equation (1.50) and the equation in (1.54) we have
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_r(c_1v_{i_1}+c_2v_{i_2}\cdots +c_rv_{i_r})- (c_1\lambda_1v_{i_1}+c_2\lambda_2v_{i_2}\cdots +c_r\lambda_rv_{i_r})=\boldzero\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and hence
    \begin{equation} c_1(\lambda_r-\lambda_1)v_{i_1}+\cdots +c_{r-1}(\lambda_r-\lambda_{r-1})v_{i_{r-1}}+\cancel{c_r(\lambda_r-\lambda_r)}v_{i_r}=\boldzero\text{.}\tag{1.55} \end{equation}
    Since \(c_1\ne 0\) and \(\lambda_1\ne \lambda_r\text{,}\) we have \(c_1(\lambda_r-\lambda_1)\ne 0\text{.}\) Thus equation (1.55) implies that the tuple \(S''=(v_{i_1}, v_{i_2}, \dots, v_{i_{r-1}})\) is a linearly dependent subtuple of \(S\) of length \(r=1\text{,}\) contradicting the minimality of \(r\text{.}\) Since we have reached a contradiction, we conclude that \(S\) must be linearly independent.

    Example 1.17.11. Exponential functions as eigenvectors.

    Let \(I\) be an interval of \(\R\) containing more than one element. For all \(\lambda\in \R\text{,}\) define \(f_\lambda\in C^\infty(\R)\) as \(f_\lambda(x)=e^{\lambda x}\text{.}\) Prove that \(S=(f_\lambda)_{\lambda\in \R}\) is an uncountably infinite linear independent tuple in \(C^{\infty}(I)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We saw in Example 1.15.10 that the functions \(f_\lambda\) are all eigenvectors of the differential operator \(T\colon C^\infty(I)\rightarrow C^\infty(I)\text{,}\) \(T(f)=f'\text{.}\) Since \(T(f_\lambda)=\lambda f_\lambda\) for all \(\lambda\text{,}\) we see that the functions \(f_\lambda\) all have distinct eigenvalues. We conclude from Theorem 1.17.10 that \((f_\lambda)_{\lambda\in \R}\) is a linearly independent tuple. Since this tuple is indexed by \(\R\) itself, we see that it is an uncountably infinite linear independent tuple.

    Corollary 1.17.12. Independence of eigenvectors.

    Let \(T\) be a linear operator on the vector space \(V\) (not necessarily finite dimensional). If \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_r\) are distinct eigenvalues of \(T\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)=\bigoplus_{i=1}^r E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\tag{1.56} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    Suppose we have a decomposition of $\boldzero$ of the form
    \begin{equation} \boldzero=v_1+v_2+\cdots +v_r, v_i\in E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\tag{1.57} \end{equation}
    Let $J=\{j\in \{1,2,\dots, r\}\colon v_j\ne \boldzero\}$. If $J$ is nonempty, then $v_j$ is a $\lambda_j$-eigenvector, and we have
    \begin{equation*} \boldzero=\sum_{j\in J}v_j\text{,} \end{equation*}
    a nontrivial linear combination of the tuple \(S=(v_j)_{j\in J}\text{.}\) This is a contradiction, as this tuple of eigenvectors (with distinct eigenvalues) is linearly independent by Theorem 1.17.10. Thus \(J\) is empty, and we see that the decomposition (1.57) must be the trivial one. We conclude from Theorem 1.8.13 that subspace sum \(W=\sum_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)\) is direct: i.e., that
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)=\bigoplus_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as desired.
    Theorem 1.17.10 and Corollary 1.17.12 give us the necessary theoretical tools to complete the proof of Theorem 1.17.4.

    Proof of Theorem 1.17.4.

    Recall that we have already provided an argument proving that statements (1) and (2) of Theorem 1.17.4 are equivalent. We will prove the cycle of implications (2)\(\implies\)(3)\(\implies\)(4)\(\implies\)(2)

    Implication: (2)\(\implies\)(3).

    Assume \(T\) has an eigenbasis \(B\text{.}\) Since the elements of \(B\) are eigenvectors, after a re-ordering of \(B\text{,}\) we can assume \(B\) is of the form
    \begin{equation*} B=(v_{1,1},\dots, v_{1,n_1},v_{2,1},\dots, v_{2,n_2},\dots, v_{\ell,1},\dots, v_{\ell,n_{\ell}})\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where for all \(1\leq i\leq \ell\) and \(1\leq j\leq n_i\text{,}\) \(v_{i,j}\) is an eigenvector of \(T\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda_i\text{.}\) (Note that we do not assume that \(\ell=r\) here, but it follows from the rest of our argument that that this must be the case.) Since \(B\) is a basis, given any \(v\in V\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} v \amp =\underset{w_1}{\underbrace{\sum_{j=1}^{n_1}v_{1,j}}}+\underset{w_2}{\underbrace{\sum_{j=1}^{n_2}v_{2,j}}}+\cdots +\underset{w_\ell}{\underbrace{\sum_{j=1}^{n_\ell}v_{\ell,j}}}\\ \amp =w_1+w_2+\cdots +w_\ell\text{,} \end{align*}
    where \(w_i=\sum_{j=1}^{n_i}v_{i,j}\in E(\lambda_i, T)\) for all \(1\leq i\leq \ell\text{.}\) This proves \(V=\sum_{i=1}^\ell E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\) By Corollary 1.17.12, we conclude that \(V=\bigoplus_{i=1}^\ell E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\) Lastly, since clearly
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^\ell E(\lambda_i, T)\subseteq \sum_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we have
    \begin{align*} V \amp =\sum_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)\\ \amp = \bigoplus_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/cor_eigenvectors_ind.html}{\text{1.17.12}})\text{,} \end{align*}
    as desired.

    Implication: (3)\(\implies\)(4).

    This is a direct consequence of Theorem 1.8.13.

    Implication: (4)\(\implies\)(2).

    Assume \(\sum_{i=1}^r\dim E(\lambda_i, T)=\dim V=n\text{.}\) For each \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) let
    \begin{equation*} B_i=(v_{i,1}, v_{i,2},\dots, v_{i,n_{i}}) \end{equation*}
    be a basis of \(E(\lambda_i, T)\text{.}\) Since \(\len B_i=n_i\text{,}\) we see that
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^rn_i=\sum_{i=1}^{r}\dim E(\lambda_i, T)=n\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Consider the concatentation \(B=B_1*B_2*\cdots * B_r\) of the bases \(B_i\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} B=(v_{1,1},\dots, v_{1,n_1}, v_{2,1},\dots, v_{2,n_2},\dots, v_{r,1},\dots, v_{r,n_r})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since each \(B_i\) is the basis of \(E(\lambda_i, T)\text{,}\) and since
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T)=\bigoplus_{i=1}^rE(\lambda_i, T) \end{equation*}
    by Corollary 1.17.12, it follows that \(B\) is linearly independent. (This is left as an exercise.) Since \(\len B=n=\dim V\text{,}\) it follows from Corollary 1.8.9 that \(B\) is a basis of \(V\text{.}\) Since each element of \(B\) is an eigenvector of \(T\text{,}\) we conclude that \(B\) is an eigenbasis of \(T\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.17.13. Distinct eigenvalues.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(\dim V=n< \infty\text{.}\) If \(T\) has \(n\) distinct eigenvalues, then \(T\) is diagonalizable.

    Proof.

    Let \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots, \lambda_n\) be the \(n\) distict eigenvalues of \(T\text{,}\) and let \(B=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_n)\) be a tuple of eigenvectors satisfying \(T(v_i)=\lambda_i\) for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.17.10, \(B\) is linearly independent. Since \(\dim V=n=\len B\text{,}\) it follows that \(B\) is an eigenbasis of \(V\text{.}\) From Theorem 1.17.4, we conclude that \(T\) is diagonalizable.

    Remark 1.17.14. Distinct eigenvalues.

    Mark well that the implication in Corollary 1.17.13 is not an if and only if! Consider the identity transformation \(I_V\colon V\rightarrow V\) on a nonzero finite-dimensional vector space \(V\text{.}\) Given any basis \(B\) of \(V\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} [I_V]_B=I\in F^{n,n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(I_V\) is diagonalizable. And yet, \(I_V\) has only one eigenvalue, namely \(\lambda=1\text{.}\)

    Example 1.17.15. Complex linear operator.

    Assume \(T\) is a linear operator of the 3-dimensional \(\C\)-vector space \(V\) and that the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) is \(f(x)=x^3-1\text{.}\) Decide whether \(T\) is diagonalizable.
    Solution.
    Over \(\C\) the characteristic polynomial factors as
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-1)(x^2+x+1)=(x-1)(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{align*} \alpha \amp =-\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\\ \beta \amp =-\frac{1}{2}-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus \(T\) has three distinct eigenvalues \(1, \alpha, \beta\text{.}\) Since \(\dim V=3\text{,}\) it follows from Corollary 1.17.13 that \(T\) is diagonalizable.

    Example 1.17.16. Real linear operator.

    Assume \(T\) is a linear operator of the 3-dimensional \(\R\)-vector space \(V\text{,}\) that the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) is \(f(x)=x^3-1\text{,}\) and that \(T\ne I_V\text{.}\) Decide whether \(T\) is diagonalizable.
    Solution.
    Over \(\R\) the characteristic polynomial \(f\) factors as
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-1)(x^2+x+1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(1\) is the only eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\) If \(T\) were diagonalizable, we would have
    \begin{equation*} V=\bigoplus E(\lambda_i, T)=E(1,T)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But then \(v\in E(1,T)\) for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) in which case
    \begin{equation*} T(v)=1v=v \end{equation*}
    for all \(v\in V\text{:}\) i.e., we would have \(T=I_V\) in this case. We conclude that \(T\) is not diagonalizable.
