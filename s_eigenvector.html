    Section 1.15 Eigenvectors

    For the rest of this course we will focus our investigation on linear transformations of the form \(T\colon V\rightarrow V\text{:}\) that is, transformations from a space \(V\) into itself. When \(V\) is finite-dimensional we can get a computational grip on \(T\) by choosing an ordered basis \(B\) and considering the matrix representation \([T]_B\text{.}\) As was illustrated in Example 1.14.15, different matrix representations \([T]_B\) and \([T]_{B'}\) provide different insights into the nature of \(T\text{.}\) Furthermore, we see from this example that if the action of \(T\) on a chosen basis is simple to describe, then so too is the matrix representation of \(T\) with respect to that basis.
    A particularly agreeable situation arises when the basis \(B=(v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n)\) satisfies
    \begin{equation*} T(v_i)=c_iv_i, c_i\in \R \end{equation*}
    for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\) Using recipe (1.34) we in this case that the corresponding matrix representation
    \begin{equation*} [T]_B=\begin{bmatrix} c_1 \amp 0\amp \dots \amp \amp 0\\ 0 \amp c_2\amp 0\amp \dots \amp 0\\ 0\amp 0\amp \ddots \amp \amp 0\\ \vdots \amp \amp \amp \amp \vdots \\ 0\amp 0\amp \dots \amp 0\amp c_n \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    is diagonal! Diagonal matrices are about as simple as they come: they wear all of their properties (rank, nullity, invertibility, etc.) on their sleeve. If we hope to find a diagonal matrix representation of \(T\text{,}\) then we should seek nonzero vectors \(v\) satisfying \(T(v)=cv\) for some \(c\in \R\text{:}\) these are called eigenvectors of \(T\text{.}\)

    Subsection Definition and examples

    Convention 1.15.1.

    Henceforth we will focus on the study of linear transformations \(T\colon V\rightarrow V\) from a vector space to itself. Recall that in this setting we denote \(\mathcal{L}(V,V)=\mathcal{L}(V)\) and \([T]_B^B=[T]_B\) (in the case where \(V\) has a finite basis \(B\)). Furthermore, we call a linear transformation \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) a linear operator on \(V\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.15.2. Eigenvectors and eigenvalues.

    Let \(T\colon V\rightarrow V\) be a linear transformation. A nonzero vector \(v\in V\) satisfying
    \begin{equation} T(v)=\lambda v\tag{1.43} \end{equation}
    for some \(\lambda\in F\) is called an eigenvector of \(T\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{,}\) or more concisely, a \(\lambda\)-eigenvector.

    Convention 1.15.3. Eigenvectors of matrix transformations.

    Let \(A\in F^{n,n}\) be a square matrix. As a form of conflation of \(A\) with its corresponding matrix transformation \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^n\text{,}\) we will call \(\boldx\) an eigenvector of \(A\) if it is an eigenvalue of \(T_A\text{;}\) similarly, we will call \(\lambda\) an eigenvalue of \(A\) if it is an eigenvalue of \(T_A\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.15.4.

    You ask: Why use \(\lambda\) instead of \(c\) or \(k\text{?}\) My answer: tradition!

    Remark 1.15.5.

    Note well the important condition that an eigenvector must be nonzero. This means the zero vector \(\boldzero\) by definition is not an eigenvector. If we allowed \(\boldzero\) as an eigenvector, then the notion of the eigenvalue of an eigenvector would no longer be well-defined. This is because for any linear transformation we have
    \begin{equation*} T(\boldzero)=\boldzero, \end{equation*}
    which implies that
    \begin{equation*} T(\boldzero)=\lambda\boldzero \end{equation*}
    for all \(\lambda\in \R\text{.}\)

    Example 1.15.6. Dilations, zero and identity transformations.

    Assume \(V\) is nonzero. Recall that given a scalar \(c\in F\text{,}\) we define the dilation \(c_V\colon V\rightarrow V\) as \(c_V(v)=cv\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) Note that the zero transformation \(0_V\colon V\rightarrow V\) and identity transformation \(I_V\colon V\rightarrow V\) are examples of dilations, taking \(c=0\) and \(c=1\text{,}\) respectively.
    Find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a dilation \(c_V\colon V\rightarrow V\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Since \(c_V(v)=cv\) for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) we see that all nonzero vectors \(v\in V\) are eigenvectors of eigenvalue \(c\text{,}\) and thus that \(c\) is the only eigenvalue of \(c_V\text{.}\)
    In particular, consider the cases \(c=0\) and \(c=1\text{,}\) we see that \(0\) is the only eigenvalue of the zero transformation, and \(1\) is the only eigenvalue of the identity transformation.

    Example 1.15.7. Reflection.

    Let \(\ell\) be a line in \(\R^2\) passing through the origin, and define \(T\colon \R^2\rightarrow \R^2\) to be reflection through \(\ell\text{.}\) (See Example 1.14.15.) Find all eigenvectors and eigenvalues of \(T\text{.}\) Use a geometric argument. You may assume that \(T\) is linear.
    Solution.
    Since the reflection operator fixes all elements of the line \(\ell\text{,}\) we have \(T(\boldx)=\boldx\) for any \(\boldx\in \ell\text{.}\) This shows that any nonzero element of \(\ell\) is an eigenvectors of \(T\) with eigenvalue \(1\text{.}\)
    Similarly, since \(\ell^\perp\) is orthogonal to \(\ell\text{,}\) reflection through \(\ell\) takes any element \(\boldx=(x,y)\in \ell^\perp\) and maps it to \(-\boldx=(-x,-y)\text{.}\) Thus any nonzero element \(\boldx\in \ell^\perp\) is an eigenvector of \(T\) with eigenvalue \(-1\text{.}\)
    We claim that these two cases exhaust all eigenvectors of \(T\text{.}\) Indeed, in general a nonzero vector \(\boldv\) lies in the line \(\ell'=\Span\{\boldx\}\text{,}\) and its reflection \(T(\boldx)\) lies in the line \(\ell''=\Span\{T(\boldx)\}\text{,}\) which itself is the result of reflecting the line \(\ell'\) through \(\ell\text{.}\) Now assume \(T(\boldx)=\lambda\boldx\text{.}\) We must have \(\lambda\ne 0\text{,}\) since \(T(\boldv)\ne \boldzero\) if \(\boldx\ne \boldzero\text{;}\) but if \(\lambda\ne 0\) it follows that the line \(\ell=\Span\{\boldx\}\) and its reflection \(\ell''=\Span\{T(\boldv)\}\) are equal. Clearly the only lines that are mapped to themselves by reflection through \(\ell\) are \(\ell\) and \(\ell^\perp\text{.}\) Thus if \(\boldx\) is an eigenvector of \(T\) it must lie in \(\ell\) or \(\ell^\perp\text{.}\)

    Example 1.15.8. Rotation in \(\R^2\).

    Fix an angle \(\theta\in [0,2\pi)\) and consider the linear transformation \(T_A\colon \R^2\rightarrow \R^2\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{amatrix}[rr] \cos\theta\amp -\sin \theta \\ \sin\theta \amp \cos\theta \end{amatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Geometrically, \(T_A\) is rotation by \(\theta\) about the origin. Find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(T_A\text{.}\) You should treat three separate cases: \(\theta=0\text{,}\) \(\theta=\pi\text{,}\) \(\theta\ne 0,\pi\text{.}\)

    Example 1.15.9. Transposition.

    Consider the linear transformation
    \begin{align*} S\colon M_{22} \amp\rightarrow M_{22} \\ A \amp\mapsto A^T \text{.} \end{align*}
    Determine all eigenvectors and eigenvalues of \(S\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    To be an eigenvector of \(S\) a nonzero matrix \(A\) must satisfy \(S(A)=\lambda A\) for some \(\lambda\in \R\text{.}\) Using the definition of \(S\text{,}\) this means
    \begin{equation} A^T=\lambda A\text{.}\tag{1.44} \end{equation}
    We ask for which scalars \(\lambda\in \R\) does there exist a nonzero matrix \(A\) satisfying (1.44). Let’s consider some specific choices of \(\lambda\text{.}\)
    Case: \(\lambda=1\).
    In this case (1.44) reads \(A^T=A\text{.}\) Thus the eigenvectors of \(S\) with eigenvalue \(1\) are precisely the nonzero symmetric matrices: i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{amatrix}[rr]a\amp b\\ b\amp c \end{amatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Case: \(\lambda= -1\).
    For this choice of \(\lambda\) we seek nonzero matrices satisfying \(S(A)=A^T=(-1)A=-A\text{.}\) These are precisely the nonzero skew-symmetric matrices: i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{amatrix}[rr]0\amp a\\ -a \amp 0 \end{amatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Case: \(\lambda\ne \pm 1\).
    Suppose \(A=\begin{amatrix}[cc]a\amp b \\ c\amp d \end{amatrix}\) satisfies \(A^T=\lambda A\text{.}\) Equating the entries of these two matrices yields the system
    \begin{align*} a \amp =\lambda a \\ d \amp = \lambda d\\ b \amp =\lambda c \\ c \amp =\lambda b \text{.} \end{align*}
    The first two equations imply \(a=d=0\text{,}\) using the fact that \(\lambda\ne 1\text{.}\) The second two equations imply further that \(b=\lambda^2 b\) and \(c=\lambda^2 c\text{.}\) Since \(\lambda\ne \pm 1\text{,}\) \(\lambda^2\ne 1\text{.}\) It follows that \(b=c=0\text{.}\) We conclude that for \(\lambda\ne \pm 1\text{,}\) if \(A^T=\lambda A\text{,}\) then \(A=\boldzero\text{.}\) It follows that \(\lambda\) is not an eigenvalue of \(S\) in this case.
    In summation, our analysis shows that the transposition operator \(S\) has exactly two eigenvalues, \(\lambda_1=1\) and \(\lambda_2=-1\text{,}\) that the eigenvectors of \(S\) with eigenvalue 1 are the nonzero symmetric matrices, and that the eigenvalues of \(S\) with eigenvalue \(-1\) are the nonzero skew-symmetric matrices.

    Example 1.15.10. Differentiation.

    Let \(T\colon C^\infty(\R)\rightarrow C^\infty(\R)\) be defined as \(T(f)=f'\text{.}\) Find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(T\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    An eigenvector of \(T\) is a nonzero function \(f\) satisfying \(T(f)=\lambda f\) for some \(\lambda\text{.}\) By definition, this means
    \begin{equation} f'=\lambda f\tag{1.45} \end{equation}
    for some \(\lambda\in\R\text{.}\) Thus \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\) if and only if the differential equation (1.45) has a nonzero solution. This is true for all \(\lambda\in \R\text{!}\) Indeed for any \(\lambda\) the exponential function \(f(x)=e^{\lambda x}\) satisfies \(f'(x)=\lambda e^{\lambda x}=\lambda f(x)\) for all \(x\in \R\text{.}\) Furthermore, any solution to (1.45) is of the form \(f(x)=ce^{\lambda x}\) for some \(c\in \R\text{.}\) We conclude that (a) every \(\lambda\in \R\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\text{,}\) and (b) for a given \(\lambda\text{,}\) the \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors of \(T\) are precisely the functions of the form \(f(x)=ce^{\lambda x}\) for some \(c\ne 0\text{.}\)

    Subsection Eigenspaces

    The next theorem and its corollary turn out to be crucial for computing eigenvalues and eigenvectors. They are simple consequences of the observation that \(v\) satisfies \(T(v)=\lambda v\) if and only if \((T-\lambda I_V)(v)=\boldzero\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.15.11. Eigenvalues and null spaces.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) and let \(\lambda \in F\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\)
    2. \(\lambda I_V-T\) is not injective.
    3. \(\NS (\lambda I_V-T)\ne \{\boldzero\}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    We have
    \begin{align*} \lambda \text{ eigenvalue of } T \amp \iff T(v)=\lambda v \text{ for some nonzero} v\in V\\ \amp\iff T(v)=\lambdaI_V(v) \text{ for some nonzero} v\in V \\ \amp \iff (\lambda I_V-T)(v)=\boldzero \text{ for some nonzero} v\in V\\ \amp \iff \NS(\lambda I_V-T)\ne \{\boldzero\}\\ \amp \iff \lambda I_V-T \text{ not injective}\text{.} \end{align*}
    The following corollary elaborates on Theorem 1.15.11 in the special case where \(V\) is finite dimensional.

    Corollary 1.15.12.

    Let \(V\) be finite dimensional, let \(T\in\mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) and let \(\lambda\in F\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\)
    2. \(\lambda I_V-T\) is not injective. (Equivalently, \(\NS (T-\lambda I_V)\ne \{\boldzero\}\text{.}\))
    3. \(\lambda I_V-T\) is not surjective. (Equivalently, \(\im (T-\lambda I_V)\ne V\text{.}\))
    4. \(\lambda I_V-T\) is not invertible.

    Proof.

    Let \(S=\lambda I_V-T\text{.}\) The additional equivalent statements we obtain when \(\dim V<\infty\) are the result of the fact (special to this setting) that \(S\) is invertible if and only if it is injective if and only if it is surjective.
    From Theorem 1.15.11 and its proof, we see that the \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors of a linear operator \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) are precisely the nonzero elements of \(\NS(\lambda I_V-T)\text{.}\) This motivates the following definition.

    Definition 1.15.13. Eigenspaces.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{.}\) For all \(\lambda\in F\) we define the \(\lambda\)-eigenspace of \(T\), denoted \(E(\lambda, T)\text{,}\) as
    \begin{equation} E(\lambda, T)=\NS(\lambda I_V-T)\text{.}\tag{1.46} \end{equation}
    Note that the nonzero elements of \(E(\lambda, T)\) are precisely the \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors of \(T\text{,}\) and in particular, that \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\) if and only if \(E(\lambda, T)\) is a nonzero subspace of \(V\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.15.14. Eigenspaces as packets of eigenvectors.

    It is important to realize that if \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of a linear operator \(T\text{,}\) then there is not just one \(\lambda\)-eigenvector, but rather an entire subspace \(E(\lambda, T)\) of eigenvectors: that is, each of the nonzero elements of \(E(\lambda, T)\) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{.}\) Thus every linear operator has associated to it various “packets” of eigenvectors \(E(\lambda, T)\) of varying size, as measured by \(\dim E(\lambda, T)\text{.}\)

    Example 1.15.15. Eigenspaces of transposition.

    Let \(S\colon F^{n,n}\rightarrow F^{n,n}\) be defined as \(S(A)=A^T\text{.}\) From our analysis in Example 1.15.9 we see that the eigenspaces of \(S\) are described as follows:
    \begin{align*} E(1,S) \amp =\{A\in F^{n,n}\colon A^T=A\}\\ E(-1,S) \amp =\{A\in F^{n,n}\colon A^T=-A\}\\ E(\lambda, S) \amp =\{\boldzero\} \text{ for } \lambda\ne \pm 1\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since \(E(1,S)\) and \(E(-1,S)\) are the only nonzero eigenspaces of \(S\text{,}\) we see (again) that \(1\) and \(-1\) are the only eigenvalues of \(S\text{.}\)

    Example 1.15.16. Eigenspaces of differentiation.

    Let \(I\) be an interval in \(\R\text{,}\) and let \(T\colon C^\infty(I)\rightarrow C^{\infty}(I)\) be defined as \(T(f)=f'\text{.}\) From our analysis in Example 1.15.10 we conclude that for all \(\lambda\in \R\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} E(\lambda, T)=\{f\in C^\infty(I)\colon f(x)=ce^{\lambda x} \text{ for some } c\in \R\}=\Span (f_\lambda)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(f_\lambda(x)=e^{\lambda x}\text{.}\) Since \(f_\lambda\ne 0\) for all \(\lambda\text{,}\) we see that each eigenspace of \(T\) is 1-dimensional, spanned by \(f_\lambda\text{.}\) Thus the eigenvectors of \(T\) are packaged in the uncountably many eigenspaces \(E(\lambda, T)=\Span(f_\lambda)\text{.}\)
