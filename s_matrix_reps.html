    Section 1.13 Coordinate vectors and matrix representations

    Given a basis \(B=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) of a vector space \(V\text{,}\) we know from Theorem 1.7.5 that any \(v\in V\) can be expressed in a unique way as
    \begin{equation*} v=\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(c_i=0\) for all but finitely many \(i\text{.}\) The notion of a coordinate vector turn this observation into a computational tool by exploiting the resulting correspondence
    \begin{equation} v\in V \longleftrightarrow (c_i)_{i\in I}\in F^I\text{.}\tag{1.30} \end{equation}
    We will use the correspondence (1.30) in two distinct ways, as described below.
    1. Given an \(n\)-dimensional vector space \(V\) and basis \(B\text{,}\) the correspondence (1.30) is an isomorphism that allows us to treat elements of the abstract space \(V\) as if they were elements of \(F^n\text{,}\) and to then make use of our wealth of computational procedures related to \(n\)-tuples.
    2. The correspondence (1.30) is also useful when working in \(F^n\) itself. Namely, there will be situations where it is convenient to represent vectors with a particular nonstandard basis \(B\text{,}\) as opposed to the standard basis \((\bolde_1, \bolde_2, \dots, \bolde_n)\text{.}\) In this setting the correspondence (1.30) will be used as a “change of coordinates” technique.

    Subsection Coordinate vectors

    Definition 1.13.1. Coordinate vectors.

    Let \(B=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) be a basis of the vector space \(V\text{.}\) Given \(v\in V\text{,}\) let \((c_i)_{i\in I}\) be the unique choice of scalars such that \(v\) can be expressed as the linear combination
    \begin{equation} v=\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i\text{.}\tag{1.31} \end{equation}
    We define the tuple \((c_i)_{i\in I}\in F^I\) to be the coordinate vector of \(v\) with respect to \(B\text{,}\) denoted \([v]_B\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation} [v]_B=(c_i)_{i\in I}\text{.}\tag{1.32} \end{equation}
    We call the function
    \begin{align*} [\phantom{v}]_B\colon V \amp \rightarrow F^I\\ v \amp \longmapsto [v]_B \end{align*}
    the coordinate vector map with respect to \(B\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.13.2. Coordinate vectors.

    Let \(B=(v_i)_{i\in I}\) be a basis of the vector space \(V\text{,}\) and let \([\phantom]_B\colon V\rightarrow F^I\) be the coordinate vector map with respect to \(B\text{.}\)
    1. \([\phantom{v}]_B\) is an injective transformation from \(V\) to \(F^I\text{.}\)
    2. \([\phantom{v}]_B\) is an isomorphism from \(V\) to \(F^I\) if and only if \(I\) is finite.

    Proof.

    1. We use the 1-step technique to show \([\phantom{v}]_B\) is a linear transformation. Given \(v,w\in V\) and scalars \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) we first express
      \begin{align*} v \amp =\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i\\ w \amp =\sum_{i\in I}d_iv_i \end{align*}
      as linear combinations of elements of \(B\text{,}\) and then compute
      \begin{align*} [cv+dw]_{B} \amp =[\sum_{i\in I}(cc_i+dd_i)v_i \amp (\text{vect. arith.})\\ \amp =(cc_i+dd_i)_{i\in I} \amp (\text{def. of } [\phantom{v}]_B)\\ \amp = c(c_i)_{i\in I}+d(d_i)_{i\in I}\amp (\text{vect. arith.}) \\ \amp =c[v]_B+d[w]_B \amp (\text{def. of } [\phantom{v}]_B)\text{.} \end{align*}
      Next, we prove \([\phantom{v}]_B\) is injective by showing that its null space is trivial. We have Given any \(v\in V\text{,}\) writing \(v=\sum_{i\in i}c_iv_i\) as a linear combination of the elements of \(B\text{,}\) we see that
      \begin{align*} [v]_B=\boldzero \amp \iff c_i=0 \text{ for all } i\in I \\ \amp \iff \sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i=\boldzero \amp (B \text{ lin. ind.})\\ \amp \iff v=\boldzero\text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus \(\NS [\phantom{v}]_B=\{\boldzero\}\text{.}\)
    2. First assume \(I\) is finite, with \(\abs{I}=n\in \Z_{\geq 0\}\text{.}\) In this case, we have
      \begin{equation*} \dim V=\dim F^I=n\text{,} \end{equation*}
      and since \([\phantom{v}]_B\) is injective (by (a)), we conclude \([\phantom{v}]_B\) is an isomorphism (1.12.5).
      Lastly, assume \(I\) is infinite. Given any \(v\in V\text{,}\) the coefficients \(c_i\) appearing in the unique linear combination expression \(v=\sum_{i\in I}c_iv_i\) satisfy \(c_i=0\) for all but finitely many \(i\in I\text{.}\) It follows that its image \([v]_B=(c_i)_{i\in I}\) is a tuple with only finitely many nonzero coordinates. But since \(I\) is infinite, there are tuples in \(F^I\text{,}\) with infinitely many nonzero coordinates: e.g., the tuple \(\boldy=(1)_{i\in I}\) consisting of all 1’s. It follows that \(\im [\phantom{v}]_B\) is not surjective (e.g., \(\boldy\notin \im [\phantom{v}]_B\)), and hence not an isomorphism.

    Procedure 1.13.3. Computing coordinate vectors.

    Let \(B=(v_i)_{i\in }\) be a basis of the vector space \(V\text{.}\) Given \(v\in V\text{,}\) compute the coordinate vector \([v]_B\in F^I\) by following these steps.
    1. Set up the vector equation
      \begin{equation} v=\sum_{i\in i}c_iv_i\tag{1.33} \end{equation}
      in the unknowns \(c_i\in F\text{.}\)
    2. Solve for the unknowns \(c_i\) in some manner. A surefire technique is to reduce the vector equation (1.33) to a linear system and use Gaussian elimination. However, there are some situations when you can simply produce the scalars \(c_i\) by inspection.
    3. Conclude that \([v]_B=(c_i)_{i\in I}\text{.}\)
    As illustrated by the next example, one setting for which we can compute \([v]_B\) by inspection (see (2) of Procedure 1.13.3) is when \(B\) is one of our standard ordered bases.

    Example 1.13.4. Standard bases.

    Computing coordinate vectors relative to one of our standard ordered bases for \(F^n\text{,}\) \(F^{m,n}\text{,}\) or \(P_{n}\) amounts to just listing the coefficients or entries used to specify the given vector. The examples below serve to illustrate the general method in this setting.
    1. Consider the standard basis \(B=(\bolde_1, \bolde_2, \bolde_3)\) of \(F^3\text{.}\) For any \(\boldx=(a,b,c)\in F^3\) we have \([\boldx]_{B}=(a,b,c)\text{,}\) since \((a,b,c)=a\bolde_1+b\bolde_2+c\bolde_3\text{.}\)
    2. Consider the standard basis \(B=(E_{11}, E_{12}, E_{21}, E_{22})\) of \(F^{2,2}\text{.}\) For any \(A=\begin{bmatrix}a\amp b\\ c\amp d \end{bmatrix}\) we have \([A]_B=(a,b,c,d)\) since
      \begin{equation*} \underset{A}{\begin{bmatrix}a\amp b\\ c\amp d \end{bmatrix}}=a\underset{E_{11}}{\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0 \\ 0\amp 0\end{bmatrix}}+b\underset{E_{12}}{\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 1 \\ 0\amp 0\end{bmatrix}}+c\underset{E_{21}}{\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 0 \\ 1\amp 0\end{bmatrix}}+d\underset{E_{22}}{\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 0 \\ 0\amp 1\end{bmatrix}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    3. Let \(B=(1,x,x^2,\dots)\) be the standard basis of \(P(I)\text{,}\) where \(I\) is an infinite subset of the field \(F\text{.}\) Given any polynomial \(f(x)=\anpoly\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation*} [f]_B=(a_0,a_1,\dots, a_n, 0,0,\dots)\text{,} \end{equation*}
      since
      \begin{equation*} f=a_0\cdot 1+a_1x+\cdots a_nx^n+0x^{n+1}+0x^{n+2}+\cdots\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Remark 1.13.5. Coordinate vectors with respect to standarad bases.

    As illustrated in the example above, in the very particular case where \(V=F^n\) and \(B=(\bolde_1,\bolde_2,\dots, \bolde_n)\text{,}\) the coordinate vector map \([\phantom{v}]_B\colon F^n \rightarrow F^n\) is just the identity map: i.e., we have
    \begin{equation*} [(a_1,a_2,\dots, a_n)]_B=(a_1,a_2,\dots, a_n) \end{equation*}
    for all \((a_1,a_2,\dots, a_n)\in F^n\text{.}\) As simple as this observation is, it is worth pointing out, as this particular case often arises when computing examples (or proving theorems).

    Example 1.13.6. Reorderings of standard bases.

    If we choose an alternate ordering of one of the standard ordered bases, the entries of the coordinate vector are reordered accordingly, as illustrated by the examples below.
    1. Consider the reordered basis \(B=(\bolde_2, \bolde_1, \bolde_3)\) of \(F^3\text{.}\) Given \(\boldx=(a,b,c)\in \R^3\) we have \([\boldx]_B=(b,a,c)\text{,}\) since
      \begin{equation*} \boldx=b\bolde_2+a\bolde_1+c\bolde_3\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. Consider the reordered basis \(B=(E_{22},E_{21},E_{12},E_{11})\) of \(F^{2,2}\text{.}\) Given \(A=\begin{bmatrix}a\amp b\\ c\amp d\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) we have \([A]_B=(d,c,b,a)\text{,}\) since
      \begin{equation*} \underset{A}{\begin{bmatrix}a\amp b\\ c\amp d \end{bmatrix}}=d\underset{E_{22}}{\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 0 \\ 0\amp 1\end{bmatrix}}+ c\underset{E_{21}}{\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 0 \\ 1\amp 0\end{bmatrix}}+ b\underset{E_{12}}{\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 1 \\ 0\amp 0\end{bmatrix}}+ a\underset{E_{22}}{\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0 \\ 0\amp 0\end{bmatrix}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.13.7. Nonstandard bases.

    For a nonstandard ordered basis, we usually compute coordinate vectors by solving a relevant system of linear equations, as the examples below illustrate.
    1. Let \(V=\R^2\text{,}\) \(B=(\boldx_1=(1,2),\boldx_2=(1,1))\text{,}\) and \(\boldx=(3,3)\text{.}\) Compute \([\boldx]_B\text{.}\) More generally, compute \([(a,b)]_B\) for an arbitrary \((a,b)\in \R^2\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(V=P_2(I)\text{,}\) \(B=(f_1=1+x+x^2, f_2=1-x, f_3=1-x^2)\text{,}\) and \(f=1+2x+x^2\text{.}\) Compute \([f]_B\text{.}\) More generally, compute \([a+bx+cx^2]_B\) for an arbitrary element \(a+bx+cx^2\in P_2(I)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. Using Procedure 1.13.3, we compute \([(3,3)]_B\) by finding the unique pair \((c_1, c_2)\) satisfying
      \begin{equation*} (3,3)=c_1(1,2)+c_2(1,1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      By inspection, we see that
      \begin{equation*} (3,3)=3(1,1)=0(1,2)+3(1,1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      We conclude that
      \begin{equation*} [\boldx]_{B}=(0,3)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      More generally, to compute \([\boldx]_B\) for an arbitrary \(\boldx=(a,b)\in \R^2\text{,}\) we must find the pair \((c_1,c_2)\) satisfying \((a,b)=c_1(1,2)+c_2(1,1)\text{,}\) or equivalently
      \begin{equation*} \begin{linsys}{2} c_1\amp +\amp c_2 \amp =\amp a\\ 2c_1\amp +\amp c_2\amp =\amp b \end{linsys}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      The usual Gaussian elimination technique yields the unique solution \((c_1,c_2)=(-a+b,2a-b)\text{,}\) and thus
      \begin{equation*} [\boldx]_B=(-a+b, 2a-b) \end{equation*}
      for \(\boldx=(a,b)\text{.}\)
    2. To compute \([f]_B\) we must find the unique triple \((c_1,c_2,c_3)\) satisfying
      \begin{equation*} 1+2x+x^2=c_1(1+x+x^2)+c_2(1-x)+c_3(1-x^2)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      The equivalent linear system once we combine like terms and equate coefficients (1.4.13) is
      \begin{equation*} \begin{linsys}{3} c_1\amp +\amp c_2\amp +\amp c_3\amp =\amp 1\\ c_1\amp -\amp c_2\amp \amp \amp =\amp 2\\ c_1\amp \amp \amp -\amp c_3\amp =\amp 1 \end{linsys}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      which row reduces to the system
      \begin{equation*} \begin{linsys}{3} c_1\amp +\amp c_2\amp +\amp c_3\amp =\amp 1\\ \amp \amp c_2\amp \amp \frac{1}{2}c_3\amp =\amp -\frac{1}{2}\\ \amp \amp \amp \amp c_3\amp =\amp \frac{1}{3} \end{linsys}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      The unique solution to this system is \((c_1,c_2,c_3)=(4/3, -2/3, 1/3)\text{.}\) We conclude
      \begin{equation*} [f]_B=\frac{1}{3}(4, -2, 1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      The same reasoning shows that more generally, given \(f=a+bx+cx^2\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation*} [f]_B=\frac{1}{3}(a+b+c, a-2b+c, a+b-2c)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Procedure 1.13.8. Contracting and extending to bases in general spaces.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space of dimension \(n\text{,}\) and let \(S=(v_1, v_2,\dots, v_r)\) be a tuple of vectors of \(V\text{.}\)
    1. Contracting to a basis.
      Let \(W=\Span S\text{.}\) To contract \(S\) to a basis \(W\text{,}\) proceed as follows.
      1. Pick any ordered basis \(B\) of \(V\) and let \(S'=\{[v_1]_B, [v_2]_B, \dots, [v_r]_B\}\subseteq \R^n\text{.}\)
      2. Use a the column space algorithm to contract \(S'\) to a basis \(B'=([v_{i_1}]_B, [v_{i_2}]_B, \dots, [v_{i_k}]_B)\) of \(\Span S'\text{.}\)
      3. The tuple \(B=(v_{i_1}, v_{i_2}, \dots, v_{i_k})\) is a basis for \(W=\Span S\text{.}\)
    2. Extending to a basis.
      Assume \(S\) is linearly independent. To extend \(S\) to a basis of \(V\) proceed as follows.
      1. Pick any ordered basis \(B\) of \(V\) and let \(S'=([v_1]_B, [v_2]_B, \dots, [v_r]_B)\text{,}\) the corresponding tuple in \(F^n\text{.}\)
      2. Use an appropriate fundamental space algorithm to extend \(S'\) to a basis \(B'=([v_1]_B, [v_2]_B, \dots, [v_r]_B, \boldx_{r+1}, \dots, \boldx_n)\) of \(F^n\text{.}\)
      3. For all \(r+1\leq i\leq n\) find vectors \(v_{i}\in V\) satisfying \([v_i]_B=\boldx_i\text{.}\) The tuple \(B=(v_1,\dots, v_r,v_{r+1},\dots,v_n)\) is a basis of \(V\text{.}\)

    Example 1.13.9.

    Let \(S=(A_1,A_2,A_3,A_4)\) of the tuple of matrices of \(\R^{2,2}\text{,}\) where
    \begin{align*} A_1\amp =\begin{bmatrix}2\amp 1\\ 0\amp -2 \end{bmatrix}\amp A_2\amp =\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 1\\ 1\amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \\ A_3\amp =\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 1\\ 2\amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\amp A_4\amp =\begin{bmatrix}-1\amp 0\\ 1\amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \text{,} \end{align*}
    and define \(W=\Span S\text{.}\)
    1. Contract \(S\) to a basis of \(W\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(W'=\{A\in \R^{2,2}\colon \tr A=0\}\text{,}\) where for any \(n\times n\) matrix \(A=[a_{ij}]\text{,}\) \(\tr A=\sum_{i=1}^na_ii\text{,}\) the {\em trace} of \(A\text{:}\) i.e., \(W'\) is the space of all trace-zero \(2\times 2\) matrices. Show that \(W\subseteq W'\text{,}\) and determine whether \(W=W'\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. Let \(B=(E_{11}, E_{12}, E_{21}, E_{22})\) be the standard basis of \(\R^{2,2}\text{.}\) Apply \([\phantom{v}]_B\) to the elements of the given \(S\) to get a corresponding set \(S'\subseteq\R^4\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} S'=(\underset{[A_1]_B}{(2,1,0,-2)}, \underset{[A_2]_B}{(1,1,1,-1)}, \underset{[A_3]_B}{(0,1,2,0)}, \underset{[A_4]_B}{(-1,0,1,1)})\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Apply the column space procedure to contract \(S'\) to a basis \(B'\) of \(\Span S'\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \begin{amatrix}[rrrr] 2\amp 1\amp 0\amp -1 \\ 1\amp 1\amp 1\amp 0\\ 0\amp 1\amp 2\amp 1\\ -2\amp -1\amp 0\amp 1 \end{amatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{Gauss. elim.}} \begin{amatrix}[rrrr] \boxed{1} \amp \frac{1}{2} \amp 0 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \amp \boxed{1} \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{amatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      We conclude that the first two vectors of \(S'\) form a basis for \(\Span S'\text{;}\) it follows that the first two elements of \(S\) form a basis for \(W=\Span S\text{.}\) That is \(W=\Span ((A_1,A_2))\text{.}\) In particular, this means \(\dim W=2\text{.}\)
    2. Since \(\tr A_i=0\) for all \(1\leq i\leq 4\text{,}\) we have \(A_i\in W'\) for all \(i\text{,}\) and hence \(W=\Span (A_1,A_2,A_3,A_4)\subseteq W'\text{.}\)
      By inspection, we see that the space \(W'\) of all trace-zero matrices has basis
      \begin{equation*} \left\( \begin{amatrix}[rr]1\amp 0\\ 0 \amp -1 \end{amatrix}, \begin{amatrix}[rr]0\amp 1\\ 0\amp 0 \end{amatrix}, \begin{amatrix}[rr]0 \amp 0\\ 1\amp 0 \end{amatrix} \right\)\text{,} \end{equation*}
      and hence \(\dim W'=3\text{.}\) Since \(\dim W< \dim W'\text{,}\) we conclude that \(W\ne W'\text{.}\)

    Subsection Matrix representations

    Of course, we are not just interested in studying vector spaces and their elements, but also linear transformations \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) between vector spaces. Once again, by choosing bases for \(V\) and \(W\) we are able to get a computational grip on the transformation \(T\text{.}\) When both \(V\) and \(W\) are finite dimensional, this takes the form of a matrix representation of \(T\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.13.10. Matrix representations of linear transformations.

    Let \(V\) and \(W\) be vector spaces with ordered bases \(B=(v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n)\) and \(B'\text{,}\) respectively. Given a linear transformation \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\text{,}\) the matrix representing \(T\) with respect to \(B\) and \(B'\), is the \(m\times n\) matrix \([T]_B^{B'}\) whose \(j\)-th column is \([T(v_j)]_{B'}\text{,}\) considered as a column vector: i.e.,
    \begin{equation} [T]_B^{B'}=\begin{amatrix}[cccc]\vert \amp \vert \amp \amp \vert \\ \left[T(v_1)\right]_{B'}\amp [T(v_2)]_{B'}\amp \dots \amp [T(v_n)]_{B'} \\ \vert \amp \vert \amp \amp \vert \end{amatrix}\text{.}\tag{1.34} \end{equation}
    In the special case where \(W=V\) and we pick \(B'=B\) we write simply \([T]_B\text{.}\)

    Example 1.13.11. Matrix representation.

    The function
    \begin{align*} T\colon \R^3 \amp \rightarrow \R^{2,2}\\ (x,y,z) \amp \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} x+y+z\amp x-z\\ x+2y\amp y-z \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    is linear. Compute \([T]_{B}^{B'}\text{,}\) where \(B\) and \(B'\) are the standard bases for \(\R^3\) and \(\R^{2,2}\text{,}\) respectively.
    Solution.
    We have \(B=(\bolde_1,\bolde_2, \bolde_3)\) and \(B'=(E_{11},E_{12},E_{21},E_{22})\text{.}\) By definition, we have
    \begin{align*} [T]_{B}^{B'} \amp =\begin{bmatrix} \vert \amp \vert \amp \vert \\ [T(\bolde_1)]_{B'} \amp [T(\bolde_2)]_{B'}\amp [T(\bolde_3)]_{B'} \\ \vert \amp \vert \amp \vert \end{bmatrix} \text{.} \end{align*}
    We first compute \(T(\bolde_i)\) for each \(1\leq i\leq 3\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} T(\bolde_1) \amp = T(1,0,0)=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 1\\ 1 \amp 0\end{bmatrix}\\ T(\bolde_2) \amp = T(0,1,0)=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0 \\ 2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\\ T(\bolde_3) \amp = T(0,0,1)=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp -1 \\ 0 \amp -1\end{bmatrix} \text{.} \end{align*}
    To finish our computation, we must compute \([T(\bolde_i)]_{B'}\) for each \(1\leq i\leq 3\text{.}\) Since \(B'\) is the standard basis of \(\R^{2,2}\text{,}\) this is not difficult: in general we have
    \begin{equation*} \left[ \begin{bmatrix}a\amp b\\ c\amp d\end{bmatrix}\right]_{B'}=(a,b,c,d)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus
    \begin{align*} [T(\bolde_1)]_{B'} \amp = (1,1,1,0)\\ [T(\bolde_2)]_{B'} \amp = (1,0,2,1)\\ [T(\bolde_3)]_{B'} \amp = (1,-1,0,-1) \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} [T]_{B}^{B'}=\begin{amatrix}[rrr] 1\amp 1\ \amp 1\\ 1\amp 0\amp -1\\ 1\amp 2\amp 0\\ 0\amp 1\amp -1\end{amatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.13.12. Differentiation.

    Let \(I\) be an infinite subset of \(\R\text{,}\) and define \(T\colon P_n(I)\rightarrow P_{n-1}(I)\) as \(T(f)=f'\text{.}\) Compute \([T]_B^{B'}\text{,}\) where \(B\) and \(B'\) are the standard bases of \(P_n(I)\) and \(P_{n-1}(I)\)
    Solution.
    Let \(B=(1,x,x^2,\dots, x^n)\) and \(B'=(1,x,x^2,\dots, x^n)\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation*} T(x^k)=\frac{d}{dx}(x^k)=kx^{k-1} \end{equation*}
    for all \(0\leq k\leq n\text{.}\) Since \(B'\) is standard, we easily compute
    \begin{equation*} [T(x^k)]_{B'}=(0,0,\dots, \underset{(k-1)-\text{th coor.}}{k},0,\dots, 0)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Using recipe (1.34) we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} [T]_B^{B'}= \begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1\amp 0 \amp 0\amp \dots \amp 0 \\ 0\amp 0\amp 2 \amp 0 \amp \amp 0 \\ 0\amp 0\amp 0 \amp 3 \amp 0 \amp \vdots \\ \vdots \\ 0\amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \dots \amp n \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In more detail, \([T]_B^{B'}\) is the \((n-1)\times n\) matrix whose first column is the zero column vector, and whose \(k\)-th column for \(2\leq k\leq n\) is the column vector that has \(k\) in the \((k-1)\)-th entry and zeros elsewhere.

    Example 1.13.13. Different choice of bases.

    Define \(T\colon \R^2\rightarrow \R^2\) as \(T(x,y)=(4x-3y,2x-y)\text{.}\)
    1. Compute \([T]_B\text{,}\) where \(B=(\bolde_1, \bolde_2)\) is the standard basis of \(\R^2\text{.}\)
    2. Compute \([T]_{B'}\text{,}\) where \(B'=((1,1), (1,-1))\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. According to Example 1.13.16, since \(B\) is the standard basis \([T]_B\) is the matrix \(A\) such that \(T=T_A\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} [T]_B\amp=\begin{bmatrix} \vert \amp \vert \\ T(\bolde_1)\amp T(\bolde_2)\\ \vert \amp \vert \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{amatrix}[rr] 4\amp -3\\ 2\amp -1 \end{amatrix} \text{.} \end{align*}
    2. We have
      \begin{align*} [T]_{B'}=[T]_{B'}^{B'} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \vert\amp \vert\\ [T((1,1))]_{B'}\amp [T(1,-1)]_{B'}\\ \vert \amp \vert \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \vert\amp \vert\\ [(1,1)]_{B'}\amp [(7,3)]_{B'}\\ \vert \amp \vert \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = \begin{amatrix}[rr] 1\amp 5\\ 0\amp 2 \end{amatrix}\text{,} \end{align*}
      where the last equality uses the fact that \([(1,1)]_{B'}=(1,0)\) and \([(7,3)]_{B'}=(5,2)\text{,}\) as you can verify yourself.
    So we have \([T]_B=\begin{amatrix}[rr]4\amp -1\\ 2\amp -1 \end{amatrix}\) and \([T]_{B'}=\begin{amatrix}[rr]1\amp 5\\ 0\amp 2 \end{amatrix}\text{.}\) Moral: different choices of bases yield different matrix representations.

    Theorem 1.13.14. Defining property of matrix representation.

    Let \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) be a linear transformation, where \(\dim V=n\) and \(\dim W=m\text{,}\) and let \(B, B'\) be ordered bases for \(V\) and \(W\text{,}\) respectively.
    1. For all \(v\in V\) we have
      \begin{equation} [T]_{B}^{B'}[v]_B=[T(v)]_{B'}\text{.}\tag{1.35} \end{equation}
    2. Property (1.35) defines \([T]_B^{B'}\) uniquely: i.e., if \(A\) is an \(m\times n\) matrix satisfying \(A[v]_B=[v]_{B'}\) for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) then \(A=[T]_{B}^{B''}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(B=(v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n)\text{.}\)
    1. By definition we have
      \begin{equation*} [T]_B^{B'}=\begin{amatrix}[cccc]\vert \amp \vert \amp \amp \vert \\ \left[T(v_1)\right]_{B'}\amp [T(v_2)]_{B'}\amp \dots \amp [T(v_n)]_{B'} \\ \vert \amp \vert \amp \amp \vert \end{amatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Given any \(v\in V\text{,}\) we can write
      \begin{equation*} v=c_1v_1+c_2v_2+\dots +c_nv_n \end{equation*}
      for some \(c_i\in F\text{,}\) in which case we have
      \begin{align*} [T]_{B}^{B'}[v] \amp= [T]_{B}^{B'} \begin{bmatrix} c_1\\ c_2\\ \vdots \\ c_n \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp=c_1[T(v_1)]_{B'}+c_n[T(v_n)]_{B'}+\cdots +c_n[T(v_n)]_{B'} \amp (\text{column method})\\ \amp = [c_1T(v_1)+c_2T(v_2)+\cdots +c_nT(v_n)]_{B'} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/th_coor_vectors.html}{\text{1.13.2}})\\ \amp=[T(c_1v_1+c_2v_2+\cdots +c_nv_n)]_{B'} \amp (T \text{ is linear})\\ \amp =[T(v)]_{B'}\text{,} \end{align*}
      as desired.
    2. If \(A\) satisfies
      \begin{equation*} A[v]_B=[T(v)]_{B'} \end{equation*}
      for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) then in particular we have
      \begin{equation} A[v_i]_B=[T(v_i)]_{B'}\tag{1.36} \end{equation}
      for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\) Since \(v_i\) is the \(i\)-th element of \(B\text{,}\) we have \([v_i]_B=\bolde_i\text{,}\) the \(i\)-th standard basis element of \(F^n\text{.}\) Using the column method of matrix multiplciation, we see that
      \begin{equation*} A[v_i]_B=A\bolde_i=\boldc_i, \end{equation*}
      where \(\boldc_i\) is the \(i\)-th column of \(A\text{.}\) Thus (1.36) implies that the \(i\)-th column of \(A\) is equal to \([T(v_i)]_{B}\text{,}\) the \(i\)-th column of \([T]_B^{B'}\text{,}\) for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\) Since \(A\) and \([T]_{B}^{B'}\) have identical columns, we conclude that \(A=[T]_{B}^{B'}\text{,}\) as desired.

    Remark 1.13.15. Uniqueness of \([T]_B^{B'}\).

    The uniqueness property described in (2) of Theorem 1.13.14 provides an alternative way of computing \([T]_{B}^{B'}\) that can be useful in certain situations: namely, if we can produce an \(m\times n\) matrix \(A\) that satisfies the defining property
    \begin{equation*} A[v]_B=[T(v)]_{B'} \end{equation*}
    for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) then by uniqueness we must have \(A=[T]_B^{B'}\text{.}\)
    We illustrate this technique in the examples and proofs below.

    Example 1.13.16. Standard matrix.

    Let \(T\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\) be a linear transformation. Recall that there is a unique matrix \(A\in F^{m,n}\text{,}\) the standard matrix of \(T\text{,}\) such that \(T=T_A\text{.}\) Prove that \(A=[T]_B^{B'}\text{,}\) where \(B\) and \(B'\) are the standard bases of \(F^m\) and \(F^n\text{,}\) respectively. In other words, the standard matrix of \(T\) is precisely the matrix representation of \(T\) with respect to the standard bases.
    Solution.
    Let \(A\) be the standard matrix of \(T\text{.}\) By definition, this means \(A\boldx=T(\boldx)\) for all \(\boldx\in F^n\text{.}\) Using the uniqueness claim of Theorem 1.13.14, to show that \(A=[T]_{B}^{B'}\text{,}\) it suffices to show that \(A\) satisfies (1.35): i.e.,
    \begin{align*} A[\boldx]_B \amp = [T(\boldx)]_{B'} \end{align*}
    for all \(\boldx\in F^n\text{.}\) Since \(B\) and \(B'\) are standard bases of \(F^n\) and \(F^m\text{,}\) however, we have \([\boldx]_B=\boldx\) and \([T(\boldx)]_{B'}=T(\boldx)\text{.}\) Thus we must show
    \begin{equation*} A\boldx=T(\boldx)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is precisely the defining property of the standard matrix of \(T\text{.}\) Thus \(A=[T]_B^{B'}\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.13.17. Matrix representations of composition.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(U,V)\) and \(S\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) and let \(B, B', B''\) be bases for \(U\text{,}\) \(V\text{,}\) and \(W\text{,}\) respectively. We have
    \begin{equation*} [ST]_{B}^{B''}=[S]_{B'}^{B''}[T]_{B}^{B'}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Let \(C=[S]_{B'}^{B''}[T]_{B}^{B'}\text{.}\) We prove that \([ST]_{B}^{B''}=C\) by showing that \(C\) satisfies the defining equation of \([ST]_{B}^{B''}\text{:}\) i.e., that
    \begin{equation*} C[u]_B=[ST(u)]_{B''} \end{equation*}
    for all \(u\in U\text{.}\) To this end, we have
    \begin{align*} C[u]_B \amp =[S]_{B'}^{B''}[T]_{B}^{B'}[u]_B \\ \amp = [S]_{B'}^{B''}[T(u)]_{B'}\\ \amp [S(T(u))]_{B''}\\ \amp = [ST(u)]_{B''} \end{align*}
    for all \(u\in U\text{.}\) We conclude that \(C=[ST]_{B}^{B''}\) and thus
    \begin{equation*} [S]_{B'}^{B''}[T]_{B}^{B'}=[ST]_B^{B''}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as desired.
    Let \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(B'\) be as in Theorem 1.13.14. The defining property (1.35) of \([T]_B^{B'}\) can be summarized by saying that the following diagram is commutative.
    The diagram being commutative here means that starting with an element \(v\in V\) in the top left of the diagram, whether we travel to the bottom right of the diagram either by first applying \(T\) and then applying \([\hspace{5pt}]_{B'}\) (“go right, then down”), or else by first applying \([\hspace{5pt}]_B\) and then applying \([T]_B^{B'}\) (“go down, then right”), we get the same result! (The bottom map should technically be labeled \(T_A\text{,}\) where \(A=[T]_B^{B'}\text{,}\) but this would detract from the elegance of the diagram.)
    Besides commutativity, the other import feature of Figure 1.13.18 is that the two vertical coordinate transformations identify the domain and codomain of \(T\) with the familiar spaces \(F^n\) and \(F^m\) isomorphically. These properties together allow us to translate any linear algebraic question about \(T\) to an equivalent question about the matrix \(A\text{,}\) as the following theorem indicates.

    Theorem 1.13.19. Matrix representation isomorphism.

    Let \(B\) and \(B'\) be bases of the vector spaces \(V\) and \(W\text{,}\) respectively, where \(\dim V=n\) and \(\dim W=m\text{.}\)
    1. The map
      \begin{align*} [\phantom{A}]_{B}^{B'}\colon \mathcal{L}(V,W)\amp\rightarrow F^{m,n} \\ T \amp \longmapsto [T]_B^{B'} \end{align*}
      is an isomorphism.
    2. We have
      \begin{equation*} \dim \mathcal{L}(V,W)=\dim F^{m,n}=mn. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Statement (2) follows directly from (1) since \(\dim F^{m,n}=mn\text{.}\) (Alternatively, you showed on a homework that \(\mathcal{L}(V,W)\) has a basis \(B=(T_{ij})\) of length \(mn\text{.}\)) To prove (1), we will show that \([\phantom{AA}]_{B}^{B'}\) is linear and bijective.
    The linearity of \([\phantom{AA}]_{B}^{B'}\) can be proved by direct appeal to the formula (1.34) and the fact that the coordinate vector map \([\phantom{v}]_{B'}\) is linear. Readers are invited to pursue this proof on their own. We will instead take the opportunity to make use of the defining property (1.35) within a 1-step proof. Given linear maps \(T,S\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) and scalars \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) we will show that
    \begin{equation*} [cT+dS]_{B}^{B'}=c[T]_{B}^{B'}+d[S]_{B}^{B'} \end{equation*}
    by showing that the matrix \(C=c[T]_{B}^{B'}+d[S]_{B}^{B'}\) satisfies the relevant defining property
    \begin{equation*} C[v]_V=[(cT+dS)(v)]_{B'} \end{equation*}
    for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) Here goes:
    \begin{align*} C[v]_B \amp(c[T]_{B}^{B'}+d[S]_B^{B'})[v]_B\\ \amp =c[T]_{B}^{B'}[v]_B+d[S]_B^{B'}[v]_B \\ \amp =c[T(v)]_{B'}+d[S(v)]_{B'}\\ \amp =[cT(v)+dS(v)]_{B'} \amp (\text{coor. map is lin.}) \\ \amp =[(cT+dS)(v)]_{B'}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since \(C\) satisfies the relevant property, we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} C=c[T]_{B}^{B'}+d[T]_{B}^{B'}=[cT+dS]_{B}^{B'}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as desired.
    We now show directly that \([\phantom{AA}]_B^{B'}\) is bijective: i.e., given any matrix \(A\in F^{m,n}\text{,}\) we will show that there is a unique linear transformation \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) satisfying \([T]_B^{B'}\text{.}\) Let \(B=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_n)\text{.}\) Given \(A\in F^{m,n}\text{,}\) let
    \begin{equation*} \boldy_i=A[v_i]_B \end{equation*}
    for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{,}\) and let \((w_1,w_2,\dots, w_n)\) be the unique tuple of vectors in \(W\) satisfying \([w_i]_{B'}=\boldy_i\text{.}\) (There is such a unique tuple as \([\phantom{v}]_{B'}\) is an isomorphism.) By Theorem 1.9.21 there is a unique linear map \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) satisfying
    \begin{equation*} T(v_i)=w_i \end{equation*}
    for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\) We claim that \([T]_B^{B'}=A\text{:}\) indeed, letting \(A'=[T]_B^{B'}\) and using the defining property (1.35), we see that
    \begin{equation*} A'[v_i]_B=[T]_B^{B'}[v_i]=[T(v_i)]_{B'}=[w_i]_{B'}=A[v_i]_B \end{equation*}
    for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\) Since \(B\) is a basis and \([\phantom{v}]_B\) is an isomorphism, \(B''=([v_1]_B, [v_2]_B, \dots, [v_n]_B)\) is a basis of \(F^n\text{.}\) The equation above proves that \(T_{A'}\) and \(T_{A}\) agree on the basis \(B''\text{.}\) It follows from Theorem 1.9.21 (again) that \(T_{A'}=T_A\text{,}\) and hence that \(A'=A\text{,}\) as desired.

    Theorem 1.13.20. Matrix representations of.

    Let \(B\) and \(B'\) be bases of the vector spaces \(V\) and \(W\text{,}\) respectively, where \(\dim V=n\) and \(\dim W=m\text{,}\) let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) and let \(A=[T]_{B}^{B'}\text{.}\)
    1. The restriction of \([\phantom{v}]_{B}\) to \(\NS T\) defines an isomorphism \([\phantom{v}]_{B}\colon \NS T\rightarrow \NS T_{A}.\) As a result, \(v\in \NS T\) if and only if \([v]_{B}\in \NS A\text{,}\) and \(\dim \NS T=\dim\NS A\text{.}\)
    2. The restriction of \([\phantom{v}]_{B'}\) to \(\range T\subseteq W\) defines an isomorphism \([\phantom{v}]_{B'}\colon \range T\rightarrow \range T_{A}\text{.}\) As a result, \(\boldw\in \range T\) if and only if \([\boldw]_{B'}\in \CS A\text{,}\) and \(\dim \range T=\dim \CS A=\rank A\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Having made explicit the connection between the important subspaces associtated to a linear transformation and its matrix representation, we end with a corollary that connects invertibility of a matrix transformations \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^n\) and its standard matrix \(A\text{.}\) Recall (1.13.16) that in this case we have \(A=[T]_B\text{,}\) where \(B\) is the standard basis of \(F^n\text{.}\) The corollary lists 9 equivalent formulations for a matrix \(A\) (or its matrix transformation \(T_A\)) to be invertible. The statements within each column should be fairly easily seen to be equivalent; equivalence connections between the columns are easily made using the rank-nullity theorem.

    Corollary 1.13.22. Invertible matrix theorem.

    Let \(A\in F^{n,n}\) and let \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^n\) be its corresponding matrix transformation. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(T_A\) is an isomorphism.
    2. \(\displaystyle \NS T_A=\{\boldzero\}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \im T_A=F^n\)
    4. \(A\) is invertible.
    5. \(\displaystyle \NS A=\{\boldzero\}\)
    6. \(\displaystyle \CS A=F^n\)
    7. \(A\boldx=\boldy\) has a unique solution for all \(\boldy\in F^n\text{.}\)
    8. \(A\boldx=\boldzero\) has a unique solution (viz., \(\boldx=\boldzero\)).
