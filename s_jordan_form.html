    Section 1.26 Jordan form

    Definition 1.26.1. Jordan block.

    Let \(F\) be a field. Given \(\lambda\in F\) and positive integer \(k\text{,}\) the \(k\times k\) matrix
    \begin{equation*} J=\begin{bmatrix} \lambda \amp 1\amp 0 \amp \dots \amp 0\\ 0 \amp \lambda\amp 1 \amp \\ \vdots \amp \amp \ddots \amp \ddots \\ \amp \amp \amp \amp 1\\ 0\amp \dots \amp \amp \amp \lambda \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    is called a \(\lambda\)-Jordan block.

    Definition 1.26.2. Jordan basis.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional. A Jordan basis of \(T\) is a basis \(B\) for which \(A=[T]_{B}\) is block diagonal of the form
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix}J_{1}\amp \\ \amp J_{2}\amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \\ \amp \amp \amp J_{r} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    where each \(J_{i}\) is a \(\lambda_i\)-Jordan block for some \(\lambda_i\in F\text{.}\) The matrix \(A\) is called a Jordan form of \(T\) in this case.

    Definition 1.26.3. Cycle of generalized eigenvectors.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional, and let \(\lambda\) be an eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\) A cycle of generalized eigenvectors of \(T\) corresponding to \(\lambda\) is a tuple of the form
    \begin{equation*} \left((T-\lambda I)^{k-1}(\boldv), (T-\lambda I)^{k-2}(\boldv), \dots, (T-\lambda I)(\boldv), \boldv\right) \end{equation*}
    where \(\boldv\ne \boldzero\) and \(k\) is the smallest positive integer satisfying \((T-\lambda I)^{k}(\boldv)=\boldzero\text{.}\) The vector \((T-\lambda I)^{k-1}(\boldv)\) is called the initial vector of the cycle; the vector \(\boldv\) is called the end vector of the cycle.

    Theorem 1.26.4. Jordan blocks.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional.
    1. \([T]_{B}\) is a Jordan block if and only if \(B\) is a cycle of generalized eigenvectors.
    2. \([T]_{B}\) is a Jordan form if and only if \(B\) is a concatenation \(B=B_1*B_2*\cdots * B_r\) of disjoint cycles of generalized eigenvectors.
    3. A cycle of generalized eigenvectors is linearly independent, and its initial vector is an eigenvector.
    4. If \(B_1, B_2, \dots, B_\ell\) are cycles of generalized eigenvectors corresponding to a fixed \(\lambda\) whose initial vectors are linearly independent, then the concatenation \(B_1*B_2*\cdots * B_\ell\) is linearly independent.

    Proof.

    1. Suppose
      \begin{equation*} B=((T-\lambda I)^{k-1}(v),\dots, (T-\lambda I)(v), v) \end{equation*}
      is a cycle of generalized eigenvectors of \(T\) that is a basis of \(V\text{.}\) Let \(N=T-\lambda I\text{,}\) and let
      \begin{equation*} v_j=(T-\lambda I)^j(v)=N^j(v)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      (In other words, \(v_j\) is the \(k-j\)-th element of \(B\text{.}\)) Clearly we have
      \begin{equation*} N(v_j)=v_{j+1} \end{equation*}
      for all \(0\leq j\leq k-2\text{.}\) Since \(B\) is a cycle, we also have \(N(v_{k-1})=\boldzero\text{.}\) Thus, using (1.34), we have
      \begin{equation*} [N]_B=\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 1 \amp 0\amp \cdots \amp 1 \\ 0\amp 0\amp 1 \\ \vdots \amp \amp \ddots \amp \ddots \\ \amp \amp \amp \amp 1 \\ 0\amp \dots \amp \amp \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since the matrix representation operator \([\phantom{A}]_B\) is linear, we have
      \begin{align*} [T]_B \amp =[N+\lambda I]_B\\ \amp =[N]_B+\lambda[I]_B\\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda\amp 1 \amp 0\amp \cdots \amp 1 \\ 0\amp \lambda\amp 1 \\ \vdots \amp \amp \ddots \amp \ddots \amp \\ \amp \amp \amp \amp 1\\ 0\amp \dots \amp \amp \amp \lambda \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{align*}
      The reverse implication (that if \([T]_B\) is a Jordan block, then \(B\) is a cycle of generalized eigenvectors), follows essentially from the exact same observations in reverse.
    2. This follows easily from (1) and the fact that \(\Span B\) is \(T\)-invariant for any cycle of generalized eigenvectors \(B\text{.}\)
    3. This follows from Theorem 1.24.7, where we showed that if \(k\) is the smallest positive integer such that \(T^k(v)=\boldzero\text{,}\) then \((v,T(v),\dots, T^{k-1}(v))\) is linearly independent.
    4. We prove the result by induction on the sum of the lengths
      \begin{equation} n=\len B_1+\len B_2+\cdots +\len B_\ell\text{,}\tag{1.130} \end{equation}
      the base case \(n=1\) being trivial. Assume now that the result is true for any collection cycles of generalized \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors whose lengths sum to \(k< n\text{,}\) and suppose that we have cycles \(B_1,B_2,\dots, B_\ell\) whose initial vectors are linearly independent, and whose lengths satisfy (1.130). Let \(N=T-\lambda I\text{,}\) and for each \(1\leq j\leq \ell\) let
      \begin{equation*} B_j=(v_{1,j},v_{2,j},\dots, v_{n_j,j})\text{.} \end{equation*}
      By definition, this means that
      \begin{equation*} N(v_{i,j})=\begin{cases} v_{i-1,j}\amp \text{if } i\geq 2\\ \boldzero\amp \text{if } i=1 \end{cases}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      If \(\len B_j=1\) for all \(j\text{,}\) then clearly the concatenation is linearly independent. Otherwise, observe that the collection of “shortened” tuples
      \begin{equation*} \widetilde{B}_j=(v_{1,j},v_{2,j},\dots, v_{n_j-1,j}) \end{equation*}
      obtained by deleting (“popping”) the last element of each \(B_j\) of length greater than 1 are cycles with the same linearly independent initial vectors. Since the sum of the lengths of these cycles is less than \(n\text{,}\) the induction hypothesis applies to the \(\widetilde{B_j}\text{:}\) i.e., the concatenation of their elements is linearly independent.
      Now suppose we have a linear combination of the elements of \(B_i\) equal to \(\boldzero\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^\ell \sum_{i=1}^{n_j}a_{ij}v_{i,j}=\boldzero \text{.} \end{equation*}
      Now apply the linear operator \(N\) to both sides of this equation:
      \begin{align*} \sum_{j=1}^\ell \sum_{i=1}^{n_j}a_{ij}N(v_{i,j})\amp =\boldzero \\ \sum_{j=1}^\ell \sum_{i=2}^{n_j}a_{ij}v_{i-1,j}=\boldzero \amp \text{.} \end{align*}
      Note that this is a linear combination of the elements of the shortened cycles \(\widetilde{B_j}\text{.}\) Since their concatenation is linearly independent, we conclude that all of the coefficients \(a_{ij}=0\) with \(1\leq j\leq \ell\text{,}\) \(2\leq i\leq n_j\) are equal to 0. Thus the original linear combination is of the form
      \begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^\ell a_{1,j}v_{1,j}=\boldzero\text{.} \end{equation*}
      By assumption the tuple of initial vectors \((v_{1,j})\) is linearly independent. We conclude that \(a_{1,j}=0\) for all \(j\text{,}\) and thus that the tuple \((v_{i,j})\) is linearly independent, as desired.

    Theorem 1.26.5. Jordan form.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional, and suppose all roots of the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) lie in \(\F\text{.}\)
    1. There is a Jordan basis \(B\) for \(T\text{.}\)
    2. The corresponding Jordan form
      \begin{equation*} A=[T]_B=\begin{bmatrix} J_1\amp \\ \amp J_2\amp \\ \amp \amp\ddots \\ \amp \amp \amp J_r \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
      is unique up to a permutation of the Jordan blocks \(J_{i}\text{.}\)

    Proof of (1).

    We first prove that the existence of a Jordan basis. Let \(V=\oplus_{i=1}^rG(\lambda_i, T)\) be the decomposition of \(V\) into generalized eigenspaces, as per Theorem 1.25.7. Observe that since each \(G(\lambda_i, T)\) is \(T\)-invariant, if we can find Jordan bases \(B_1,B_2,\dots, B_r\) for \(T\vert_{G(\lambda_i, T)}\text{,}\) then the concatenation
    \begin{equation*} B=B_1*B_2*\cdots *B_r \end{equation*}
    is a Jordan basis for \(T\text{.}\) Thus it suffices to prove the result for linear operators whose characteristic polynomial splits as \((x-\lambda)^n\text{.}\)
    We now prove by induction on \(n=\dim V\text{,}\) that if \(T\in\mathcal{L}(V)\) is a linear operator with characteristic polynomial \(f(x)=(x-\lambda)^n\text{,}\) \(\lambda\in F\text{,}\) then \(T\) has a Jordan basis. The base case \(n=1\) is trivial, since any operator on a \(1\)-dimensional space has a matrix representation of the form \([T]_B=[\lambda]\in F^{1,1}\text{,}\) which is clearly a Jordan form.
    Assume now the result applies for any operator on any \(k\)-dimensional vector space, \(k^lt; n\text{.}\) Given \(V\) with \(\dim V=n\) and \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) with characteristic polynomial \(f(x)=(x-\lambda)^n\text{,}\) let \(N=T-\lambda I\text{,}\) and let \(W=\im N\text{.}\) Since \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\text{,}\) \(N\) is non-invertible, and thus \(\im N\ne V\text{.}\) It follows that \(\dim \im N=k< n\text{.}\) Moreover, \(\im N\) is \(N\)- and thus \(T\)-invariant, and since the characteristic polynomial of \(T\vert_{\im N}\) divides \((x-\lambda)^n\text{,}\) we see that \(T\vert_\{\im N\}\) has characteristic polynomial \((x-\lambda)^k\text{.}\) Thus we may apply the induction hypothesis to conclude that \(\im N\) has a Jordan basis that is a concatenation of the cycles of generalized \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors \(B_1,B_2,\dots, B_\ell\text{.}\) Write each \(B_j\) as
    \begin{equation*} B_j=(v_{1,j},\dots, v_{n_j, j})\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and observe that by definition we have
    \begin{equation*} N(v_{i,j})=\begin{cases} v_{i-1,j}\amp \text{if } i\geq 2\\ \boldzero\amp \text{if } i=1 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    for all \(v_{i,j}\text{.}\) Since the end vector \(v_{n_j, j}\) of each cycle \(B_j\) is an element of \(\im N\text{,}\) for all \(1\leq j\leq \ell\) we can find a vector \(v_{n_{j}+1,j}\) satisfying
    \begin{equation*} N(v_{n_{j}+1,j})=v_{n_j,j}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    yielding “lengthened” cycles of generalized \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors
    \begin{equation*} \widetilde{B_j}=(v_{1,j},\dots, v_{n_j, j}, v_{n_j+1, j}) \end{equation*}
    for all \(1\leq j\leq \ell\text{.}\)
    Lastly, since the concatenation of the original cycles \(B_i\) is linearly independent, in particular, the tuple of their initial vectors \((v_{1,j})\) is linearly independent. Since furthermore these elements are elements of \(\NS N\) (\(N(v_{1,j})=\boldzero\)), we can extend this tuple to a basis
    \begin{equation} (v_{1,1},\dots, v_{1,\ell}, w_{\ell+1}, \dots, w_{t})\tag{1.131} \end{equation}
    of \(\NS N\text{.}\) We claim that the concatenation \(B\) of the cycles
    \begin{equation*} \widetilde{B_1}, \widetilde{B_1}, \dots, \widetilde{B_1}, (w_{\ell+1}), (w_{\ell+2}), \dots, (w_t) \end{equation*}
    is our desired Jordan basis of \(T\text{.}\) Since the initial vectors of these cycles is precisely the linearly independent tuple in (1.131), it follows from Theorem 1.26.4 that \(B\) is linearly independent. To prove \(B\) is a basis it suffices to show that \(\len B=\dim V=n\text{.}\) Let \(B'=B_1*B_2*\cdots *B_\ell\text{,}\) the starting Jordan basis of \(\im N\text{.}\) We created \(B\) by adding one extra vector for each of the \(\ell\) cycles \(B_j\text{,}\) as well as the \(r-\ell\) vectors \(w_j\) added to get a basis of the \(r\)-dimensional subspace \(\NS N\text{.}\) Thus we have
    \begin{align*} \len B \amp = \len B'+\ell+r-\ell \\ \amp =\dim \im N+r (\dim \im N=\len B')\\ \amp =\dim \im N+\dim \NS N\\ \amp = n \amp (\text{rank-nullity})\text{,} \end{align*}
    as desired.

    Proof of (2).

    The uniqueness of the Jordan form follows from the fact that for each generalized eigenspace \(W_\lambda=E(\lambda, T)\) the number \(\ell\) of Jordan blocks for \(T\vert_{W_\lambda}\) as well as the list of lengths of these sequences can be expressed solely in terms of \(\dim \NS (T-\lambda I)^j\) for various \(j\in \Z_+\text{,}\) and hence depend solely on \(T\) itself and not on the particular manner of choosing a Jordan basis. Let’s see why this is so.
    Assume \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) has characteristic polynomial \(f(x)=(x-\lambda)^n\text{,}\) let \(N=T-\lambda I\text{,}\) and suppose \(B=B_1*B_2*\cdots *B_\ell\) is a Jordan basis of \(T\text{.}\) Without loss of generality, we may assume that \(\len B_j\geq \len B_{j+1}\) for all \(1\leq j\leq \ell\text{.}\) Since the \(B_j\) are cycles of generalized \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors, we have
    \begin{equation*} B_j=(N^{n_j-1}(v_j),\dots, N(v_j),v_j)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(N^{n_j}(v_j)=\boldzero\) for all \(1\leq j\text{.}\) The cycle structure allows us to easily choose subtuples of the Jordan basis \(B=(N^{i}(v_j))\) that serve as bases of \(\dim \NS N^k\) for various \(k\in \Z_+\text{.}\) Consider \(\NS N\) first. We claim that the tuple
    \begin{equation} (N^{n_1-1}(v_1),N^{n_2-1}(v_2),\dots, N^{n_\ell-1}(v_\ell))\tag{1.132} \end{equation}
    consisting of initial vectors of the \(B_j\) is a basis of \(\NS N\text{.}\) Indeed, given any \(v\in V\text{,}\) since \(B=(N^i(v_j))\) is a basis of \(V\text{,}\) we can write
    \begin{equation*} v=\sum_{j=1}^\ell\sum_{i=0}^{n_j-1} a_{ij}N^{i}(v_j) \end{equation*}
    for some \(a_{ij}\in F\text{,}\) whence
    \begin{align*} v\in \NS N \iff \amp \sum_{j=1}^\ell\sum_{i=0}^{n_j-1}a_{i,j}N(N^{i}(v_j))=\boldzero\\ \amp \iff \sum_{j=1}^\ell\sum_{i=0}^{n_j-1}a_{i,j}N^{i+1}(v_j)=\boldzero \amp \\ \amp \iff \sum_{j=1}^\ell\sum_{i=0}^{n_j-2}a_{i,j}N^{i+1}(v_j)=\boldzero \amp (N^{n_j}(v_j)=\boldzero)\\ \amp \iff a_{i,j}=0 \text{ for } 1\leq j\leq \ell, 1\leq i\leq n_j-2\text{,} \end{align*}
    where the last line follows from linear independence. Thus \(v\in \NS N\) if and only if \(v=\sum_{j=1}^\ell a_{n_j-1, j}N^{n_{j}-1}(v_j)\text{,}\) showing that the tuple in (1.132) spans \(\NS N\text{.}\) Since that tuple is linearly independent, we conclude it is a basis, as claimed.
    A similar argument, replacing \(N\) with \(N^k\) in our computations above, shows that the tuple
    \begin{equation*} (N^{i}(v_j))_{\substack{1\leq j\leq \ell\\ n_j-k+1\leq i\leq n_j}} \end{equation*}
    is a basis of \(\NS N^k\text{.}\) The idea behind these arguments is nicely visualized by arranging the cycles \(B_j\) in vertical columns as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{cccc} B_1 \amp B_2\amp \dots \amp B_\ell\\ \hline N^{n_1-1}(v_1) \amp N^{n_2-1}(v_2) \amp \dots \amp N^{n_\ell-1}(v_\ell)\\ N^{n_1-2}(v_1) \amp N^{n_2-2}(v_2) \amp \amp \vdots \\ N^{n_1-3}(v_1) \amp \vdots \amp \amp v_\ell \\ \vdots \amp v_2\amp \amp \\ v_1 \amp \amp \amp \end{array}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Applying \(N\) to the basis \(B\) moves vectors in the top row to \(\boldzero\) and moves all other vectors up one row. Moreover, from our discussion above, we see that the top \(k\) rows of the array form a basis for \(\NS N^{k}\text{,}\) and thus that the number \(r_k\) of vectors in the \(k\)-th row of the array is given by
    \begin{equation} \dim \NS N^k-\dim \NS N^{k-1}\text{,}\tag{1.133} \end{equation}
    for all \(1\leq k\leq n\text{.}\) This means the precise shape of the array is determined by the values (1.133), which depend only on \(T\text{.}\) Knowing the precise shape of this array tells us in turn the essential details of the Jordan block decomposition. For example, we have
    \begin{equation*} \dim \NS N=r_1=\ell=\# (\text{cycles }B_j)=\#(\text{Jordan blocks})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly, we can deduce the length of each column in our array, and hence \(\len B_j\) (equivalently, the size of the \(j\)-th Jordan block), from the sequence \(r_1,r_2,\dots, r_n\text{.}\) Indeed, we have
    \begin{equation*} \len B_j=\max\{k \colon r_k\geq j\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as one easily verifies. For example, if the sequence is
    \begin{equation*} r_1=5, r_2=4, r_3=2, r_4=2, r_5=0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    then we deduce that
    \begin{equation*} \len B_1=4, \len B_2=4, \len B_3=2, \len B_4=2, \len B_5=1\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Definition 1.26.6. Jordan form.

    Let \(T,S\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional. Assume that the characteristic polynomials of \(T\) and \(S\) both split completely over the base field. We say \(T\) and \(S\) have the same Jordan form if they have Jordan forms that differ by a permutation of the Jordan blocks.

    Procedure 1.26.7. Dot diagram.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional. Fix an eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{,}\) and let \(B=B_{1}*B_{2}*\dots * B_{r}\) be a Jordan basis of \(G(\lambda, T)\text{,}\) where each \(B_{j}\) is a cycle of generalized \(\lambda\)-eigenvectors satisfying \(\len B_j\geq \len B_{j+1}\) for all \(1\leq j\leq r-1\text{.}\) The dot diagram of \(B\) is an staircase pattern of dots constructed in the manner described below. For all \(i\geq 1\text{,}\) let \(r_{i}\) be the number of dots in the \(i\)-th row of the dot diagram.
    • Dots are placed into a rectangular grid containing \(\len B_1\) rows and \(r\) columns. For all \(1\leq i\leq p\text{,}\) the \(i\)-th row contains \(r_{i}\) dots, and these are placed consecutively starting in the leftmost column.
    • We have
      \begin{equation*} r_i=\dim \NS(T-\lambda I)^{i}-\dim\NS(T-\lambda I)^{i-1}. \end{equation*}
      for all \(1\leq i\text{.}\)
    The dot diagram is interpreted as follows.
    • For any \(k\geq 1\text{,}\) the total number of dots in the first \(i\) rows of the diagram is equal to \(\dim \NS (T-\lambda I)^{k}\text{:}\) i.e.,
      \begin{equation*} \dim \NS (T-\lambda I)^k=\sum_{i=1}^kr_i\text{.} \end{equation*}
      In particular, we have
      \begin{equation*} r=r_1=\dim \NS(T-\lambda I)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Thus the number of \(\lambda\)-Jordan blocks of \(T\) is equal to the number of dots in the first row of the diagram, which is equal to \(\dim \NS(T-\lambda I)\text{.}\)
    • For each \(1\leq j\leq r\text{,}\) the number of dots \(c_j\) in the \(j\)-th column of the diagram is equal to the length of \(B_j\text{.}\) Equivalently, \(c_j\) is the size of the \(j\)-th \(\lambda\)-Jordan block.

    Theorem 1.26.8. Minimal polynomial from Jordan form.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite dimensional, and suppose the characteristic polynomial \(f\) of \(T\) factors as
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\prod_{i=1}^r(x-\lambda_i)^{n_i}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\lambda_i\ne \lambda_j\) for \(i\ne j\text{,}\) and \(\lambda_i\in F\text{.}\) Let \(m_T(x)\) be the minimal polynomial of \(T\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation*} m_T(x)=\prod_{i=1}^r(x-\lambda_i)^{m_i}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(m_i\) is the maximum size of a \(\lambda_i\)-Jordan block appearing in a Jordan form for \(T\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(V=\bigoplus_{i=1}^{r}G(\lambda_i, T)\text{,}\) and for each \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) let \(T_i=T\vert_{G(\lambda_i, T)}\) and let \(m_i(x)\) be the minimal polynomial of \(T_i\text{.}\) Since \(m_T(T)=0_V\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} m_i(T)=m_i(T_i)=0_{G(\lambda_i,T)} \end{equation*}
    for all \(i\text{,}\) and hence \(m_i\mid m\) for all \(i\text{;}\) since further, we have \(m_i(x)=(x-\lambda_i)^{m_i}\) for some \(m_i\in \Z\) with \(\lambda_i\ne \lambda_j\text{,}\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \prod_{i=1}^{r}m_i(x)\mid m_T(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Lastly, using a decomposition \(v=w_1+w_2+\cdots +w_r\text{,}\) with \(w_r\in G(\lambda_i, T)\text{,}\) we see easily that
    \begin{equation*} \prod_{i=1}^{r}m_i(T)v=\sum_{i=1}^{r}\prod_{i=1}^{r}m_i(T)w_i=\boldzero \text{,} \end{equation*}
    and hence that \(m_T(x)\mid \prod_{i=1}^{r}m_i(x)\text{.}\) Since \(m_T(x)\) and \(\prod_{i=1}^{r}m_i(x)\) are both monic, and are each a factor of the other, it follows that \(m_T(x)=\prod_{i=1}^{r}m_i(x)\text{.}\) In other words, the minimal polynomial of \(T\) is the product of the minimal polynomials of the restrictions \(T\vert_{G(\lambda_i, T)}\text{.}\)
    Since we compute a Jordan form of \(T\) be computing Jordan forms for each restriction \(T\vert_{G(\lambda_i, T)}\text{,}\) it now suffices to prove the claim for \(T\vert_{G(\lambda_i, T)}\text{.}\) Thus without loss of generality, we may assume that \(T\) has characteristic polynomial \(f(x)=(x-\lambda)^n\text{.}\) Let \(B\) be a Jordan basis of \(T\text{,}\) and let
    \begin{equation*} A=[T]_B=\begin{bmatrix} J_1\amp 0 \amp \dots \amp \amp 0 \\ 0\amp J_2\amp 0 \\ \vdots \amp \amp \vdots \amp \\ 0\amp \dots \amp \amp \amp J_\ell \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    be the corresponding Jordan form of \(T\text{,}\) where each \(J_i\) is a \(\lambda\)-Jordan block of dimension \(k_i\times k_i\text{.}\) Since each \(J_i\) has characteristic polynomial \((x-\lambda)^{k_i}\text{,}\) we see that the minimal polynomial of \(J_i\) is of the form \((x-\lambda)^{j_i}\) for some \(j_i\leq k_i\text{.}\) An easy proof by induction now shows that
    \begin{equation*} (J_i-\lambda I)^\ell=\boldzero \iff \ell \geq k_i\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, in general the minimal polynomial of a \(\lambda\)-Jordan block of dimension \(k\times k\) is \((x-\lambda)^k\text{,}\) as is easily shown. Lastly, from the block diagonal description of \(A=[T]_B\text{,}\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} g(A)=\begin{bmatrix} g(J_1)\amp 0 \amp \dots \amp 0 \\ 0\amp g(J_2)\amp 0 \\ \vdots \amp \amp \vdots \amp \\ 0\amp \dots \amp \amp \amp g(J_k) \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    for any polynomial \(g\in P(F)\text{.}\) We conclude that
    \begin{align*} g(A)=\boldzero \amp \iff g(J_i)=\boldzero \text{ for all } 1\leq i\leq \ell\\ \amp \iff (x-\lambda)^{k_j}\mid g(x) \text{ for all } 1\leq i\leq \ell\\ \amp \iff (x-\lambda)^m\mid g(x) \text{ where } m=\max\{k_j\}\text{.} \end{align*}
    It follows that the minimal polynomial of \(A\text{,}\) and hence of \(T\) is \((x-\lambda)^m\text{,}\) where \(m\) is the maximum size of the Jordan blocks \(J_i\text{,}\) as desired.

    Theorem 1.26.9. Similarity.

    Let \(A, A'\in \F^{n,n}\) and suppose both characteristic polynomials factor completely over \(\F\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(A\) and \(A'\) are similar (i.e. \(A'=P^{-1}AP\) for some invertible \(P\)).
    2. \(T_A\) and \(T_A'\) have the same Jordan form.

    Proof.

    Assume \(A\) and \(A'\) are similar, and thus that there is an invertible matrix \(Q\) such that \(A=Q^{-1}A'Q\text{.}\) Let \(J\) be a Jordan form for \(T_A\text{.}\) By definition, there is a basis \(B'\) such that
    \begin{equation*} [T_A]_{B'}=J\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Let \(B\) be the standard basis, and let \(P=\underset{B'\to B}{P}\text{.}\) Using the change of basis formula, we have
    \begin{equation*} J=P^{-1}AP\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and thus
    \begin{equation*} J=P^{-1}Q^{-1}A'QP=(QP)^{-1}A'(QP)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Equivalently, letting \(B''\) be the basis consisting of the columns of \(QP\text{,}\) we see that
    \begin{equation*} J=[T_{A'}]_{B''}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(A\) and \(A'\) have the same Jordan form.
    Now assume \(A\) and \(A'\) have the same Jordan form \(J\text{.}\) Reasoning as above, this implies that there are invertible matrices \(P\) and \(Q\) such that
    \begin{equation*} J=P^{-1}AP=Q^{-1}A'Q\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It follows that
    \begin{equation*} A=PQ^{-1}A'QP^{-1}=(QP^{-1})^{-1}A'(QP^{-1})\text{,} \end{equation*}
    showing that \(A\) and \(A'\) are similar.

    Theorem 1.26.10. Diagonal plus nilpotent.

    Let \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) where \(V\) is finite-dimensional, and assume the characteristic polynomial of \(T\) splits completely over the base field. There are operators \(S,N\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) satisfying the following conditions:
    • \(T=S+N\text{;}\)
    • \(S\) is diagonalizable;
    • \(N\) is nilpotent (i.e., \(N^k=0_V\) for some \(k\in \Z_+\)).
    In other words \(T\) is the sum of a diagonalizable and a nilpotent operator.

    Proof.

    Let \(B\) be a Jordan basis of \(T\text{,}\) and let
    \begin{equation*} J=[T]_B=\begin{bmatrix}J_{1}\amp \\ \amp J_{2}\amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \\ \amp \amp \amp J_{\ell} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    be its corresponding Jordan form. It is not difficult to see that we can write
    \begin{equation*} J=D+M\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(D\) is diagonal and \(M\) is upper triangular with zeros along the diagonal. Moreover, this implies that \(M^k=\boldzero\) for some \(k\in \Z\text{:}\) i.e., any such \(M\) is nilpotent. Since the matrix representation operator \([\phantom{A}]_B\) is an isomorphism of \(\mathcal{L}(V)\) to \(F^{n,n}\text{,}\) \(n=\dim V\text{,}\) there are operators \(S, N\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) satisfying
    \begin{equation*} [S]_B=D, [N]_B=M\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Furthermore, since \(D\) is diagonal and \(M\) is a nilpotent matrix, it follows that \(S\) is diagonalizable and \(N\) is a nilpotent operator. Lastly, since
    \begin{equation*} [S+N]_B=[S]_B+[N]_B=D+M=J=[T]_B\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and since \([\phantom{A}]_B\) is bijective, it follows that \(T=S+N\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.26.11. Square-roots of complex matrices.

    Let \(A\in \C^{n,n}\) be an invertible matrix. There is a matrix \(B\) such that \(B^2=A\text{.}\) In other words, every invertible complex matrix has a square root.
    It follows that any invertible operator on a finite-dimensional complex vector space (\(F=\C\)) has a square-root.

    Proof.

    From calculus we learn that the infinite series
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=0}^\infty {1/2\choose i}x^i=1+\frac{1}{2}x-\frac{1}{8}x^2+\frac{1}{16}x^3-\cdots \end{equation*}
    converges to \(\sqrt{1+x}\) for all \(x\in (-1,1)\text{.}\) It follows from power series theory that for all \(N\text{,}\) the truncated series
    \begin{equation*} f_m(x)=\sum_{i=0}^m{1/2\choose i}x^i \end{equation*}
    satisfies
    \begin{equation*} f_m(x)f_m(x)=1+x+x^{m+1}h_m(x) \end{equation*}
    for some polynomial \(h_m(x)\text{.}\) Thus if \(A=I+N\) where \(N\) is a nilpotent matrix satisfying
    \begin{equation*} N^{m+1}=\boldzero\text{,} \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} f_m(N)f_m(N)=I+N+N^{m+1}h_m(N)=I+N, \end{equation*}
    since \(N^{m+1}=\boldzero\text{.}\) This proves that any matrix of the form \(I+N\) has a square root \(B\text{,}\) and that in fact we may take \(B=f_m(N)\) for \(m\) sufficiently large.
    Now assume \(A\) is invertible. From Theorem 1.26.10 and Jordan theory, we can write \(A=D+N\text{,}\) where \(D\) is diagonal and \(N\) is nilpotent, and furthermore the diagonal entries of \(D\) are the eigenvalues of \(A\text{,}\) which are all nonzero. It follows that \(D\) is diagonal. Write \(A=D(I+D^{-1}N)\text{.}\) Since \(D^{-1}\) is diagonal, it commutes with all matrices, and it follows that \(D^{-1}N\) is also nilpotent. By the previous paragraph there is a square-root \(B\) of \(I+D^{-1}N\text{.}\) Let \(E\) be a diagonal square-root of \(D\) (it is easy to see such a matrix exists), and let \(C=EB\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{align*} C^2 \amp =EBEB\\ \amp =EEBB \amp (E \text{ diagonal})\\ \amp =E^2B^2\\ \amp =D(I+D^{-1}N)\\ \amp =A\text{.} \end{align*}
