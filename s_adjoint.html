    Section 1.21 Adjoints

    Subsection Adjoints

    Definition 1.21.1. Adjoint of transformation.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,}_V)\) and \((W,\angvec{\, ,}_W)\) be inner product spaces. Given \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) we call a function \(T^*\colon W\rightarrow V\) an adjoint of \(T\) if it satisfies
    \begin{align} \angvec{T(v),w}_W \amp =\angvec{v,T^*(w)}_V\tag{1.84}\\ \angvec{T^*(w),v}_V \amp =\angvec{w,T(v)}_V\tag{1.85} \end{align}
    for all \(v\in V\) and \(w\in W\text{.}\) Furthermore, we call each of the equivalent properties (1.84)–(1.85) the adjoint property.

    Remark 1.21.2. Adjoint properties.

    To see that properties (1.84)–(1.85) are equivalent, note that
    \begin{align*} \angvec{v,T^*(w)}_V \amp =\angvec{T(v),w}_W\\ \amp \iff \overline{\angvec{v,T^*(w)}}_V=\overline{\angvec{T(v),w}}_W\\ \amp \iff \angvec{T^*(w),v}_V=\angvec{w,T(v)}_W \amp (\text{conj. sym.})\text{.} \end{align*}

    Convention 1.21.3. Adjoints.

    As is evident in the statement of Definition 1.21.1, discussion of adjoint transformations generally involves two different inner product spaces with two different inner products. Although we will be careful to use subscript notation in statements of definitions or theory, henceforth we will drop the subscripts on \(\angvec{\, ,}_V\) and \(\angvec{\, ,}_W\) when constructing proofs or computing examples. The reader can easily determine which inner product is meant by determining which space (\(V\) or \(W\)) the input vectors are elements of.
    As shown in in Theorem 1.21.19, \(T^*\) is guaranteed to exist if \(\dim V< \infty\text{.}\) However, it is not true in general that every linear transformation of inner product spaces has an adjoint.

    Example 1.21.4. Nonexistence of an adjoint.

    Consider the vector space \(C^\infty([0,1])\) together with the integral inner product
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{f,g}=\int_0^1f(x)g(x)\, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The linear operator \(T\colon C^\infty([0,1])\rightarrow C^\infty([0,1])\) defined as \(T(f)=f'\) does not have an adjoint.
    Solution.
    This is left as a homework exercise. It is worth noting that if we restrict \(T\) to certain \(T\)-invariant subspaces \(W\) of \(C([0,1])\text{,}\) the resulting linear transformation \(T\vert_W\colon W\rightarrow W\) does indeed have an adjoint. For example, we may take the space of polynomial functions
    \begin{equation*} W=P([0,1])\text{,} \end{equation*}
    or the trigonometric polynomial space
    \begin{equation*} W=\Span(1,\cos 2\pi x, \sin 2\pi x, \cos 3\pi x, \sin 3\pi x,\dots )\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.21.5. Adjoint properties.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, }_V)\text{,}\) \((W,\angvec{\, }_W)\text{,}\) and \((U,\angvec{\, ,}_U)\) be inner product spaces, Let \(T,T_1,T_2\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) and let \(S\in \mathcal{L}(W,U)\text{.}\)
    1. There is at most one adjoint of \(T\text{:}\) i.e., if there exists a function \(T^*\) satisfying the adjoint property, then it is unique.
    2. An adjoint of \(T\) is linear: i.e., if \(T^*\) exists, then \(T^*\in \mathcal{L}(W,V)\)
    3. If \(T_1^*\) and \(T_2^*\) exist, then so does \((cT_1+dT_2)^*\) for any scalars \(c,d\in F\text{.}\) Moreover, in this case, we have
      \begin{equation} (cT_1+dT_2)^*=\overline{c}T_1^*+\overline{d}T_2^*\text{.}\tag{1.86} \end{equation}
    4. If \(T^*\) exists, then so does \((T^*)^*\text{.}\) Moreover, in this case we have
      \begin{equation} (T^*)^*=T\text{.}\tag{1.87} \end{equation}
    5. If \(T^*\) and \(S^*\) exist, then so does \((ST)^*\text{.}\) Moreover, in this case we have
      \begin{equation} (ST)^*=T^*S^*\text{.}\tag{1.88} \end{equation}
    6. Given a scalar \(c\in F\text{,}\) let \(c_V\colon V\rightarrow V\) be the dilation map defined as \(c_V(v)=cv\text{.}\) We have
      \begin{equation} (c_V)^*=\overline{c}_V\text{.}\tag{1.89} \end{equation}
      As a consequence, since \(I_V=1_V\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation} (I_V)^*=I_V\text{.}\tag{1.90} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    1. Suppose \(T^*\colon W\rightarrow V\) and \(S\colon W\rightarrow V\) are adjoint functions of \(T\text{:}\) i.e., \(T^*\) and \(S\) both satisfy (1.84). It follows that for all \(v\in V\) and \(w\in W\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \angvec{v,S(w)-T^*(w)} \amp =\angvec{v,S(w)}-\angvec{v,T^*(w)} \\ \amp = \angvec{T(v),w}-\angvec{T(v),w} \amp (S, T^* \text{ adj. of } T) \\ \amp = 0\text{.} \end{align*}
      Since \(\angvec{v,S(w)-T^*(w)}=0\) for all \(v\text{,}\) it follows from Theorem 1.18.9 that \(S(w)-T^*(w)=0\) for all \(w\in W\text{,}\) and hence that \(S=T^*\text{,}\) as desired.
    2. Assume an adjoint \(T^*\colon V\rightarrow W\) exists. We use the 1-step technique to show \(T^*\) is linear. Given any \(w_1,w_2\in W\) and \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) we will show that \(T^*(cw_1+dw_2)=cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2)\) by proving that
      \begin{equation*} \angvec{T^*(cw_1+dw_2),v}=\angvec{cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2),v} \end{equation*}
      for all \(v\in V\text{,}\) using Theorem 1.18.9. To this end, we have
      \begin{align*} \angvec{T^*(cw_1+dw_2),v} \amp = \angvec{cw_1+dw_2,T(v)}\\ \amp = c\angvec{w_1,T(v)}+d\angvec{w_2,T(v)}\\ \amp = c\angvec{T^*(w_1),v}+d\angvec{T^*(w_2),v}\\ \amp =\angvec{cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2),v} \end{align*}
      for all \(v\text{.}\) Thus \(T^*(cw_1+dw_2)=cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2)\text{,}\) as desired.
    3. This is left as an exercise.
    4. Assume \(T^*\) and \(S^*\) exists. By uniqueness of adjoints, it suffices to show that \(T^*S^*\) satisfies the adjoint property. We have
      \begin{align*} \angvec{v,T^*S^*(u)} \amp = \angvec{v,T^*(S^*(u))} \\ \amp =\angvec{S(v),T^*(u)}\\ \amp =\angvec{T(S(v)),u}\\ \amp = \angvec{TS(v),u} \end{align*}
      for all \(v\in V\) and \(u\in U\text{.}\) Since this property uniquely defines the adjoint \((TS)^*\text{,}\) we conclude that \((TS)^*=S^*T^*\text{.}\)
    5. Assume \(T^*\) exists. From (2) we know that \(T^*\) is linear. That \(T\) is its adjoint follows now from the equivalence of the two properties (1.84)–(1.85):i.e., we can think of the first as saying that \(T^*\) is an adjoint of \(T\text{,}\) and the second as saying that \(T\) is an adjoint of \(T^*\text{.}\)
    6. This is left as an exercise.
    Before getting to our existence result, we consider a few examples where we can determine an adjoint map explicitly. For our first example of computing an adjoint, we consider a linear transformation \(T\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\text{.}\) Recall that \(T=T_A\) for a unique matrix \(A\in F^{m,n}\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.21.19, there is an adjoint transformation \(T^*\colon F^{m}\rightarrow F^{n}\text{.}\) What is the matrix \(A'\) such that \(T^*=T_{A'}\text{?}\) This is answered in Theorem 1.21.9, which makes use of the following definition.

    Definition 1.21.6. Conjugate and adjoint of a matrix.

    Let \(F\in \{\R,\C\}\text{,}\) and let \(A=[a_{ij}]\in F^{m,n}\text{.}\)
    The conjugate of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\overline{A}\) is the \(m\times n\) matrix defined as
    \begin{equation*} \overline{A}=[\overline{a_{ij}}]\in F^{m,n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The adjoint of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(A^*\text{,}\) is the \(n\times m\) matrix defined as
    \begin{equation*} A^*=(\overline{A})^T=\overline{(A^T)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Remark 1.21.7. Adjoint matrix.

    Thanks to the definition \(A^*=(\overline{A})^T=\overline{(A^T)}\text{,}\) many elementary properties of the adjoint operation follow from related properties enjoyed by the transpose operator, with complex conjugation intervening to spice things up. We leave it to the reader to prove that the following properties for matrices \(A,B\) (of appropriate size) and scalars \(c,d\text{:}\)
    \begin{align} \overline{cA+dB} \amp =\overline{c}\overline{A}+\overline{d}\overline{B} \tag{1.91}\\ (cA+dB)^* \amp =\overline{c}A^*+\overline{d}B^*\tag{1.92}\\ (AB)^* \amp =B^*A^*\tag{1.93}\\ (A^*)^* \amp =A\text{.}\tag{1.94} \end{align}

    Theorem 1.21.8. Standard inner product.

    Let \(F\in \{\R, \C\}\text{,}\) and let \(\angvec{\, ,}\) be the standard inner product on \(F^n\) as defined in Definition 1.18.4. For all \(\boldx, \boldy\in F^n\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation} \angvec{\boldx,\boldy}=\boldy^*\,\boldx\text{.}\tag{1.95} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    Let \(\boldx=(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) and \(\boldy=(y_1,y_2,\dots, y_n)\text{.}\) Treating \(\boldx\) and \(\boldy\) as column vectors (Fiat 1.4.8), we have
    \begin{align*} \boldy^*\,\boldx\amp =\rowvec{\overline{y_1}, \dots, \overline{y_n}}\,\colvec{x_1\\ \vdots \\ x_n} \\ \amp = \sum_{i=1}^n\overline{y_i}x_i\\ \amp = \sum_{i=1}^nx_i\overline{y_i}\\ \amp =\angvec{\boldx, \boldy}\text{.} \end{align*}
    The next result tells us that adjoints of matrix transformations \(T_A\) exist, and that in fact the adjoint of \(T_A\) is the matrix transformation \(T_{A^*}\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.21.9. Adjoint of a matrix transformation.

    Assume \(F\in \{\R,\C\}\text{,}\) and let \(A\in F^{m,n}\text{.}\) For all \(\boldx\in F^n\) and \(\boldy\in F^m\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align} \angvec{A\boldx,\boldy}_{F^m} \amp = \angvec{\boldx,A^*\boldy}_{F^n}\tag{1.96}\\ \angvec{A^*\boldx,\boldy}_{F^n} \amp =\angvec{\boldx, A\boldy}_{F^m}\text{,}\tag{1.97} \end{align}
    where \(\angvec{\, ,}_{F^n}\) and \(\angvec{\, ,}_{F^m}\) denote the standard inner products on \(F^n\) and \(F^m\text{,}\) respectively.
    As a consequence, given a matrix transformation \(T_A\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation} (T_A)^*=T_{A^*}\text{,}\tag{1.98} \end{equation}
    where the adjoint is taken with respect to the standard inner products on \(F^n\) and \(F^m\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Equation (1.98) follows directly from equations (1.96)–(1.97), as these are precisely the adjoint properties for the matrix transformation \(T_A\text{.}\) We prove (1.96) (which is equivalent to (1.97)):
    \begin{align*} \angvec{\boldx,A^*\boldy} \amp = (A^*\boldy)^*\boldx \amp \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq_standard_inner_product_matrix.html}{\text{(1.95)}}\\ \amp =\boldy^* (A^*)^*\boldx \amp \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq_adjoint_prod.html}{\text{(1.93)}} \\ \amp = \boldy^* A\boldx \amp \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq_adj_adj_matrix.html}{\text{(1.94)}}\\ \amp = \angvec{A\boldx, \boldy} \amp \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq_standard_inner_product_matrix.html}{\text{(1.95)}}\text{.} \end{align*}

    Remark 1.21.10. Adjoint of real matrix transformation.

    As usual, observe that if \(F=\R\) complex conjugation operation can be disregarded. Let’s give a quick summary of what the previous definitions and theorems boil down to in this setting.
    • Given \(A\in \R^{m,n}\text{,}\) we have \(A^*=A^T\text{.}\)
    • Given \(\boldx, \boldy\in \R^n\text{,}\) we have \(\boldx\cdot \boldy=\boldx^T\boldy\text{.}\)
    • Given \(T_A\colon \R^n\rightarrow \R^m\text{,}\) we have \(T_A^*=T_{A^T}\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.21.11. Matrix representation of adjoint.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,}_V)\) be an inner product space of finite dimension \(n\text{,}\) and let \(B\) be an orthonormal basis of \(V\text{.}\) Given \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V)\) and adjoint \(T^*\in\mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation} [T^*]_{B}=\left([T]_B\right)^*\text{.}\tag{1.99} \end{equation}
    More generally, given \(S\in \mathcal{L}(V)\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation} S=T^* \iff [S]_B=\left([T]_B\right)^*\text{.}\tag{1.100} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    It suffices to show the equivalence (1.100). The key ingredients to the proof are summarized as follows:
    1. Since \(B\) is an orthormal basis, we have
      \begin{align*} \angvec{v,v'}_V \amp =\angvec{[v]_B,[v']_B]}\text{,} \end{align*}
      where the inner products on the right is the standard one on \(F^{n}\text{.}\)
    2. Matrices \(A,C\in F^{m,n}\) satisfy
      \begin{equation*} \angvec{A\boldx,\boldy}=\angvec{\boldx, C\boldy} \end{equation*}
      for all \(\boldx\in F^{n}\) and \(\boldy\in F^{m}\) if and only if \(C=A^*\text{.}\) This follows from Theorem 1.21.9 and uniqueness of the adjoint (if it exists).
    We now prove the equivalence:
    \begin{align*} S=T^* \amp \iff \angvec{T(v),w}=\angvec{v,S(w)} \text{ for all } v,w\in V \hspace{10pt} (\text{def. of adj.})\\ \amp \iff \angvec{[T(v)]_B,[w]_{B}}=\angvec{[v]_B,[S(w)]_B} \text{ for all } v,w\in V \hspace{10pt} (\text{by (i) above}) \\ \amp \iff \angvec{[T]_B[v]_{B},[w]_{B}}=\angvec{[v]_B,[S]_{B}[w]_B} \text{ for all } v,w\in V \hspace{10pt} \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq_matrixrep_prop.html}{\text{(1.35)}}\\ \amp \iff \angvec{[T]_B\,\boldx,\boldy}=\angvec{\boldx,[S]_{B}\,\boldy} \text{ for all } \boldx,\boldy\in F^n\hspace{10pt} ([\phantom{v}]_B \text{ an isom.}) \\ \amp \iff [S]_B=\left([T]_B\right)^* \hspace{10pt} (\text{by (ii) above}) \text{.} \end{align*}

    Warning 1.21.12. Matrix representation of adjoint.

    It is absolutely essential that the basis in \(B\) in Corollary 1.21.11 is orthonormal. As an example of what can go wrong when this condition is dropped, consider \(T=T_A\colon \C^2\rightarrow \C^2 \text{,}\) where \(A=\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp i\\ 0\amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) According to Theorem 1.21.9, we have
    \begin{equation*} T_A^*=T_{A^*}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where the adjoint is taken with respect to the standard inner product on \(\C^2\text{.}\) Of course, if we pick \(B\) to be the standard basis of \(\C^2\text{,}\) which is orthonormal with respect to this inner product, we have
    \begin{align*} [T]_B \amp = A=\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp i\\ 0\amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \\ [T^*]_B\amp =[T_{A^*}]_B=A^*=\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp 0 \\ -i\amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{align*}
    and we see that \([T^*]_B=[T]_B^*\text{,}\) as predicted by Corollary 1.21.11. Now consider the non-orthonormal basis \(B'=((1,0),(1,1))\text{.}\) We readily compute
    \begin{align*} [T]_{B'} \amp =\begin{bmatrix} 0\amp i\\ 0\amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \\ [T^*]_{B'} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} -i\amp -i \\ i\amp i \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{align*}
    and observe that
    \begin{equation*} [T]_{B'}^*\ne [T^*]_{B'} \end{equation*}
    in this case.

    Remark 1.21.13. Matrix representation of adjoint.

    Not surprisingly, a more general version of Corollary 1.21.11 holds for linear operators \(T\colon V\rightarrow W\) between two different finite-dimensional inner product spaces. However, in that setting we need to choose two bases \(B\) and \(B'\text{,}\) and consider the matrix represetations \([T]_B^{B'}\) and \([T^*]_{B'}^B\text{,}\) resulting in some thorny notation. . I thought the generalized result was not interesting enough to justify this notational.
    It is also worth noting that the equivalence (1.100) in Corollary 1.21.11 implies the existence of adjoints in the setting it specified. However, Theorem 1.21.19 guarantees the existence of adjoints in a much more general setting.
    We now consider some more exotic/abstract examples.

    Example 1.21.14. Adjoint of inclusion map.

    Let \(W\) be a finite-dimensional subspace of the inner product space \((V,\angvec{\, })\text{,}\) and let \(i\colon W\rightarrow V\) be the inclusion map. Given an explicit description of the adjoint map \(i^*\colon V\rightarrow W\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Since \(W\) is finite dimensional, we have \(V=W\oplus W^\perp\text{,}\) and can define the orthogonal projection map \(\proj_W\colon V\rightarrow W\text{.}\) We claim that \(i^*=\proj_W\text{,}\) and prove our claim by showing that
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{i(w'),v}=\angvec{w,\proj_W(v)} \end{equation*}
    for all \(w'\in W\) and \(v\in V\text{.}\) Given any \(v\in V\text{,}\) we write \(v=w+w^\perp\text{,}\) where by definition \(w=\proj_W(v)\in W\) and \(w^\perp\in W^\perp\text{.}\) It follows that for any \(w'\in W\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{i(w'),v} \amp =\angvec{w',v}\\ \amp = \angvec{w',w+w^\perp}\\ \amp =\angvec{w',w}+\angvec{w',w^\perp}\\ \amp =\angvec{w',w} \amp (\angvec{w',w^\perp}=0)\\ \amp = \angvec{w',\proj_W(v)}\text{,} \end{align*}
    as desired.

    Example 1.21.15. Derivative operator.

    Let \(W\) be the set of functions \(C^\infty(\R)\) of period 1. The operation
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{f,g}=\int_0^1f(x)g(x)\, dx \end{equation*}
    defines an inner product on \(W\text{.}\) It is easy to show that if \(f\in W\text{,}\) then \(f'\in W\text{:}\) i.e., the derivative of a \(C^\infty\) period-1 function is a \(C^\infty\) period-1 function. Thus we can define a linear transformation
    \begin{align*} T\colon W \amp \rightarrow W\\ f \amp \mapsto f'\text{.} \end{align*}
    Show that \(T^*=-T\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Observe that \(-T(g)=-g'\text{,}\) by definition. We must show that \(-T\) satisfies the adjoint property with respect to \(T\text{.}\) Given any \(f,g\in W\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{T(f),g} \amp = \int_0^1f'(x)g(x)\, dx\\ \amp = f(x)g(x)\Bigr\vert_0^1-\int_0^1f(x)g'(x)\, dx \amp (\text{int. by parts.})\\ \amp = 0+\int_0^1f(x)(-g'(x))\, dx \amp (f(0)=f(1), g(0)=g(1))\\ \amp = \angvec{f,-T(g)} \amp (-T(g)=-g')\text{.} \end{align*}

    Example 1.21.16. Integral transformation.

    Let \(I=[a,b]\) be a closed interval in \(\R\text{.}\) Let
    \begin{equation*} K\colon I\times I\rightarrow \R\text{,} \end{equation*}
    be a continuous function. Given any \(f\in C([a,b])\text{,}\) you can show that the integral function
    \begin{equation*} F(x)=\int_a^bF(x,y)f(y)\, dy \end{equation*}
    is continuous on \([a,b]\) and that the resulting map
    \begin{align*} T\colon C([a,b]) \amp\rightarrow C([a,b]) \\ f \amp \mapsto F(x)=\int_a^bK(x,t)f(t)\, dt \end{align*}
    is a linear transformation. Show, using Fubini’s theorem for double integrals, that the adjoint of \(T\) is the function
    \begin{align*} T^*\colon C([a,b]) \amp \rightarrow C([a,b])\\ g \amp \mapsto G(x)=\int_a^b F(s,x)g(s)\, ds \text{.} \end{align*}
    Solution.
    This is left as an exercise.

    Subsection Existence of adjoints

    Definition 1.21.17. Linear functional.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space. A linear functional on \(V\) is a linear transformation \(\phi\colon V\rightarrow F\) from \(V\) to \(F\text{.}\) The vector space \(\mathcal{L}(V,F)\) of all linear functionals on \(V\) is called the dual space of \(V\text{,}\) and is denoted \(V^*\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.21.18. Riesz representation.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space.
    1. For all \(v_0\in V\text{,}\) the function \(\phi_{v_0}\colon V\rightarrow F\) defined as \(\phi_{v_0}(v)=\angvec{v,v_0}\) is a linear functional.
    2. The function
      \begin{align*} \Phi\colon V \amp \rightarrow V^*=\mathcal{L}(V,F)\\ v \amp \mapsto \phi_v \text{,} \end{align*}
      where \(\phi_v(v')=\angvec{v',v}\) is injective and satisfies
      \begin{equation*} \Phi(cv_1+dv_2)=\overline{c}v_1+\overline{d}v_2\text{.} \end{equation*}
      In other words, \(\Phi\) is conjugate linear.
    3. Assume \(\dim V< \infty\text{.}\) In this case the map \(\Phi\) above is bijective. In particular, given a linear functional \(\phi\colon V\rightarrow F\text{,}\) there is a unique vector \(v_0\in V\) such that \(\phi=\phi_{v_0}\text{:}\) i.e., such that \(\phi(v)=\angvec{v,v_0}\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. This was already proved in Theorem 1.18.9. The current statement is just a slight reformulation of this fact, using the language of linear functionals.
    2. We use the 1-step technique. Given any \(v_1,v_2\in V\) and \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) the function \(\Phi(cv_1+dv_2)=\phi_{cv_1+dv_2}\) satisfies
      \begin{align*} \phi_{cv_1+dv_2}(v) \amp = \angvec{v,cv_1+dv+2} \\ \amp = \overline{c}\angvec{v,v_1}+\overline{d}\angvec{v,v_2}\\ \amp = \overline{c}\phi_{v_1}(v)+\overline{d}\phi_{v_2}(v)\\ \amp =(\overline{c}\phi_{v_1}+\overline{d}\phi_{v_2})(v) \end{align*}
      for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) Thus \(\phi_{cv_1+dv_2}=\overline{c}\phi_{v_1}+\overline{d}\phi_{v_2}\text{:}\) i.e., \(\Phi(cv_1+dv_2)=\overline{c}\Phi(v_1)+\overline{d}\Phi(v_2)\text{.}\)
      We now show \(\Phi\) is injective. Given \(v_1,v_2\in V\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \Phi(v_1)=\Phi(v_2) \amp \iff \phi_{v_1}=\phi_{v_2}\\ \amp \iff \phi_{v_1}(v)=\phi_{v_2}(v) \text{ for all } v\in V\\ \amp \iff \angvec{v,v_1}=\angvec{v,v_2} \text{ for all } v\in V\\ \amp \iff v_1=v_2 \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/th_inner_prod.html}{\text{1.18.9}})\text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus \(\Phi\) is injective.
    3. Let \(\dim V=n< \infty\text{.}\) Since \(\dim F=1\text{,}\) by Theorem 1.13.19 we have
      \begin{equation*} \dim V^*=\dim \mathcal{L}(V,F)=n\cdot 1=n=\dim V\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since \(\Phi\) is conjugate linear, and not linear, we cannot invoke Corollary 1.11.4 directly to conclude that it is surjective. However, the same result does in fact apply to conjugate linear maps for the following two reasons (left to the reader to prove):
      • the image of a conjugate linear map is a subspace of the codomain (just as with linear maps);
      • the image of a linearly independent tuple under an injective conjugate linear map is a linearly independent tuple.
      Let \(B=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_n)\) be a basis of \(V\text{.}\) From the two points above, we see that \(\im \Phi\) is a subspace of \(V^*\) containing the linearly independent tuple \(\Phi(B)=(\Phi(v_1),\dots, \Phi(v_n))\text{.}\) It follows that \(\dim \im \Phi=\dim V^*=n\text{,}\) and thus that \(\im \Phi=V^*\text{,}\) as desired.
      Alternatively, we can give a more constructive proof of the surjectivity of \(\Phi\) as follows:
      • pick an orthonormal basis \(B=(v_1,v_2,\dots, v_n)\) of \(V\text{;}\)
      • given \(\phi\in V^*=\mathcal{L}(V,F)\text{,}\) let \(c_i=\phi(v_i)\) for all \(i\in I\text{;}\)
      • let \(v=\sum_{i=1}^n\overline{c_i}v_i\text{;}\)
      • for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{,}\) we have
        \begin{align*} \phi_v(v_i) \amp =\angvec{v_i, v}\\ \amp = \angvec{v_i, \overline{c}_iv_i} \amp (i\ne j\implies \angvec{v_i, c_jv_j}=0)\\ \amp = c_i\angvec{v_i,v_i} \amp (\text{conj. homog.})\\ \amp =c_i \amp (\norm{v_i}=1)\text{;} \end{align*}
      • since \(\phi\) and \(\phi_v\) agree on the basis \(B\text{,}\) we have \(\phi=\phi_v\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.21.19. Adjoint of a transformation.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,}_V)\) and \((W,\angvec{\, ,}_W)\) be inner product spaces, and assume \(V\) is finite dimensional. Every linear transformation \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) has a unique adjoint \(T^*\in \mathcal{L}(W,V)\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    First we prove the existence of \(T^*\) by defining it explicitly via a somewhat involved recipe. Given any \(w\in W\text{,}\) the function
    \begin{equation*} f_w(v)=\angvec{T(v),w} \end{equation*}
    is easily seen to be a linear functional on \(V\text{:}\) i.e., \(f_w\in V^*=\mathcal{L}(V,F)\text{.}\) By Riesz representation there is a unique vector \(v_w\in V\) satisfying \(f_w(v)=\angvec{v,v_w}\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) We define \(T^*(w)=v_0\text{.}\)
    Having defined \(T^*\colon W\rightarrow V\) as a function,, we now show that it satisfies equation (1.84); equation (1.85) then follows immediately from the conjugate symmetry property. Given any \(v,w\in V\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{T(v),w} \amp = f_w(v) \amp (f_w(v)=\angvec{T(v),w})\\ \amp =\angvec{v,v_w} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/th_Riesz_rep.html}{\text{1.21.18}})\\ \amp =\angvec{v,T^*(w)} \amp (\text{def. } T^*) \text{,} \end{align*}
    as desired.
    The uniqueness and linearity of \(T^*\) follow from Theorem 1.21.5.

    Theorem 1.21.20. Null space and image of adjoint.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,}_V)\) and \((W,\angvec{\, ,})\) be inner product spaces. Assume \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) has adjoint \(T^*\in\mathcal{L}(W,V)\text{.}\)
    1. \(\NS T^*\).
      We have
      \begin{equation} \NS T^* =(\im T)^\perp\text{.}\tag{1.101} \end{equation}
    2. \(\im T^*\).
      We have
      \begin{equation} \im T^* \subseteq (\NS T)^\perp\text{.}\tag{1.102} \end{equation}
      If we assume further that \(\dim V< \infty\text{,}\) then
      \begin{equation} \im T^*=(\NS T)^\perp\text{.}\tag{1.103} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    Assume \(T^*\) exists. We first prove that \(\NS T^*=\im T^\perp\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{align*} w\in \NS T^* \amp \iff T^*(w)=\boldzero\\ \amp \iff \angvec{T^*(w),v}=\boldzero \text{ for all } v\in V\\ \amp \iff \angvec{w,T(v)}=0 \text{ for all } v\in V \amp (\text{adj. prop.})\\ \amp \iff \angvec{w,u}=0 \text{ for all } u\in \im T \amp (\text{def. } \im T)\\ \amp \iff w\in \im T^\perp\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus \(\NS T^*=\im T^\perp\text{.}\)
    To prove that \(\im T^*=\NS T^\perp\text{,}\) we use the fact that \(T=(T^*)^\perp\) and apply the result above to compute
    \begin{align*} \NS T \amp =\NS (T^*)^*\\ \amp = (\im T^*)^\perp\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus \(\NS T=(\im T^*)^\perp\text{.}\) Since \((\im T^*)^\perp\subseteq V\) is finite dimensional, we have \(((\im T^*)^\perp)^\perp=\im T^*\text{,}\) and thus
    \begin{equation*} \im T^*=((\im T^*)^\perp)^\perp=\NS T^\perp\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as desired.
