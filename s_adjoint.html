    Section 1.21 Adjoints

    Definition 1.21.1. Linear functional.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space. A linear functional on \(V\) is a linear transformation \(\phi\colon V\rightarrow F\) from \(V\) to \(F\text{.}\) The vector space \(\mathcal{L}(V,F)\) of all linear functionals on \(V\) is called the dual space of \(V\text{,}\) and is denoted \(V^*\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.21.2. Riesz representation.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,})\) be an inner product space.
    1. For all \(v_0\in V\text{,}\) the function \(\phi_{v_0}\colon V\rightarrow F\) defined as \(\phi_{v_0}(v)=\angvec{v,v_0}\) is a linear functional.
    2. The function
      \begin{align*} \Phi\colon V \amp \rightarrow V^*=\mathcal{L}(V,F)\\ v \amp \mapsto \phi_v \text{,} \end{align*}
      where \(\phi_v(v')=\angvec{v',v}\text{,}\) is an injective linear transformation.
    3. Assume \(\dim V< \infty\text{.}\) In this case the map \(\Phi\) above is an isomorphism. In particular, given a linear functional \(\phi\colon V\rightarrow F\text{,}\) there is a unique vector \(v_0\in V\) such that \(\phi=\phi_{v_0}\text{:}\) i.e., such that \(\phi(v)=\angvec{v,v_0}\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. This was already proved in Theorem 1.18.9. The current statement is just a slight reformulation of this fact, using the language of linear functionals.
    2. We use the 1-step technique. Given any \(v_1,v_2\in V\) and \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) the function \(\Phi(cv_1+dv_2)=\phi_{cv_1+dv_2}\) satisfies
      \begin{align*} \phi_{cv_1+dv_2}(v) \amp = \angvec{v,cv_1+dv+2} \\ \amp = c\angvec{v,v_1}+d\angvec{v,v_2}\\ \amp = c\phi_{v_1}(v)+d\phi_{v_2}(v)\\ \amp =(c\phi_{v_1}+d\phi_{v_2})(v) \end{align*}
      for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) Thus \(\phi_{cv_1+dv_2}=c\phi_{v_1}+d\phi_{v_2}\text{:}\) i.e., \(\Phi(cv_1+dv_2)=c\Phi(v_1)+d\Phi(v_2)\text{.}\)
      To show \(\Phi\) is injective, we will show that \(\NS \Phi=\{\boldzero\}\text{.}\) Given any \(v_0\in V\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \Phi(v_0)=\boldzero \amp \iff \phi_{v_0}=0_{V,F}\\ \amp \iff \phi_{v_0}(v)=0 \text{ for all } v\in V\\ \amp \iff \angvec{v,v_0}=0 \text{ for all } v\in V\\ \amp \iff v_0=\boldzero \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/th_inner_prod.html}{\text{1.18.9}}) \text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus \(\NS \Phi=\{\boldzero\}\) and \(\Phi\) is injective.
    3. Let \(\dim V=n< \infty\text{.}\) Since \(\dim F=1\text{,}\) by Theorem 1.13.19 we have
      \begin{equation*} \dim V^*=\dim \mathcal{L}(V,F)=n\cdot 1=n=\dim V\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since \(\Phi\) is an injective linear transformation between two vector spaces of the same finite dimension, it follows from Corollary 1.11.4 that \(\Phi\) is bijective, and hence an isomorphism.

    Theorem 1.21.3. Adjoint of a transformation.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,}_V)\) and \((W,\angvec{\, ,}_W\) be inner product spaces, and assume \(V\) is finite dimensional. Given any \(T\in \mathcal{V,W}\text{,}\) there is a unique \(T^*\colon W\rightarrow V\) satisfying
    \begin{align} \angvec{T(v),w}_W \amp =\angvec{v,T^*(w)}_V\tag{1.84}\\ \angvec{T^*(w),v}_V \amp =\angvec{w,T(v)}_V\text{.}\tag{1.85} \end{align}

    Proof.

    In the argument that follows, we will drop the subscripts on \(\angvec{\, ,}_V\) and \(\angvec{\, ,}_W\text{.}\) The reader can easily determine which inner product is meant by determining which space (\(V\) or \(W\)) the input vectors are elements of.
    First we prove the existence of \(T^*\) by defining it explicitly via a somewhat involved recipe. Given any \(w\in W\text{,}\) the function
    \begin{equation*} f_w(v)=\angvec{T(v),w} \end{equation*}
    is easily seen to be a linear functional on \(V\text{:}\) i.e., \(f_w\in V^*=\mathcal{L}(V,F)\text{.}\) By Riesz representation there is a unique vector \(v_w\in V\) satisfying \(f_w(v)=\angvec{v,v_w}\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) We define \(T^*(w)=v_0\text{.}\)
    Having defined \(T^*\colon W\rightarrow V\) as a function,, we now show that it satisfies equation (1.84); equation (1.85) then follows immediately from the conjugate symmetry property. Given any \(v,w\in V\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{T(v),w} \amp = f_w(v) \amp (f_w(v)=\angvec{T(v),w})\\ \amp =\angvec{v,v_w} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/th_Riesz_rep.html}{\text{1.21.2}}\\ \amp =\angvec{v,T^*(w)} \amp (\text{def. } T^*) \text{,} \end{align*}
    as desired.
    Next, we use the fact that \(T^*\) satisfies (1.85) to show it is linear. Given any \(w_1,w_2\in W\) and \(c,d\in F\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{T^*(cw_1+dw_2),v} \amp = \angvec{cw_1+dw_2,T(v)}\\ \amp = c\angvec{w_1,T(v)}+d\angvec{w_2,T(v)}\\ \amp = c\angvec{T^*(w_1),v}+d\angvec{T^*(w_2),v}\\ \amp =\angvec{cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2),v} \end{align*}
    for all \(v\text{.}\) It follows from Theorem 1.18.9 that \(T^*(cw_1+dw_2)=cT^*(w_1)+dT^*(w_2)\text{.}\) Thus \(T^*\) is linear.
    Lastly, we show that \(T^*\) is the unique element of \(\mathcal{L}(W,V)\) satisfying (1.84). If \(S\in \mathcal{L}(W,V)\) satisfies
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{T(v),w}_W=\angvec{v,S(w)}_V \end{equation*}
    for all \(v\in V\) and \(w\in W\text{,}\) then for all \(w\in W\) and \(v\in V\) we have
    \begin{align*} \angvec{v,S(w)-T^*(w)} \amp =\angvec{v,S(w)}-\angvec{v,T^*(w)} \\ \amp = \angvec{T(v),w}-\angvec{T(v),w}\\ \amp = 0\text{.} \end{align*}
    It follows that \(S(w)-T^*(w)=0\) for all \(w\in W\text{,}\) and hence that \(S=T^*\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.21.4. Adjoint of transformation.

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, ,}_V)\) and \((W,\angvec{\, ,}_W\) be inner product spaces. Given \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\text{,}\) we call a transformation \(T^*\in \mathcal{L}(W,V)\) the adjoint of \(T\) if it satisfies properties (1.84)–(1.85). Furthermore, we call each of the equivalent properties (1.84)–(1.85) the adjoint property.
    For our first example of computing an adjoint, we consider a linear transformation \(T\colon F^n\rightarrow F^m\text{.}\) Recall that \(T=T_A\) for a unique matrix \(A\in F^{m,n}\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.21.3, there is an adjoint transformation \(T^*\colon F^{m}\rightarrow F^{n}\text{.}\) What is the matrix \(A'\) such that \(T^*=T_{A'}\text{?}\) This is answered in Theorem 1.21.7, which makes use of the following definition.

    Definition 1.21.5. Conjugate and adjoint of a matrix.

    Let \(F\in \{\R,\C\}\text{,}\) and let \(A=[a_{ij}]\in F^{m,n}\text{.}\)
    The conjugate of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\overline{A}\) is the \(m\times n\) matrix defined as
    \begin{equation*} \overline{A}=[\overline{a_{ij}}]\in F^{m,n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The adjoint of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(A^*\text{,}\) is the \(n\times m\) matrix defined as
    \begin{equation*} A^*=(\overline{A})^T=\overline{(A^T)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.21.6. Standard inner product.

    Let \(F\in \{\R, \C\}\text{,}\) and let \(\angvec{\, ,}\) be the standard inner product on \(F^n\text{.}\) For all \(\boldx, \boldy\in F^n\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation} \angvec{\boldx,\boldy}=\boldy^*\,\boldx\text{.}\tag{1.86} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    Let \(\boldx=(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) and \(\boldy=(y_1,y_2,\dots, y_n)\text{.}\) Treating \(\boldx\) and \(\boldy\) as column vectors (Fiat 1.4.8), we have
    \begin{align*} \boldy^*\,\boldx\amp =\rowvec{\overline{y_1}, \dots, \overline{y_n}}\,\colvec{x_1\\ \vdots \\ x_n} \\ \amp = \sum_{i=1}^n\overline{y_i}x_i \end{align*}

    Theorem 1.21.7. Adjoint of a matrix transformation.

    Assume \(F\in \{\R,\C\}\text{.}\) Let \(T\in\mathcal{L}(F^n, F^m)\text{,}\) and let \(A\in F^{m,n}\) be the unique matrix such that \(T=T_A\text{.}\) The adjoint \(T^*\in \mathcal{L}(F^m, F^n)\text{,}\) taken with respect to the standard inner products on \(F^n\) and \(F^m\text{,}\) is the matrix transformation \(T_{A^*}\text{.}\)

    Let \((V,\angvec{\, }_V)\text{,}\) \((W,\angvec{\, }_W)\text{,}\) \((U,\angvec{\, ,}_U\) be inner product spaces.
    1. Assume \(V\) and \(W\) are finite-dimensional. If \(T\in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) and \(S\in \mathcal{L}(W,U)\text{,}\) then
